This chapter opens Part II of the text. Our goal in the coming chapters is to develop the ergodic theory of dynamical systems: the study of the long-run statistical behavior of orbits. To do this properly, we need a language for assigning sizes to sets, defining averages, and making precise the notion that a property holds for "almost every" initial condition. That language is measure theory.

We do not attempt a complete development of the subject. Instead, we assemble the definitions, theorems, and examples that will be used repeatedly in what follows. The reader who has seen measure theory before may skim this chapter and return to it as needed. The reader encountering it for the first time should work through the definitions carefully and attempt the exercises; the intuition will develop over the next several chapters as we put these tools to work.

## 7.1 Why Measure Theory?

Consider the doubling map $T: [0,1) \to [0,1)$ defined by $T(x) = 2x \pmod{1}$. In Chapter 4 we observed that this map exhibits sensitive dependence on initial conditions: nearby orbits diverge exponentially. Yet numerical experiments suggest a remarkable regularity in the *statistical* behavior of orbits. If we histogram the first $N$ iterates of a "typical" starting point $x_0$, the histogram converges to the uniform distribution on $[0,1]$ as $N \to \infty$.

To make this precise, we need to answer several questions:

1. What do we mean by the "size" of a subset of $[0,1]$? (For intervals this is clear, but what about more complicated sets?)
2. What does "typical" mean? We want to exclude a "negligible" set of exceptional initial conditions — but negligible in what sense?
3. How do we define the "average" of a function along an orbit, and in what sense does it converge?

The Riemann integral, familiar from calculus, is insufficient. It cannot integrate the characteristic function $\mathbf{1}_{\mathbb{Q} \cap [0,1]}$ of the rationals in $[0,1]$, even though the rationals form a "negligible" set by any reasonable standard. More fundamentally, the space of Riemann-integrable functions is not complete in any useful norm, which makes it ill-suited for limit theorems.

Lebesgue's insight was to partition the *range* of a function rather than its domain: instead of asking "what does $f$ do on each small subinterval?", ask "on what set does $f$ take values in each small range?" This requires us to measure the sizes of potentially complicated subsets of the domain — and that demands a theory of measure.

The payoff is substantial. Lebesgue integration gives us powerful convergence theorems (Section 7.4), complete function spaces (Section 7.5), and a framework in which the fundamental theorems of ergodic theory can be stated and proved.

## 7.2 Sigma-Algebras and Measurable Spaces

### 7.2.1 Definition and First Examples

**Definition 7.1** (Sigma-algebra). Let $X$ be a nonempty set. A collection $\mathcal{F}$ of subsets of $X$ is a *$\sigma$-algebra* (or *$\sigma$-field*) on $X$ if:

1. $X \in \mathcal{F}$.
2. If $A \in \mathcal{F}$, then $A^c := X \setminus A \in \mathcal{F}$. (Closure under complementation.)
3. If $A_1, A_2, A_3, \ldots \in \mathcal{F}$, then $\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n \in \mathcal{F}$. (Closure under countable unions.)

The pair $(X, \mathcal{F})$ is called a *measurable space*, and the elements of $\mathcal{F}$ are called *measurable sets*.

Note that closure under complementation and countable unions implies closure under countable intersections (by De Morgan's law), under finite unions, under finite intersections, and that $\emptyset = X^c \in \mathcal{F}$.

**Example 7.2.** The following are $\sigma$-algebras on a set $X$:

- **Trivial $\sigma$-algebra**: $\mathcal{F} = \{\emptyset, X\}$. This is the smallest $\sigma$-algebra on $X$.
- **Discrete $\sigma$-algebra**: $\mathcal{F} = 2^X$ (the power set of $X$). This is the largest $\sigma$-algebra on $X$.
- If $A \subseteq X$, then $\mathcal{F} = \{\emptyset, A, A^c, X\}$ is a $\sigma$-algebra.

For most of our applications, $X$ will be a topological space (often a metric space), and the relevant $\sigma$-algebra is the following.

**Definition 7.3** (Borel $\sigma$-algebra). Let $X$ be a topological space. The *Borel $\sigma$-algebra* $\mathcal{B}(X)$ is the $\sigma$-algebra generated by the open subsets of $X$. Its elements are called *Borel sets*.

We make the notion of "generated by" precise next.

### 7.2.2 Generated Sigma-Algebras

**Proposition 7.4.** Let $X$ be a set and let $\{\mathcal{F}_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in I}$ be any nonempty family of $\sigma$-algebras on $X$. Then $\bigcap_{\alpha \in I} \mathcal{F}_\alpha$ is a $\sigma$-algebra on $X$.

*Proof.* We check the three axioms. (i) $X \in \mathcal{F}_\alpha$ for every $\alpha$, so $X \in \bigcap \mathcal{F}_\alpha$. (ii) If $A \in \bigcap \mathcal{F}_\alpha$, then $A \in \mathcal{F}_\alpha$ for every $\alpha$, hence $A^c \in \mathcal{F}_\alpha$ for every $\alpha$, hence $A^c \in \bigcap \mathcal{F}_\alpha$. (iii) Identical argument for countable unions. $\square$

**Definition 7.5.** Let $\mathcal{C}$ be any collection of subsets of $X$. The *$\sigma$-algebra generated by $\mathcal{C}$*, denoted $\sigma(\mathcal{C})$, is the intersection of all $\sigma$-algebras on $X$ that contain $\mathcal{C}$:

$$\sigma(\mathcal{C}) = \bigcap \{ \mathcal{F} : \mathcal{F} \text{ is a } \sigma\text{-algebra on } X \text{ and } \mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{F} \}.$$

This is the smallest $\sigma$-algebra containing $\mathcal{C}$. The collection $\mathcal{C}$ is called a *generating family* for $\sigma(\mathcal{C})$.

Thus the Borel $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{B}(X)$ is $\sigma(\tau)$, where $\tau$ is the topology on $X$. On $\mathbb{R}$, the Borel $\sigma$-algebra is also generated by any of the following collections:

- All open intervals $(a, b)$.
- All closed intervals $[a, b]$.
- All half-open intervals $(-\infty, a]$, for $a \in \mathbb{R}$.
- All open intervals with rational endpoints.

The last of these is a countable generating family, which gives some sense of why $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$, though enormous, is still "manageable." (In fact $|\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})| = |\mathbb{R}| = \mathfrak{c}$, while $|2^{\mathbb{R}}| = 2^{\mathfrak{c}} > \mathfrak{c}$, so most subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ are *not* Borel.)

### 7.2.3 Measurable Functions

**Definition 7.6.** Let $(X, \mathcal{F})$ and $(Y, \mathcal{G})$ be measurable spaces. A function $f: X \to Y$ is *$(\mathcal{F}, \mathcal{G})$-measurable* (or simply *measurable* when the $\sigma$-algebras are understood) if

$$f^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{F} \quad \text{for every } B \in \mathcal{G}.$$

When $Y = \mathbb{R}$ (or $\overline{\mathbb{R}} = \mathbb{R} \cup \{-\infty, +\infty\}$) equipped with its Borel $\sigma$-algebra, we call $f$ a *measurable function* on $(X, \mathcal{F})$.

**Proposition 7.7.** Let $(X, \mathcal{F})$ be a measurable space. A function $f: X \to \mathbb{R}$ is measurable if and only if $f^{-1}((-\infty, a]) \in \mathcal{F}$ for every $a \in \mathbb{R}$.

*Proof.* The "only if" direction is immediate since $(-\infty, a]$ is a Borel set. For the "if" direction, note that the collection $\{B \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}) : f^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{F}\}$ is a $\sigma$-algebra (check the axioms) that contains all half-lines $(-\infty, a]$. Since these half-lines generate $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$, this $\sigma$-algebra contains $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$. $\square$

**Proposition 7.8.** Let $f, g: X \to \mathbb{R}$ be measurable functions. Then $f + g$, $fg$, $\max(f,g)$, $\min(f,g)$, and $|f|$ are measurable. If $(f_n)$ is a sequence of measurable functions, then $\sup_n f_n$, $\inf_n f_n$, $\limsup_n f_n$, and $\liminf_n f_n$ are measurable.

The proof is a sequence of exercises in manipulating preimages and is left to the reader (Exercise 7.1). The crucial point is that measurability is preserved under *countable* operations — this is the advantage over continuity, which is only preserved under finite operations.

## 7.3 Measures

### 7.3.1 Definition and Basic Properties

**Definition 7.9.** Let $(X, \mathcal{F})$ be a measurable space. A *measure* on $(X, \mathcal{F})$ is a function $\mu: \mathcal{F} \to [0, +\infty]$ satisfying:

1. $\mu(\emptyset) = 0$.
2. **Countable additivity** ($\sigma$-additivity): if $A_1, A_2, \ldots \in \mathcal{F}$ are pairwise disjoint, then
$$\mu\!\left(\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n\right) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \mu(A_n).$$

The triple $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ is called a *measure space*.

**Definition 7.10.** A measure $\mu$ is:

- *Finite* if $\mu(X) < \infty$.
- A *probability measure* if $\mu(X) = 1$. We then call $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ a *probability space*.
- *$\sigma$-finite* if $X = \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty A_n$ with $\mu(A_n) < \infty$ for each $n$.

In ergodic theory, we work almost exclusively with probability measures or finite measures.

The next proposition collects the essential properties that follow from countable additivity. These will be used without comment in later chapters.

**Proposition 7.11** (Basic properties of measures). Let $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ be a measure space.

1. **Monotonicity**: If $A \subseteq B$ and $A, B \in \mathcal{F}$, then $\mu(A) \leq \mu(B)$.
2. **Subadditivity**: If $A_1, A_2, \ldots \in \mathcal{F}$, then $\mu(\bigcup_n A_n) \leq \sum_n \mu(A_n)$.
3. **Continuity from below**: If $A_1 \subseteq A_2 \subseteq \cdots$ and $A_n \in \mathcal{F}$, then
$$\mu\!\left(\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n\right) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \mu(A_n).$$
4. **Continuity from above**: If $A_1 \supseteq A_2 \supseteq \cdots$, $A_n \in \mathcal{F}$, and $\mu(A_1) < \infty$, then
$$\mu\!\left(\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n\right) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \mu(A_n).$$

*Proof.* (1) Write $B = A \cup (B \setminus A)$ as a disjoint union, so $\mu(B) = \mu(A) + \mu(B \setminus A) \geq \mu(A)$.

(2) Define $B_1 = A_1$ and $B_n = A_n \setminus \bigcup_{k=1}^{n-1} A_k$ for $n \geq 2$. Then the $B_n$ are pairwise disjoint, $\bigcup B_n = \bigcup A_n$, and $B_n \subseteq A_n$, so

$$\mu\!\left(\bigcup_n A_n\right) = \sum_n \mu(B_n) \leq \sum_n \mu(A_n).$$

(3) Using the same telescoping trick: set $B_1 = A_1$ and $B_n = A_n \setminus A_{n-1}$ for $n \geq 2$. These are disjoint with $\bigcup_{k=1}^n B_k = A_n$ and $\bigcup_{k=1}^\infty B_k = \bigcup_n A_n$. Then

$$\mu\!\left(\bigcup_n A_n\right) = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \mu(B_k) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{k=1}^{n} \mu(B_k) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \mu(A_n).$$

(4) Apply (3) to the increasing sequence $C_n = A_1 \setminus A_n$:

$$\mu(A_1) - \mu\!\left(\bigcap_n A_n\right) = \mu\!\left(\bigcup_n C_n\right) = \lim_n \mu(C_n) = \mu(A_1) - \lim_n \mu(A_n).$$

The finiteness of $\mu(A_1)$ is used to cancel it from both sides. $\square$

The hypothesis $\mu(A_1) < \infty$ in (4) is essential. Without it, the result can fail: let $A_n = [n, \infty)$ with Lebesgue measure. Then $\mu(A_n) = \infty$ for all $n$, but $\bigcap_n A_n = \emptyset$, so $\mu(\bigcap_n A_n) = 0 \neq \lim_n \mu(A_n)$.

### 7.3.2 Examples

**Example 7.12** (Counting measure). Let $X$ be any set, $\mathcal{F} = 2^X$, and define $\mu(A) = |A|$ (the cardinality of $A$, with $\mu(A) = \infty$ if $A$ is infinite). This is a measure, called the *counting measure* on $X$.

**Example 7.13** (Dirac measure). Fix a point $x_0 \in X$. The *Dirac measure* (or *point mass*) at $x_0$ is

$$\delta_{x_0}(A) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } x_0 \in A, \\ 0 & \text{if } x_0 \notin A. \end{cases}$$

This is a probability measure on $(X, \mathcal{F})$ for any $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F}$. It models a deterministic outcome: all the "mass" is concentrated at a single point.

**Example 7.14** (Lebesgue measure). The *Lebesgue measure* $\lambda$ on $(\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$ is the unique measure satisfying $\lambda((a,b)) = b - a$ for every bounded open interval. Its construction is sketched in Section 7.3.3 below. The restriction of $\lambda$ to $[0,1]$ gives a probability measure on $([0,1], \mathcal{B}([0,1]))$.

Lebesgue measure is the formalization of "length" (in $\mathbb{R}$), "area" (in $\mathbb{R}^2$), and "volume" (in $\mathbb{R}^n$). It is the default measure for dynamics on the interval and the torus.

### 7.3.3 Construction of Lebesgue Measure (Sketch)

The construction proceeds in three steps. We outline it for $[0,1]$; the construction on $\mathbb{R}$ is analogous.

**Step 1: Outer measure.** For any $A \subseteq [0,1]$, define the *Lebesgue outer measure*

$$\lambda^*(A) = \inf\!\left\{ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (b_n - a_n) : A \subseteq \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} (a_n, b_n) \right\},$$

where the infimum is over all countable covers of $A$ by open intervals.

The outer measure $\lambda^*$ is defined on *all* subsets of $[0,1]$, is monotone, and is countably subadditive: $\lambda^*(\bigcup_n A_n) \leq \sum_n \lambda^*(A_n)$. However, it is *not* countably additive on all subsets.

**Step 2: Carath\'{e}odory's criterion.** Call a set $A \subseteq [0,1]$ *Lebesgue measurable* if for every $E \subseteq [0,1]$,

$$\lambda^*(E) = \lambda^*(E \cap A) + \lambda^*(E \cap A^c).$$

Carath\'{e}odory's theorem states that the collection $\mathcal{L}$ of Lebesgue measurable sets is a $\sigma$-algebra and that $\lambda^*$ restricted to $\mathcal{L}$ is a complete measure.

**Step 3: Verification.** One checks that every Borel set is Lebesgue measurable (so $\mathcal{B}([0,1]) \subseteq \mathcal{L}$) and that $\lambda^*((a,b)) = b - a$ for intervals. The restriction $\lambda = \lambda^*|_{\mathcal{L}}$ is *Lebesgue measure*.

The $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{L}$ is strictly larger than $\mathcal{B}([0,1])$: there exist Lebesgue measurable sets that are not Borel. However, for essentially all purposes in dynamics, we work with the Borel $\sigma$-algebra or identify sets that differ by a set of measure zero.

**Remark 7.15.** Not every subset of $[0,1]$ is Lebesgue measurable. The construction of a non-measurable set (due to Vitali, 1905) requires the axiom of choice: partition $[0,1)$ into equivalence classes under the relation $x \sim y$ iff $x - y \in \mathbb{Q}$, and select one representative from each class. The resulting set cannot be assigned a Lebesgue measure consistent with translation invariance. This is the fundamental reason we need $\sigma$-algebras: we must restrict our attention to a well-behaved collection of sets.

### 7.3.4 Null Sets and "Almost Everywhere"

**Definition 7.16.** A set $N \in \mathcal{F}$ is a *null set* (or has *measure zero*) if $\mu(N) = 0$. A property is said to hold *$\mu$-almost everywhere* (abbreviated $\mu$-a.e., or simply a.e. when $\mu$ is understood) if it holds on $X \setminus N$ for some null set $N$.

This is the precise meaning of "typical" in dynamics: a property holds for a "typical" orbit if it holds for $\mu$-almost every initial condition.

**Example 7.17.** The set $\mathbb{Q} \cap [0,1]$ of rationals in $[0,1]$ has Lebesgue measure zero (it is countable, and each singleton has measure zero). Therefore, when we say "almost every orbit of the doubling map equidistributes," we allow for a set of exceptions that includes, but may be larger than, the rationals.

### 7.3.5 Borel Measures on Metric Spaces

In the setting of dynamics, the state space is typically a metric space $(X, d)$ (often compact), and we work with Borel measures: measures defined on $\mathcal{B}(X)$.

**Definition 7.18.** A Borel measure $\mu$ on a metric space $X$ is:

- *Regular* if for every $B \in \mathcal{B}(X)$,
$$\mu(B) = \inf\{\mu(U) : U \supseteq B, \, U \text{ open}\} = \sup\{\mu(K) : K \subseteq B, \, K \text{ compact}\}.$$
- *Locally finite* if every point has a neighborhood of finite measure.

A locally finite regular Borel measure is called a *Radon measure*. On a compact metric space, every finite Borel measure is Radon. Since we will primarily work with probability measures on compact spaces, we will not need to worry about regularity issues.

## 7.4 Lebesgue Integration

### 7.4.1 Simple Functions

**Definition 7.19.** Let $(X, \mathcal{F})$ be a measurable space. A *simple function* is a measurable function $\varphi: X \to \mathbb{R}$ that takes only finitely many values. Every simple function can be written as

$$\varphi = \sum_{k=1}^{n} c_k \, \mathbf{1}_{A_k},$$

where $c_1, \ldots, c_n \in \mathbb{R}$ are distinct, $A_k = \varphi^{-1}(\{c_k\}) \in \mathcal{F}$, and $\mathbf{1}_{A_k}$ is the characteristic (indicator) function of $A_k$. This is called the *standard representation* of $\varphi$.

### 7.4.2 Definition of the Integral

The Lebesgue integral is built in three stages.

**Stage 1: Nonnegative simple functions.** For a nonnegative simple function $\varphi = \sum_{k=1}^n c_k \mathbf{1}_{A_k}$ (standard representation, all $c_k \geq 0$) on a measure space $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$, define

$$\int_X \varphi \, d\mu = \sum_{k=1}^{n} c_k \, \mu(A_k),$$

with the convention $0 \cdot \infty = 0$.

**Stage 2: Nonnegative measurable functions.** For a nonnegative measurable function $f: X \to [0, +\infty]$, define

$$\int_X f \, d\mu = \sup\!\left\{ \int_X \varphi \, d\mu : 0 \leq \varphi \leq f, \; \varphi \text{ simple} \right\}.$$

This may equal $+\infty$.

**Stage 3: General measurable functions.** Write $f = f^+ - f^-$, where $f^+ = \max(f, 0)$ and $f^- = \max(-f, 0)$. If at least one of $\int f^+ \, d\mu$ and $\int f^- \, d\mu$ is finite, define

$$\int_X f \, d\mu = \int_X f^+ \, d\mu - \int_X f^- \, d\mu.$$

If both integrals are finite, $f$ is called *($\mu$-)integrable* (or *Lebesgue integrable*), and we write $f \in L^1(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ or simply $f \in L^1(\mu)$.

We also write $\int_A f \, d\mu = \int_X f \cdot \mathbf{1}_A \, d\mu$ for integration over a measurable subset $A$.

**Remark 7.20.** There is a useful approximation result underlying Stage 2: every nonnegative measurable function is the pointwise limit of an increasing sequence of nonnegative simple functions. This can be constructed explicitly. Given $f \geq 0$ measurable, define

$$\varphi_n(x) = \begin{cases} k \cdot 2^{-n} & \text{if } k \cdot 2^{-n} \leq f(x) < (k+1) \cdot 2^{-n}, \; k = 0, 1, \ldots, n \cdot 2^n - 1, \\ n & \text{if } f(x) \geq n. \end{cases}$$

Then $0 \leq \varphi_1 \leq \varphi_2 \leq \cdots$ and $\varphi_n(x) \to f(x)$ for every $x$.

### 7.4.3 Basic Properties

The Lebesgue integral satisfies the following, all of which are used constantly.

**Proposition 7.21** (Properties of the integral). Let $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ be a measure space and let $f, g$ be measurable functions.

1. **Linearity**: If $f, g \in L^1(\mu)$ and $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$, then $af + bg \in L^1(\mu)$ and
$$\int (af + bg) \, d\mu = a \int f \, d\mu + b \int g \, d\mu.$$
2. **Monotonicity**: If $f \leq g$ a.e. and both integrals are defined, then $\int f \, d\mu \leq \int g \, d\mu$.
3. **Triangle inequality**: $\left|\int f \, d\mu\right| \leq \int |f| \, d\mu$.
4. **Null sets are negligible**: If $\mu(N) = 0$, then $\int_N f \, d\mu = 0$. Equivalently, if $f = g$ a.e., then $\int f \, d\mu = \int g \, d\mu$.

### 7.4.4 Convergence Theorems

The three convergence theorems below are the workhorses of measure-theoretic analysis. They tell us when we may interchange limits and integrals — a question that arises repeatedly in ergodic theory.

**Theorem 7.22** (Monotone Convergence Theorem; Beppo Levi). Let $(f_n)$ be a sequence of nonnegative measurable functions on $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ such that $f_n \leq f_{n+1}$ a.e. for all $n$. Let $f = \lim_{n \to \infty} f_n$ (pointwise a.e.). Then

$$\int_X f \, d\mu = \lim_{n \to \infty} \int_X f_n \, d\mu.$$

*Proof sketch.* The inequality $\int f \, d\mu \geq \lim_n \int f_n \, d\mu$ follows from monotonicity (since $f_n \leq f$ implies $\int f_n \leq \int f$). The reverse inequality is the substantive content. Fix a simple function $\varphi$ with $0 \leq \varphi \leq f$ and fix $\alpha \in (0,1)$. Define $A_n = \{x : f_n(x) \geq \alpha \varphi(x)\}$. Then $A_1 \subseteq A_2 \subseteq \cdots$ and $\bigcup_n A_n = X$ (a.e., since $f_n \uparrow f \geq \varphi > \alpha \varphi$ wherever $\varphi > 0$). Thus

$$\int_X f_n \, d\mu \geq \int_{A_n} f_n \, d\mu \geq \alpha \int_{A_n} \varphi \, d\mu.$$

By continuity from below applied to the measure $A \mapsto \int_A \varphi \, d\mu$, the right side converges to $\alpha \int_X \varphi \, d\mu$ as $n \to \infty$. Taking $\alpha \uparrow 1$ and then the supremum over all such $\varphi$ gives $\lim_n \int f_n \, d\mu \geq \int f \, d\mu$. $\square$

**Theorem 7.23** (Fatou's Lemma). Let $(f_n)$ be a sequence of nonnegative measurable functions. Then

$$\int_X \liminf_{n \to \infty} f_n \, d\mu \leq \liminf_{n \to \infty} \int_X f_n \, d\mu.$$

*Proof.* Define $g_n = \inf_{k \geq n} f_k$. Then $g_n \leq f_n$, the sequence $(g_n)$ is nondecreasing, and $g_n \uparrow \liminf_n f_n$. By the Monotone Convergence Theorem,

$$\int \liminf_n f_n \, d\mu = \lim_n \int g_n \, d\mu = \liminf_n \int g_n \, d\mu \leq \liminf_n \int f_n \, d\mu. \quad \square$$

**Theorem 7.24** (Dominated Convergence Theorem; Lebesgue). Let $(f_n)$ be a sequence of measurable functions on $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ such that $f_n \to f$ pointwise a.e. Suppose there exists an integrable function $g \geq 0$ with $|f_n| \leq g$ a.e. for all $n$. Then $f$ and all $f_n$ are integrable, and

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \int_X f_n \, d\mu = \int_X f \, d\mu.$$

Equivalently, $\int |f_n - f| \, d\mu \to 0$.

*Proof.* The functions $g + f_n \geq 0$ and $g - f_n \geq 0$ are nonnegative. Applying Fatou's lemma to each:

$$\int (g + f) \, d\mu \leq \liminf_n \int (g + f_n) \, d\mu = \int g \, d\mu + \liminf_n \int f_n \, d\mu,$$

$$\int (g - f) \, d\mu \leq \liminf_n \int (g - f_n) \, d\mu = \int g \, d\mu - \limsup_n \int f_n \, d\mu.$$

Since $\int g \, d\mu < \infty$, we may cancel it. The first inequality gives $\int f \, d\mu \leq \liminf_n \int f_n \, d\mu$, and the second gives $\int f \, d\mu \geq \limsup_n \int f_n \, d\mu$. Together: $\lim_n \int f_n \, d\mu = \int f \, d\mu$.

For the "equivalently" statement, apply the above to $|f_n - f|$, which is dominated by $2g$, and converges to $0$ a.e. $\square$

**Remark 7.25.** The domination hypothesis cannot be dropped. Consider $f_n = n \cdot \mathbf{1}_{(0, 1/n)}$ on $[0,1]$ with Lebesgue measure. Then $f_n \to 0$ pointwise a.e., but $\int f_n \, d\lambda = 1$ for all $n$.

### 7.4.5 Comparison with the Riemann Integral

For functions on a bounded interval $[a,b]$:

**Theorem 7.26.** If $f: [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is Riemann integrable, then $f$ is Lebesgue measurable, Lebesgue integrable, and the two integrals agree:

$$\int_a^b f(x) \, dx \;(\text{Riemann}) = \int_{[a,b]} f \, d\lambda \;(\text{Lebesgue}).$$

Moreover, $f$ is Riemann integrable if and only if it is bounded and continuous $\lambda$-a.e. (Lebesgue's criterion).

The Lebesgue integral is strictly more general: $\mathbf{1}_{\mathbb{Q} \cap [0,1]}$ is Lebesgue integrable (with integral $0$) but not Riemann integrable.

## 7.5 $L^p$ Spaces

### 7.5.1 Definitions

**Definition 7.27.** Let $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ be a measure space and let $1 \leq p < \infty$. The space $L^p(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ (or $L^p(\mu)$, or simply $L^p$) consists of all measurable functions $f: X \to \mathbb{R}$ (or $\mathbb{C}$) with

$$\|f\|_p := \left( \int_X |f|^p \, d\mu \right)^{1/p} < \infty.$$

The space $L^\infty(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ consists of all measurable functions that are bounded a.e., with

$$\|f\|_\infty := \inf\{C \geq 0 : |f| \leq C \text{ a.e.}\}$$

(the *essential supremum* of $|f|$).

**Convention.** Strictly speaking, $\|\cdot\|_p$ is a seminorm: $\|f\|_p = 0$ does not imply $f = 0$, only $f = 0$ a.e. We follow the standard convention of identifying functions that agree almost everywhere, so that elements of $L^p$ are really equivalence classes of functions. With this identification, $\|\cdot\|_p$ is a genuine norm.

The three cases of primary importance for us are:

- **$L^1(\mu)$**: the space of integrable functions. This is where the Birkhoff ergodic theorem (Chapter 9) lives.
- **$L^2(\mu)$**: the space of square-integrable functions. This is where the von Neumann mean ergodic theorem lives.
- **$L^\infty(\mu)$**: the space of essentially bounded functions. Observables in dynamical systems are often assumed to be in $L^\infty$ or at least $L^2$.

### 7.5.2 Key Inequalities

**Theorem 7.28** (H\"{o}lder's inequality). Let $p, q \in [1, \infty]$ with $\frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{q} = 1$ (conjugate exponents). If $f \in L^p$ and $g \in L^q$, then $fg \in L^1$ and

$$\|fg\|_1 \leq \|f\|_p \, \|g\|_q.$$

**Theorem 7.29** (Minkowski's inequality). For $1 \leq p \leq \infty$, if $f, g \in L^p$, then $f + g \in L^p$ and

$$\|f + g\|_p \leq \|f\|_p + \|g\|_p.$$

This confirms that $\|\cdot\|_p$ is indeed a norm (the triangle inequality).

### 7.5.3 Completeness and $L^2$ as a Hilbert Space

**Theorem 7.30** (Riesz-Fischer). For $1 \leq p \leq \infty$, the space $L^p(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ is a *Banach space* (a complete normed vector space).

*Proof sketch for $L^1$.* Let $(f_n)$ be a Cauchy sequence in $L^1$. Pass to a subsequence $(f_{n_k})$ with $\|f_{n_{k+1}} - f_{n_k}\|_1 < 2^{-k}$. Define $g = \sum_{k} |f_{n_{k+1}} - f_{n_k}|$; by the Monotone Convergence Theorem, $\|g\|_1 \leq \sum_k 2^{-k} = 1$, so $g < \infty$ a.e. The telescoping series $f_{n_1} + \sum_k (f_{n_{k+1}} - f_{n_k})$ therefore converges absolutely a.e. to some $f \in L^1$. One then verifies $f_n \to f$ in $L^1$ norm using the Dominated Convergence Theorem. $\square$

The space $L^2$ has additional structure that makes it especially useful.

**Definition 7.31.** On $L^2(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$, define the *inner product*

$$\langle f, g \rangle = \int_X f \cdot \overline{g} \, d\mu.$$

(For real-valued functions, drop the conjugate.) Then $\|f\|_2 = \sqrt{\langle f, f \rangle}$.

By the Riesz-Fischer theorem, $L^2$ is complete, and hence $L^2$ is a *Hilbert space*: a complete inner product space.

**Why $L^2$ matters for ergodic theory.** Given a measure-preserving transformation $T: X \to X$ on a probability space $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$, the *composition operator* $U_T: L^2(\mu) \to L^2(\mu)$ defined by $U_T f = f \circ T$ is a linear isometry. The mean ergodic theorem (von Neumann, 1932) asserts that the time averages $\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} U_T^n f$ converge in the $L^2$ norm. The proof uses the Hilbert space structure of $L^2$ — specifically, the orthogonal decomposition into $T$-invariant functions and their complement. This is the subject of Chapter 9.

### 7.5.4 Inclusions Between $L^p$ Spaces

For a probability measure $\mu$ (i.e., $\mu(X) = 1$), we have the chain of inclusions

$$L^\infty(\mu) \subseteq \cdots \subseteq L^2(\mu) \subseteq L^1(\mu),$$

and the norm inequalities $\|f\|_1 \leq \|f\|_2 \leq \|f\|_\infty$. This follows from H\"{o}lder's inequality with $g = 1$ (which has $\|g\|_q = 1$ on a probability space). These inclusions can *fail* for infinite measures (e.g., $f(x) = 1/x$ is in $L^2((1,\infty), \lambda)$ but not in $L^1((1,\infty), \lambda)$).

Since we work primarily with probability measures in ergodic theory, we have the convenience that bounded functions are in every $L^p$ space.

## 7.6 Product Measures and Fubini's Theorem

### 7.6.1 Product Sigma-Algebras and Product Measures

Given measurable spaces $(X, \mathcal{F})$ and $(Y, \mathcal{G})$, the *product $\sigma$-algebra* $\mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{G}$ on $X \times Y$ is the $\sigma$-algebra generated by the *measurable rectangles* $\{A \times B : A \in \mathcal{F}, B \in \mathcal{G}\}$.

**Theorem 7.32** (Product measure). Let $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ and $(Y, \mathcal{G}, \nu)$ be $\sigma$-finite measure spaces. There exists a unique measure $\mu \otimes \nu$ on $(X \times Y, \mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{G})$ satisfying

$$(\mu \otimes \nu)(A \times B) = \mu(A) \cdot \nu(B)$$

for all $A \in \mathcal{F}$, $B \in \mathcal{G}$.

The construction generalizes to finite products and, crucially for symbolic dynamics, to countably infinite products (Section 7.8.3).

### 7.6.2 Fubini-Tonelli Theorem

**Theorem 7.33** (Tonelli). Let $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ and $(Y, \mathcal{G}, \nu)$ be $\sigma$-finite measure spaces, and let $f: X \times Y \to [0, +\infty]$ be $(\mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{G})$-measurable. Then:

1. For each $x \in X$, the function $y \mapsto f(x, y)$ is $\mathcal{G}$-measurable.
2. The function $x \mapsto \int_Y f(x, y) \, d\nu(y)$ is $\mathcal{F}$-measurable.
3. $$\int_{X \times Y} f \, d(\mu \otimes \nu) = \int_X \left( \int_Y f(x,y) \, d\nu(y) \right) d\mu(x) = \int_Y \left( \int_X f(x,y) \, d\mu(x) \right) d\nu(y).$$

**Theorem 7.34** (Fubini). Under the same hypotheses as Tonelli's theorem, if $f: X \times Y \to \mathbb{R}$ (or $\mathbb{C}$) is $(\mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{G})$-measurable and $\int_{X \times Y} |f| \, d(\mu \otimes \nu) < \infty$, then all three iterated integrals in Tonelli's theorem are finite and equal.

**Remark 7.35.** The difference: Tonelli requires $f \geq 0$ (no integrability hypothesis needed); Fubini requires integrability (and then handles sign changes). In practice, one often uses Tonelli first to verify integrability, then Fubini to compute.

We will use product measures and Fubini's theorem in Chapter 11 when working with shift spaces and symbolic dynamics.

## 7.7 Conditional Expectation

Conditional expectation is a more subtle construction that will be needed in Chapter 13 when we develop measure-theoretic entropy. We state the definition and key properties without proof.

**Definition and Theorem 7.36** (Conditional expectation). Let $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ be a probability space, $\mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{F}$ a sub-$\sigma$-algebra, and $f \in L^1(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$. There exists a function $\mathbb{E}[f \mid \mathcal{G}] \in L^1(X, \mathcal{G}, \mu)$, unique up to $\mu$-a.e. equality, such that

$$\int_G \mathbb{E}[f \mid \mathcal{G}] \, d\mu = \int_G f \, d\mu \quad \text{for all } G \in \mathcal{G}.$$

The function $\mathbb{E}[f \mid \mathcal{G}]$ is called the *conditional expectation of $f$ given $\mathcal{G}$*.

The existence proof uses the Radon-Nikod\'{y}m theorem (the measure $G \mapsto \int_G f \, d\mu$ is absolutely continuous with respect to $\mu|_\mathcal{G}$). The proof can be found in Billingsley [3, Section 34] or Halmos [1, Chapter IX].

**Key properties:**

1. **Linearity**: $\mathbb{E}[af + bg \mid \mathcal{G}] = a \, \mathbb{E}[f \mid \mathcal{G}] + b \, \mathbb{E}[g \mid \mathcal{G}]$ a.e.
2. **Monotonicity**: If $f \leq g$ a.e., then $\mathbb{E}[f \mid \mathcal{G}] \leq \mathbb{E}[g \mid \mathcal{G}]$ a.e.
3. **Tower property**: If $\mathcal{H} \subseteq \mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{F}$, then $\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[f \mid \mathcal{G}] \mid \mathcal{H}] = \mathbb{E}[f \mid \mathcal{H}]$ a.e.
4. **Trivial $\sigma$-algebra**: $\mathbb{E}[f \mid \{\emptyset, X\}] = \int_X f \, d\mu$ (a constant function).
5. **Full $\sigma$-algebra**: $\mathbb{E}[f \mid \mathcal{F}] = f$ a.e.
6. **$\mathcal{G}$-measurable factors**: If $g$ is $\mathcal{G}$-measurable and bounded, then $\mathbb{E}[gf \mid \mathcal{G}] = g \, \mathbb{E}[f \mid \mathcal{G}]$ a.e.

The conditional expectation $\mathbb{E}[f \mid \mathcal{G}]$ can be understood as the "best approximation" of $f$ by a $\mathcal{G}$-measurable function, in the $L^2$ sense: it is the orthogonal projection of $f$ onto the closed subspace $L^2(X, \mathcal{G}, \mu) \subseteq L^2(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$. This geometric viewpoint connects directly to the Hilbert space ideas of Section 7.5.

## 7.8 Examples from Dynamics

We now introduce the three measures that will appear most frequently in Part II.

### 7.8.1 Lebesgue Measure on $[0,1]$

Lebesgue measure $\lambda$ on $([0,1], \mathcal{B}([0,1]))$ is the canonical probability measure for studying maps on the interval. It assigns to each subinterval $[a,b] \subseteq [0,1]$ its length $b - a$.

**Example 7.37.** The doubling map $T(x) = 2x \pmod{1}$ *preserves* Lebesgue measure: $\lambda(T^{-1}(A)) = \lambda(A)$ for every Borel set $A \subseteq [0,1]$. To verify this for an interval $[a,b]$:

$$T^{-1}([a,b]) = \left[\frac{a}{2}, \frac{b}{2}\right] \cup \left[\frac{a+1}{2}, \frac{b+1}{2}\right],$$

so $\lambda(T^{-1}([a,b])) = \frac{b-a}{2} + \frac{b-a}{2} = b - a = \lambda([a,b])$. Since intervals generate $\mathcal{B}([0,1])$, this suffices (by a standard uniqueness argument for measures agreeing on a generating $\pi$-system).

Not every map preserves Lebesgue measure. The tent map $T(x) = 1 - |2x - 1|$ does, but the logistic map $T(x) = 4x(1-x)$ does not (its invariant measure has density $\frac{1}{\pi\sqrt{x(1-x)}}$ with respect to $\lambda$).

### 7.8.2 Haar Measure on the Circle

Let $\mathbb{T} = \mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}$ denote the circle (the unit interval with endpoints identified), and let $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{T})$ be its Borel $\sigma$-algebra. *Haar measure* on $\mathbb{T}$ is the unique Borel probability measure $m$ that is invariant under rotations:

$$m(A + \alpha) = m(A) \quad \text{for all } A \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{T}), \; \alpha \in \mathbb{T},$$

where $A + \alpha = \{x + \alpha \pmod{1} : x \in A\}$.

Concretely, $m$ coincides with Lebesgue measure on $[0,1)$ under the identification $\mathbb{T} \cong [0,1)$. The existence and uniqueness of Haar measure holds for any compact topological group (this is a deep theorem of Haar and von Neumann), but on $\mathbb{T}$ the construction is elementary.

**Example 7.38.** The rotation $R_\alpha(x) = x + \alpha \pmod{1}$ preserves Haar measure for any $\alpha$. This is immediate from the definition. When $\alpha$ is irrational, $R_\alpha$ is uniquely ergodic: Haar measure is the *only* $R_\alpha$-invariant Borel probability measure. This will be proved in Chapter 10.

### 7.8.3 Bernoulli Measure on $\{0,1\}^{\mathbb{N}}$

Let $\Sigma_2 = \{0, 1\}^{\mathbb{N}}$ be the space of one-sided infinite binary sequences $\omega = (\omega_0, \omega_1, \omega_2, \ldots)$ with each $\omega_i \in \{0,1\}$. Equip $\{0,1\}$ with the discrete topology; then $\Sigma_2$ with the product topology is a compact metrizable space (it is homeomorphic to the Cantor set).

The Borel $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{B}(\Sigma_2)$ is generated by the *cylinder sets*

$$[a_0 a_1 \cdots a_{n-1}] = \{\omega \in \Sigma_2 : \omega_0 = a_0, \omega_1 = a_1, \ldots, \omega_{n-1} = a_{n-1}\},$$

where $a_i \in \{0,1\}$.

**Definition 7.39** (Bernoulli measure). Fix $p \in (0,1)$ and let $q = 1 - p$. The *$(p,q)$-Bernoulli measure* $\mu_p$ on $(\Sigma_2, \mathcal{B}(\Sigma_2))$ is the unique Borel probability measure satisfying

$$\mu_p([a_0 a_1 \cdots a_{n-1}]) = \prod_{i=0}^{n-1} p_i, \quad \text{where } p_i = \begin{cases} p & \text{if } a_i = 1, \\ q & \text{if } a_i = 0. \end{cases}$$

The fair coin measure corresponds to $p = q = 1/2$, in which case every cylinder of length $n$ has measure $2^{-n}$.

Formally, $\mu_p$ is the countably infinite product measure $\bigotimes_{n=0}^{\infty} \nu$, where $\nu$ is the measure on $\{0,1\}$ with $\nu(\{1\}) = p$ and $\nu(\{0\}) = q$. The existence of such an infinite product measure is guaranteed by the Kolmogorov extension theorem (see Billingsley [3, Theorem 36.1]).

**Example 7.40.** The *(left) shift map* $\sigma: \Sigma_2 \to \Sigma_2$ defined by $(\sigma(\omega))_n = \omega_{n+1}$ preserves the $(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2})$-Bernoulli measure. More generally, $\sigma$ preserves $\mu_p$ for every $p$. Indeed, for a cylinder set,

$$\mu_p(\sigma^{-1}([a_0 \cdots a_{n-1}])) = \mu_p([0 a_0 \cdots a_{n-1}]) + \mu_p([1 a_0 \cdots a_{n-1}]) = q \prod p_i + p \prod p_i = (p + q) \prod p_i = \mu_p([a_0 \cdots a_{n-1}]).$$

The system $(\Sigma_2, \mathcal{B}(\Sigma_2), \mu_p, \sigma)$ is called the *(one-sided) Bernoulli shift*. It is the prototypical example of a mixing dynamical system and will be studied extensively in Chapters 11 and 13.

### 7.8.4 Connection to the Doubling Map

There is a beautiful connection between the symbolic and interval settings. Define $\pi: \Sigma_2 \to [0,1]$ by

$$\pi(\omega) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{\omega_n}{2^{n+1}}.$$

This is the binary expansion map. It is surjective, continuous, and satisfies $\pi \circ \sigma = T \circ \pi$, where $T$ is the doubling map. Moreover, the $(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2})$-Bernoulli measure pushes forward to Lebesgue measure:

$$\mu_{1/2}(\pi^{-1}(A)) = \lambda(A) \quad \text{for every Borel set } A \subseteq [0,1].$$

This semiconjugacy between $(\Sigma_2, \sigma)$ and $([0,1], T)$ shows that the symbolic and geometric viewpoints are two descriptions of the same dynamical system. We will exploit such connections systematically in Part II.

## 7.10 Exercises

**Exercise 7.1** (Measurability is preserved under algebraic operations). Let $(X, \mathcal{F})$ be a measurable space and let $f, g: X \to \mathbb{R}$ be measurable functions.

(a) Prove that $f + g$ is measurable. (*Hint*: $\{f + g < a\} = \bigcup_{r \in \mathbb{Q}} (\{f < r\} \cap \{g < a - r\})$.)

(b) Prove that if $(f_n)$ is a sequence of measurable functions, then $\sup_n f_n$ is measurable. (*Hint*: $\{\sup_n f_n > a\} = \bigcup_n \{f_n > a\}$.)

(c) Deduce that $\limsup_n f_n$ and $\liminf_n f_n$ are measurable.

**Exercise 7.2** (Borel-Cantelli lemma). Let $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ be a measure space and let $(A_n)$ be a sequence of measurable sets.

(a) Prove that if $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \mu(A_n) < \infty$, then $\mu(\limsup_n A_n) = 0$, where $\limsup_n A_n = \bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} \bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_k$ is the set of points belonging to infinitely many $A_n$.

(b) Apply this to the doubling map $T(x) = 2x \pmod{1}$ on $([0,1], \lambda)$: show that for $\lambda$-almost every $x$, the orbit $(T^n(x))_{n \geq 0}$ does not visit the interval $(0, 1/n^2)$ infinitely often.

**Exercise 7.3** (Approximation in $L^p$). Let $f \in L^1([0,1], \lambda)$.

(a) Prove that for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists a continuous function $g: [0,1] \to \mathbb{R}$ with $\|f - g\|_1 < \varepsilon$. (*Hint*: First approximate by simple functions, then approximate indicator functions of intervals by continuous functions.)

(b) Prove that the set of polynomials is dense in $L^2([0,1], \lambda)$. (*Hint*: By the Weierstrass approximation theorem, polynomials are dense in $C([0,1])$ in the supremum norm, hence also in $L^2$ norm on a probability space.)

**Exercise 7.4** (Product measures and the Cantor set). Let $C \subset [0,1]$ be the standard middle-thirds Cantor set.

(a) Express $C$ as a subset of $\{0, 2\}^{\mathbb{N}}$ via ternary expansions. Describe the Borel $\sigma$-algebra on $C$ in terms of cylinder sets.

(b) Define the *Cantor measure* $\mu_C$ on $C$ as the countably infinite product of the measure $\nu$ on $\{0, 2\}$ with $\nu(\{0\}) = \nu(\{2\}) = 1/2$. Show that $\mu_C$ is a Borel probability measure on $C$, and that $\mu_C$ assigns measure $2^{-n}$ to each cylinder of length $n$.

(c) Show that $\mu_C$ is non-atomic (i.e., $\mu_C(\{x\}) = 0$ for every $x \in C$), even though $C$ has Lebesgue measure zero.

**Exercise 7.5** (Invariance of Lebesgue measure). Prove that the tent map $T: [0,1] \to [0,1]$ defined by

$$T(x) = \begin{cases} 2x & \text{if } 0 \leq x \leq 1/2, \\ 2 - 2x & \text{if } 1/2 < x \leq 1, \end{cases}$$

preserves Lebesgue measure. That is, show $\lambda(T^{-1}(A)) = \lambda(A)$ for every Borel set $A \subseteq [0,1]$.

(*Hint*: It suffices to verify this for intervals $[a,b] \subseteq [0,1]$. Compute $T^{-1}([a,b])$ explicitly.)

**Exercise 7.6** (Conditional expectation as projection). Let $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ be a probability space and $\mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{F}$ a sub-$\sigma$-algebra. Show that if $f \in L^2(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$, then $\mathbb{E}[f \mid \mathcal{G}]$ is the unique element of $L^2(X, \mathcal{G}, \mu)$ that minimizes $\|f - g\|_2$ over all $g \in L^2(X, \mathcal{G}, \mu)$. That is, $\mathbb{E}[f \mid \mathcal{G}]$ is the orthogonal projection of $f$ onto the closed subspace $L^2(X, \mathcal{G}, \mu)$.

(*Hint*: Show that $f - \mathbb{E}[f \mid \mathcal{G}]$ is orthogonal to every $g \in L^2(X, \mathcal{G}, \mu)$, using the defining property of conditional expectation.)

**Exercise 7.7** (Bernoulli measure and the law of large numbers). Let $\mu = \mu_{1/2}$ be the fair-coin Bernoulli measure on $\Sigma_2 = \{0,1\}^{\mathbb{N}}$, and let $\sigma$ be the shift map.

(a) Define $f: \Sigma_2 \to \mathbb{R}$ by $f(\omega) = \omega_0$ (the "observation" function that reads the first coordinate). Show that $f \in L^2(\mu)$ and compute $\int f \, d\mu$ and $\|f\|_2$.

(b) Show that the time average $\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} f(\sigma^n(\omega)) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \omega_n$ is the frequency of 1's among the first $N$ entries of $\omega$.

(c) The strong law of large numbers (which we will recover as a consequence of Birkhoff's ergodic theorem in Chapter 9) asserts that this time average converges to $\int f \, d\mu$ for $\mu$-almost every $\omega$. State precisely what this says about the frequency of 1's in a "typical" binary sequence.
