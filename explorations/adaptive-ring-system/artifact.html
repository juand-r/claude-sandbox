Adaptive Ring System

256 self-modifying rings. Each ring is 36 bits and is at once data, an elementary-CA rule, and a node in a transformation network. No controller, no fitness, no energy — births, deaths, mutation, and a hard cap only.

SLOTS — colour = RULE (position is address, not space)

Each cell is one of the 256 slots. Colour = that ring's 8-bit RULE. Dark = empty. Adjacent cells are not neighbours; rings interact by address via pull/push.

GENOMES — one row per slot, 36 bits

Simulator 2 — Composable Substrate (E21): does complexity grow?

The same rings, but each ring's transformation is now a short program of composable bit-operations (FWD/BACK/FLIP/COPY/XORB/SKIP) instead of one chaotic elementary-CA rule. A standing task selects programs that reproduce a fixed target (bit-complement). Watch used-computation climb past the elementary-CA ceiling (≈2 ops, E15). The corrected finding (EXPERIMENTS.md E21/R5): the composable primitive — not program/data separation — is what breaks the ceiling; both the conflated ring architecture and a separated (Avida-like) variant exceed the plateau.

RINGS — colour = program length

Each cell is a ring; colour = number of instructions in its program (dark = empty/short, bright = long). Under the task, programs lengthen as more computation is rewarded; in the control they collapse to the floor.

COMPLEXITY OVER TIME — used-ops vs the ECA ceiling

green = mean used-computation (effective ops: instructions whose removal changes the output — honest complexity, not raw length); dashed = the elementary-CA plateau (≈2, E15); blue = mean task-fit (0–1). With the task on, green rises above the dashed line.

REFERENCES & RELATED WORK

Background literature for the phenomena this project touches, and why each is relevant. These frame the concepts and the spiral model; they are not inputs to the simulations. (Citation details from memory — verify before formal use.)
  1. Wolfram, "Statistical mechanics of cellular automata," Rev. Mod. Phys. 55 (1983); A New Kind of Science (2002). — Elementary cellular automata and the 0–255 rule numbering used as each ring's transformation primitive.
  2. Cook, "Universality in elementary cellular automata," Complex Systems 15 (2004). — Rule 110 is Turing-complete: a single ECA rule can compute, yet (E15) a single 8-bit rule still is not a composable substrate — context for the complexity ceiling.
  3. Langton, "Computation at the edge of chaos," Physica D 42 (1990). — The order/chaos boundary where information is both stored and transmitted; frames E9's "edge-of-chaos" sweet spot (structured sustained novelty).
  4. Eigen, "Selforganization of matter and the evolution of biological macromolecules," Naturwissenschaften 58 (1971). — Quasispecies and the error threshold: why "zero mutation does not exist" yet too much mutation dissolves heredity (E3 fragility; the mutation/heredity tension).
  5. Maturana & Varela, Autopoiesis and Cognition (1980). — Autopoiesis = a structure that actively maintains itself; the property probed (and not found) for the emergent domains in E5/E5b.
  6. Kauffman, The Origins of Order (1993); autocatalytic sets. — Self-maintaining cycles of mutual production; frames the E1 finding that transformation builds hubs but suppresses cycles, and the autopoiesis question.
  7. May & Leonard, "Nonlinear aspects of competition between three species," SIAM J. Appl. Math. 29 (1975). — Foundational cyclic (rock-paper-scissors) competition dynamics behind E11/E16/E18.
  8. Kerr, Riley, Feldman & Bohannan, "Local dispersal promotes biodiversity in a real-life game of rock-paper-scissors," Nature 418 (2002). — Local interaction maintains coexistence; mirrors our finding that spatial separation (not self-maintenance) sustains the ecology (E5, E16).
  9. Reichenbach, Mobilia & Frey, "Mobility promotes and jeopardizes biodiversity in rock-paper-scissors games," Nature 448 (2007). — The spiral-wave result: spirals require an intermediate mobility (exchange) term. This is exactly the model in E18, and why the no-mobility E16/E17 showed no spirals.
  10. Turing, "The chemical basis of morphogenesis," Phil. Trans. R. Soc. B 237 (1952). — Reaction-diffusion pattern formation; the intellectual lineage of "emergent spatial patterns from local rules."
  11. Ray, "An approach to the synthesis of life" (Tierra), in Artificial Life II (1991). — Self-replicating machine-code organisms seeded from a hand-written ancestor; the template for option 3's plan (self-replication must usually be seeded).
  12. Lenski, Ofria, Pennock & Adami, "The evolutionary origin of complex features," Nature 423 (2003) (Avida). — Complex functions evolve when rewarded via logic tasks on a composable instruction set; directly motivates E15's task and the option-3 substrate.
  13. Moran, "Notes on continuous stochastic phenomena," Biometrika 37 (1950). — Moran's I spatial autocorrelation: the metric used early and then flagged as wrong for categorical rule fields (Reflection R1).
