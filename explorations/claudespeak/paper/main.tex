\title{Claudespeak: Characterizing the Stylistic Fingerprint of \\ a Frontier Language Model}

\author{Anonymous ACL submission}

\begin{abstract}
Large language models are widely believed to have recognizable writing styles,
but most popular claims (e.g.\ that models overuse the word \emph{delve}) are
derived from \emph{ChatGPT} and applied indiscriminately to all models. We ask
what, specifically, characterizes the prose of one frontier model---Anthropic's
Claude Opus 4.8---relative to human writing and to eight other models, holding
topic constant via four parallel corpora that span informational QA, essays, real
in-the-wild prompts, and ten professionally-authored task types. Combining
interpretable stylometry, log-odds $n$-gram selection, a length-controlled
regression, an interpretable classifier, and suffix-automaton ($n$-gram novelty)
mining, we find a stable fingerprint that is \emph{structural, rhythmic, and
interactional} rather than a matter of stereotyped vocabulary. The fingerprint
replicates with consistent sign across all four corpora, and Claude is
identifiable from prose alone in every one (prose-only classifier AUC
$0.79$--$0.95$ across four independent contrasts), driven by markedly higher
sentence-length variability, denser content-word usage, a strong em-dash habit,
and---most diagnostically---a verbatim offer-to-continue closing move (``would you
like me to go deeper into\ldots'') that, in our corpus, appears dozens of times in
Claude and \emph{zero} times across humans and all other models.
We further show that the stereotyped ``delve'' vocabulary is a GPT (and
other-model) trait that Claude \emph{avoids}: across seven models in an essay
genre, Claude is the only model that never writes \emph{delve} and uses
\emph{crucial} least. Finally, we audit corpus coverage and, finding the standard
benchmarks narrow, broaden it with two diverse sources, releasing the fully
provenanced corpus and pipeline. Sections on the effect of reasoning effort,
Claude versions, and multi-turn dynamics are forthcoming.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The claim that text ``sounds like AI'' is now commonplace, and a folk
vocabulary of tells---\emph{delve}, \emph{tapestry}, \emph{crucial},
\emph{intricate}---circulates widely. Two problems undercut these claims as
characterizations of any \emph{particular} model. First, the evidence base is
overwhelmingly about ChatGPT \citep{kobak2024delving}, yet the conclusions are
applied to ``LLMs'' generically. Second, most analyses do not control topic: a
classifier that distinguishes model from human text may be learning the prompt
distribution, not the writing style.

We study a single frontier model, Anthropic's Claude Opus 4.8, and ask a
deliberately narrow question: \emph{holding the prompt fixed, what makes Claude's
prose identifiable relative to humans and to other models?} We build two
topic-matched parallel corpora in which the same prompts are answered by Claude,
by humans, and by up to six other models, and we attack the question with five
complementary methods. Our contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \itemsep0em
  \item A parallel-corpus protocol and a fully provenanced dataset---four tracks,
    eight contrast models and two human anchors, every generation stored with
    model, decoding configuration, token usage, and timestamp---that supports
    reuse without regeneration.
  \item Evidence that Claude's fingerprint is \emph{structural and rhythmic}
    (sentence-length burstiness, content-word density, em-dashes), survives both
    markdown stripping and length control, and \emph{replicates with consistent
    sign across all four corpora} despite different prompt distributions and
    contrast sources.
  \item Identification of a verbatim \emph{interactional} signature---an
    offer-to-continue closer---via log-odds and suffix-automaton mining, present
    in Claude and absent from all other sources in our corpus.
  \item A reconciliation of the ``delve'' folklore: the stereotyped vocabulary
    is a GPT/other-model trait; Claude is the cleanest frontier model on it.
  \item A \emph{coverage taxonomy} (intent $\times$ interaction structure $\times$
    stance) and a reproducible audit showing that standard corpora occupy a
    narrow slice (single-turn, cooperative, informational). We then \emph{act on}
    the audit, broadening coverage with two diverse sources---real in-the-wild
    prompts and ten professionally-authored task types---and show the fingerprint
    persists, while naming the cells that remain under-observed (multi-turn
    corrective, contested-stance) as targets for future work. We release the audit
    tooling.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Detecting machine-generated text.}
A large literature aims to decide \emph{whether} a text is machine-generated.
Supervised detectors fine-tune classifiers such as RoBERTa on model outputs
\citep{solaiman2019release}, while zero-shot methods exploit statistical
signatures of the generating distribution: probability curvature
\citep{mitchell2023detectgpt}, its efficient conditional-sampling successor
\citep{bao2024fastdetectgpt}, structured search over weaker-model features
\citep{verma2024ghostbuster}, or the cross-perplexity of a model pair
\citep{hans2024binoculars}. ``In-the-wild'' benchmarks
\citep{li2024mage,wang2024m4} and \citet{dugan2024raid} show such detectors are
brittle across domains and attacks; paraphrasing in particular evades them
\citep{krishna2023dipper}, with adversarial training
\citep{hu2023radar} as a partial defense and arguments that reliable detection
may be fundamentally hard \citep{sadasivan2023reliable}. Watermarking
\citep{kirchenbauer2023watermark} sidesteps detection by injecting a signal at
generation time. We do not detect machine text; we instead \emph{characterize}
the style of one frontier model, assuming provenance is known.

\paragraph{Attributing text to a model.}
Closer to our aim is work on \emph{which} model wrote a text. Surveys frame this
as authorship attribution in the LLM era \citep{huang2024authorship}, and a recent
forensic survey organizes the field into detection, attribution, and
\emph{characterization} \citep{kumarage2024forensicsurvey}---the last being our
category. Attribution methods range from multi-class benchmarks
\citep{uchendu2021turingbench} to contrastive-perplexity origin tracing
\citep{li2023origintracing}, information-density features
\citep{venkatraman2024gptwho}, learned style representations
\citep{riverasoto2024fewshot}, and interpretable stylometry
\citep{kumarage2023neural}; notably, stylistic signatures persist even in text
optimized to fool detectors \citep{riverasoto2025fingerprints}. We complement
attribution: rather than optimizing a label, we use an interpretable,
length-controlled regression and log-odds $n$-gram selection
\citep{monroe2008fightin} to surface \emph{what} distinguishes Claude, and we
audit which stylistic claims hold up under corpus coverage.

\paragraph{Characterizing model writing style.}
Most directly related is recent work that compares the \emph{style} of LLM and
human text rather than detecting it. \citet{reinhart2025llmwrite} build parallel
human/LLM corpora and apply Biber's grammatical/rhetorical features, finding
systematic, scale-growing differences; \citet{sun2025idiosyncrasies} show models
are mutually distinguishable from word-level idiosyncrasies that survive paraphrase;
and \citet{mcgovern2024fingerprints} recover model ``fingerprints'' from simple
lexical/POS frequencies. Classic stylometry has likewise been applied to AI
creative writing \citep{osullivan2025stylometry}, and a parallel line operationalizes
low-quality ``AI slop'' as measurable dimensions \citep{shaib2025slop}. We share the
parallel-corpus, interpretable-feature stance but go deeper on a single model: we
characterize Claude specifically, isolate an \emph{interactional} closing move that
feature inventories do not capture, add suffix-automaton novelty evidence, and show
the fingerprint replicates across four corpora.

\paragraph{Excess vocabulary and ``AI-ese.''}
\citet{kobak2024delving} quantify LLM-marker words (e.g.\ ``delve'') as excess
vocabulary in the biomedical literature, and corpus-scale estimators track the
rising LLM footprint in papers, peer reviews, and society at large
\citep{liang2024mapping,liang2024monitoring}, with parallel evidence from arXiv
abstracts \citep{geng2024transforming} and even spoken language
\citep{yakura2024spoken}. Popular accounts trace the habit to RLHF annotation
pipelines \citep{willison2024delve}, a hypothesis given peer-reviewed support by
\citet{juzek2024delve}. Such word-lists, however, are largely a ChatGPT/GPT
signature and are unstable over time---``delve'' fell once it was publicly flagged
\citep{geng2025coevolution}. We therefore treat the ``delve'' folklore as a
hypothesis to test rather than a given: our markers are discovered data-drivenly
from parallel corpora, and we show this trait is \emph{not} characteristic of Claude.

\paragraph{Stylometry and $n$-gram novelty.}
Our features build on classical stylometry, notably function-word frequencies
and Burrows's Delta \citep{burrows2002delta}. To probe memorization
\citep{carlini2023memorization} versus genuine novelty, we adopt suffix-automaton
$n$-gram search \citep{merrill2024rustydawg,merrill2024ngramnovelty}, unbounded
$n$-gram indexing \citep{liu2024infinigram}, and related corpus-search tooling
\citep{elazar2024wimbd}, applying novelty mining to a single model's outputs
comparatively. We read novelty as relative distinctiveness, not creativity: it
correlates with but does not equal creative quality
\citep{lu2024creativityindex,saakyan2026deathofnovelty}.

\paragraph{Post-training, persona, and behavioral signatures.}
The voice we characterize is a product of post-training: instruction tuning and
RLHF \citep{ouyang2022instructgpt} and, for Claude, RL from AI feedback
\citep{bai2022constitutional} produce the ``assistant'' and its register, which can
be framed as a trained persona \citep{shanahan2023roleplay} with steerable,
training-induced trait directions \citep{chen2025personavectors}; Anthropic
describes Claude's voice as deliberately shaped by character training
\citep{anthropic2024claudecharacter}. Several of our specific markers have known
post-training causes: reward correlates with length \citep{singhal2023length},
preference models favor lists/bold/emphasis \citep{zhang2024formatbias}, and RLHF
reduces output diversity \citep{kirk2024rlhfdiversity,padmakumar2024diversity}---a
verbosity-and-formatting-and-homogenization profile consistent with what we observe.
Frontier models also exhibit behavioral signatures: verbosity bias in LLM judges
\citep{zheng2023mtbench}, sycophancy and agreement under pressure
\citep{perez2022discovering,sharma2023sycophancy,xu2023askagain}, exaggerated
safety/over-refusal \citep{rottger2024xstest}, persona/identity drift over turns
\citep{li2024instability,choi2024identitydrift}, and self-interaction attractors
such as the ``spiritual bliss'' state reported for Claude
\citep{anthropic2025claude4}. These motivate our future-work agenda on multi-turn
corrective behavior, where stylistic fingerprints may interact with concession and
drift dynamics.

\paragraph{Data.}
We draw on parallel human/LLM corpora including HC3 \citep{guo2023hc3} and
AlpacaEval \citep{alpacaeval2023}, and broaden coverage with real in-the-wild user
prompts from WildChat \citep{zhao2024wildchat} and the professionally
hand-authored No Robots dataset \citep{norobots2023}, repurposing all of them for
comparative stylometric characterization rather than detection or preference
ranking. Other large conversation corpora---LMSYS-Chat-1M and Chatbot Arena
\citep{zheng2024lmsyschat1m,zheng2023mtbench}---offer naturally parallel,
multi-model and multi-turn material for future extensions.

\section{Corpus}
We construct four parallel tracks (Table~\ref{tab:corpus}). In each, a fixed set
of prompts is answered by multiple sources, so that any feature separating Claude
reflects \emph{how} it writes rather than \emph{what} it was asked. Claude
(and GPT-4o on the HC3 track) are generated by us with adaptive thinking at
\texttt{effort=high}; all other cells are reused from the source datasets. Every
record stores full provenance---model and version, decoding configuration,
token usage, timestamp, and code commit---plus the verbatim API response.

The first two tracks (HC3, AlpacaEval) supply a human anchor and a modern
multi-model contrast but, as our coverage audit shows (\S\ref{sec:coverage}), they
occupy a narrow behavioral slice: short, single-turn, information-seeking
\emph{questions}. The last two tracks are added precisely to broaden that slice.
\textbf{WildChat} contributes real, in-the-wild first-turn user prompts (heavy on
creative writing, code, and roleplay) with the original GPT-4-0314 reply as a
reused contrast. \textbf{No Robots} contributes professionally hand-written
prompts \emph{and} answers, balanced across ten task types (generation,
brainstorming, rewriting, summarization, classification, extraction, coding,
chat, and open/closed QA); it is our second human anchor and the first one on
non-QA tasks. Across all tracks there are eight distinct non-Claude models
(GPT-3.5-era ChatGPT, GPT-4o, GPT-4-turbo, GPT-4-0314, Gemini-pro, DeepSeek-67B,
Qwen2-72B, Llama-3-70B) and two human anchors (informal web text in HC3,
professional annotators in No Robots).

\subsection{Corpus statistics}
\label{sec:stats}
Table~\ref{tab:stats} summarizes volume and length. The four tracks span a wide
range of registers and lengths. Prompts grow from terse HC3/AlpacaEval questions

\subsection{Coverage: auditing and expanding the behavioral slice}
\label{sec:coverage}
Because style is context-dependent, we audit \emph{what behavior the prompts
elicit}, labeling every prompt by intent, speech act, register, tone, and topic,
plus interaction flags (\texttt{src/analyze\_coverage.py}; large tracks sampled at
$n{=}400$). Labels are produced by Claude Opus 4.8 itself (the model under study),
a circularity we flag in Limitations.\todo[inline]{TODO (human validation
pending): hand-annotate a $\sim$50-prompt sample, report inter-annotator agreement
with the LLM labels, and add a small validation table. Quick human pass to be
added.}

The audit shows that HC3 and AlpacaEval alone occupy a narrow region: HC3 is 96\%
explanation/QA/advice and 75\% \emph{questions}; AlpacaEval is broader but still
question/directive and information-oriented. Treating this as a coverage problem
to fix rather than merely report, we add the WildChat and No Robots tracks, and
the four tracks are \emph{complementary} (Table~\ref{tab:coverage}): WildChat is
dominated by creative writing (42\%) and code (11\%), No Robots by classification/
extraction (25\%), summarization/rewriting (19\%), and QA (20\%). Intents that
were absent or near-absent in the first two tracks---creative writing, code,
roleplay, summarization, rewriting, classification, extraction, and
personal/emotional---are now substantially represented. The expansion also shifts
the other axes (Appendix~\ref{app:stats}): the dominant speech act flips from HC3's
75\% \emph{questions} to 59--73\% \emph{directives} on the new tracks; a non-trivial
\emph{formal} register appears (10\% of WildChat prompts) where it was nearly
absent; and topics broaden from HC3's health/science/finance concentration to
include entertainment, software, the arts, and personal life.

Two limits remain, and we are explicit about them. First, the corpus is still
\emph{single-turn}: only WildChat shows a non-trivial fraction of prompts that
imply follow-up (7\%), and essentially none are adversarial or corrective, so
behaviors gated on a multi-turn \emph{declarative} challenge---e.g.\ the concession
move ``you're right to push back on that''---remain out of scope and motivate the
multi-turn extension (\S\ref{sec:secondary}). Second, contested-stance prompts
(those that hand Claude an assertion to agree with or contest) are still a small
minority on every track. The fingerprint we report should therefore be read as
Claude's \emph{single-turn} voice---but now across a markedly broader range of
single-turn tasks than informational QA alone.

\section{Methods}
We compute interpretable features per response (lexical: type-token ratio,
hapax rate, function-word rate; punctuation: em-dash, colon, question,
exclamation, emoji; syntax: mean sentence length and \emph{burstiness}, the
ratio of the standard deviation to the mean of words-per-sentence;
markdown/structure; and rhetoric). Rates are per 100 or 1000 words so that text
length does not mechanically dominate. We compute two dozen such features per response. We summarize source differences
with Cohen's $d$ (Claude vs.\ a comparison group); all reported effects are
significant at $p<0.001$ (Welch's $t$, $n{=}200$ Claude vs.\ ${\ge}200$
comparison). The classifier is $\ell_2$-regularized logistic regression
($C{=}1.0$, balanced class weights). To separate \emph{voice} from
\emph{formatting}, we additionally compute all prose features after stripping
markdown markup. We mine characteristic $n$-grams with the
\citet{monroe2008fightin} log-odds estimator; quantify length-independence with
OLS (\S\ref{sec:length}); measure separability with $\ell_2$-regularized logistic
regression under 5-fold cross-validation; and mine arbitrary-length spans and
$n$-gram novelty with a suffix automaton \citep{merrill2024rustydawg}.

\section{Results}

\subsection{A large signal, dominated at first by formatting}
The strongest raw separators between Claude and the other HC3 sources are
markdown-structural: markdown headers ($d{=}2.9$), bold ($d{=}2.3$), and bullets
($d{=}1.8$), which the human and old-ChatGPT cells use essentially never. This
is real but is a \emph{formatting} habit; the decisive question is whether
anything survives removing it.

\subsection{The voice survives markdown stripping}
\label{sec:prose}
With all markup stripped, Claude still separates strongly on prose
(Table~\ref{tab:prose}): it interleaves short and long sentences far more than
any other source (burstiness), writes denser content-word prose (lower
function-word rate), and retains a marked em-dash habit. These are the
load-bearing stylistic findings.

\subsection{The signature is not a length artifact}
\label{sec:length}
On the HC3 track, Claude writes longer answers (mean 253 words vs.\ 120 for
humans, 228 for GPT-4o), so we test whether the prose effects merely track
length. Within every word-count quartile the effects persist, and a
length-controlled regression (HC3)
($\text{feature}\sim\text{is\_claude}+z(\text{n\_words})$) leaves the Claude
coefficient large and highly significant: burstiness $t{=}20.6$, function-word
rate $t{=}{-}14.4$, em-dash $t{=}12.6$, question rate $t{=}4.6$. Type-token ratio
is length-sensitive and we treat lexical-diversity claims as length-conditioned.
The effects also hold \emph{within every domain} (Appendix~\ref{app:domain}):
across all nine domains of the HC3 and AlpacaEval tracks, burstiness has $d>0$,
function-word rate $d<0$ (Claude denser), and em-dash and question rate $d>0$, so
the fingerprint is not a topic artifact; the next section extends this check to two
further corpora.

\subsection{The fingerprint replicates across all four tracks}
\label{sec:crosstrack}
The prose and length analyses above are on HC3. Do the same features distinguish
Claude when the prompt distribution and the contrast source change? We compute
Claude-vs-pooled Cohen's $d$ separately within each track (for WildChat, on the
matched 628-prompt subset, preserving the parallel-corpus control;
\texttt{src/analyze\_tracks.py}). The headline features carry the \emph{same sign
in all four tracks} (Table~\ref{tab:crosstrack}): Claude writes with higher
sentence-length burstiness, denser content-word prose (lower function-word rate),
more em-dashes, more markdown scaffolding, and more questions---against a human
panel, against six modern models, against GPT-4-0314 on real prompts, and against
professional human writers on diverse tasks. Magnitudes are largest on HC3, whose
short, informal contrast cells make the gap widest, and shrink on the harder,
more varied contrasts, but the direction never flips. (Two weaker features---
colon rate and type-token ratio---are \emph{not} sign-stable across tracks and we
do not claim them as part of the fingerprint.) The interactional question habit is
the one headline feature that fades on No Robots ($d{=}0.03$), whose
classification, extraction, and rewriting tasks do not invite an offer to
continue---a sensible boundary condition rather than a failure
(\S\ref{sec:ngram}).

\subsection{The interactional signature: an offer-to-continue closer}
\label{sec:ngram}
Data-driven $n$-gram mining \citep{monroe2008fightin} surfaces a clear
interactional fingerprint rather than a content-word list. The most Claude-like
trigrams (HC3) are \emph{would you like}, \emph{like me to}, \emph{me to
explain}, \emph{in more detail}, \emph{to go deeper}; the anti-signature---terms
Claude \emph{underuses}---is led by the formal frame \emph{it is important to}.
At the sentence level this closer registers as a striking jump in interrogatives:
\textbf{17\%/10\% of Claude's response sentences are questions (HC3/AlpacaEval),
versus $\le$4\% for every other source} (Appendix~\ref{app:stats}).
Suffix-automaton mining (\S\ref{sec:dawg}) confirms the pattern at arbitrary
length. Figure~\ref{fig:example} shows the fingerprint in a single matched pair.

\subsection{The ``delve'' vocabulary is a GPT trait Claude avoids}
\label{sec:delve}
On the AlpacaEval track---an essay/instruction genre that \emph{does} elicit the
stereotyped words---Claude is the cleanest of seven models
(Table~\ref{tab:delve}). It is the only model that never writes \emph{delve} or
\emph{delves}, and it uses \emph{crucial} least of all seven (0.039 per 1k vs.\
DeepSeek 0.054, Gemini 0.090, Llama 0.185, GPT-4o 0.340, Qwen 0.346, GPT-4-turbo
0.422). Claude's own lexical lean is conversational
(``actually'' occurs $\sim$6$\times$ more often in Claude than GPT-4o on HC3);
this conversational lean is a secondary signal beneath the structural one.

\subsection{Separability}
\label{sec:clf}
An interpretable classifier confirms a stable, learnable voice
(Table~\ref{tab:clf}). Stripping markdown reduces AUC only modestly, so the
classifier is not merely reading layout; and its top coefficients are exactly the
features of \S\ref{sec:prose} (burstiness, function-word density, em-dash,
question rate), an independent-method confirmation. Raw length is not a feature,
and residualizing every feature on response length barely changes separation
(prose-only AUC $0.946{\to}0.931$ on HC3, $0.867{\to}0.861$ on AlpacaEval), so the

\subsection{Arbitrary-length spans and $n$-gram novelty}
\label{sec:dawg}
Suffix-automaton mining \citep{merrill2024rustydawg} gives the most concrete form of
the signature. Within each track, spans up to \emph{eight words} occur dozens of
times in Claude and \emph{zero} times across that track's non-Claude sources
(HC3: humans $+$ 2 models; AlpacaEval: 6 models, no human): on HC3,
\emph{would you like me to explain any} ($\times$20) and \emph{would you like me
to go deeper into} ($\times$14); on AlpacaEval, \emph{would you like me to expand
on any} ($\times$7). Claude also has the highest $n$-gram novelty of any source
(Figure~\ref{fig:novelty}; e.g.\ bigram novelty 0.519 vs.\ 0.27--0.36 for the six
AlpacaEval models): its exact phrasing overlaps least with the pool. We read
novelty as relative distinctiveness, not absolute creativity (see Limitations).

\section{Discussion}
Across four corpora, five methods, eight contrast models, and two human anchors,
the same fingerprint recurs and survives every control: Claudespeak is
markdown-scaffolded, rhythmically varied, content-dense, and
engagement-and-offer-oriented prose---and specifically \emph{not} the ``delve''
register. That the signature holds in sign from terse factual QA to in-the-wild
creative writing to constrained extraction tasks suggests it is a property of the
model rather than of any one prompt genre. A methodological lesson follows:
borrowed AI-word lists largely measure \emph{GPT}, and characterizing a specific
model requires data-driven discovery against topic-matched references.

\section*{Limitations}
We study a single Claude configuration (Opus 4.8, \texttt{effort=high}); the
effort, version, and multi-turn axes are not yet measured (\S\ref{sec:secondary}).
Our coverage expansion (\S\ref{sec:coverage}) broadens the corpus well beyond
informational QA---into creative writing, code, summarization, rewriting,
classification, and extraction---but it remains \emph{single-turn}: context-gated
behaviors that require a multi-turn declarative challenge (corrective concession,
sustained refusal) are still out of scope and are the target of the planned
multi-turn extension (English only). The WildChat Claude cells are currently a
628/1500 subset (generation interrupted); all WildChat comparisons use the matched
subset, and the remaining cells and refreshed tables are pending. Reused contrast
completions are their published vintages (e.g.\ GPT-4-0314, gemini-pro~1.0,
Qwen2-72B, GPT-4-turbo/4o~2024), modern-ish but not the absolute latest. $n$-gram
novelty is measured relative to our corpus pool, not a large external reference,
and may be inflated for Claude as the lone out-of-cluster (and only freshly
generated) source. The two human anchors differ in register---HC3 is informal web
text, No Robots is professional annotation---which lets us check that the
human--Claude gap is not merely an artifact of informal writing (the fingerprint
holds against both), though neither is edited published prose. The coverage
audit's labels are produced by an LLM (the model under study), introducing a
circularity; a human-validation pass on a labeled sample is pending. Finally, raw
length is excluded from the classifier features and the feature effects are
length-controlled (\S\ref{sec:length}), but some prose features still correlate
with length; we report a length-residualized classifier as a check
(\S\ref{sec:clf}).

\section*{Data and Reproducibility}
We do not test mechanism, but the interactional, low-formality, offer-oriented
profile is consistent with post-training (RLHF) that rewards helpful, engaging,
and appropriately hedged responses. Code and the fully provenanced corpus
(generations plus metadata, and the audit tooling) will be released at an
anonymized URL. HC3, AlpacaEval, WildChat, and No Robots are public research
artifacts used under their licenses; we redistribute only derived statistics and
our own model generations.

\section*{Ethics Statement}
This work characterizes model writing style using public datasets and model
outputs; it involves no human subjects or private data. Stylistic fingerprints
can aid provenance and detection but could also inform evasion; we report
aggregate, interpretable features rather than a deployable detector.
