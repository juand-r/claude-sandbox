\chapter{Entropy Rate and the Identity $h = \log(1/R)$}
\label{ch09:top}

This chapter builds a bridge between information theory and generating functions. The bridge is real, but one must keep the objects straight. There are three related quantities that are easy to confuse:
\begin{itemize}
  \item the Shannon entropy rate of a probabilistic source,
  \item the growth rate of a typical set,
  \item and the length distribution of outputs from a halting model.
\end{itemize}
Only the second of these is directly encoded by the counting-type generating function that leads to the identity
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:h-log-R}
  h = \log_2(1/R).
\end{equation}
A main goal of the chapter is to make that distinction explicit.

\section{Entropy Rate}

Let $X_1,X_2,\dots$ be a stationary stochastic process over a finite alphabet $\Sigma$. Define the block entropy
\[
  H_n := H(X_1^n) = -\sum_{w \in \Sigma^n} p(w) \log_2 p(w),
\]
where $p(w)=\Pr(X_1\cdots X_n = w)$.

\begin{proposition}
For a stationary finite-alphabet source, the limit
\[
  h = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{H_n}{n}
\]
exists. Moreover,
\[
  h = \lim_{n \to \infty} H(X_n \mid X_1^{n-1}).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The chain rule gives
\[
  H_{m+n} = H(X_1^{m+n}) \le H(X_1^m) + H(X_{m+1}^{m+n}).
\]
By stationarity,
\[
  H(X_{m+1}^{m+n}) = H(X_1^n),
\]
so $(H_n)$ is subadditive:
\[
  H_{m+n} \le H_m + H_n.
\]
A standard subadditivity theorem therefore implies that $H_n/n$ has a limit.

Also,
\[
  H_n = \sum_{t=1}^n H(X_t \mid X_1^{t-1})
\]
by the chain rule. Stationarity implies that the sequence of conditional entropies
\[
  H(X_n \mid X_1^{n-1})
\]
is nonincreasing in $n$ and bounded below by $0$, so it converges. Its Ces\`aro average is exactly $H_n/n$, hence both limits agree.
\end{proof}

The quantity $h$ is the average uncertainty per new symbol once arbitrarily long context is known. It always satisfies
\[
  0 \le h \le \log_2 |\Sigma|.
\]
The upper bound is attained by the uniform i.i.d. source; in that case every new symbol is maximally unpredictable.

\section{Shannon--McMillan--Breiman and Typical Sets}

From this point through the typical-set construction, assume in addition that the stationary source is ergodic. The key imported theorem is the asymptotic equipartition property.

\begin{theorem}[Shannon--McMillan--Breiman, used as a black box]
For an ergodic stationary source with entropy rate $h$,
\[
  -\frac{1}{n}\log_2 p(X_1\cdots X_n) \to h
\]
almost surely.
\end{theorem}

This motivates the usual typical sets.

\begin{definition}
For $\varepsilon > 0$, the $\varepsilon$-typical set at length $n$ is
\[
  T_n^{(\varepsilon)} = \left\{w \in \Sigma^n : \left| -\frac{1}{n}\log_2 p(w) - h \right| < \varepsilon \right\}.
\]
\end{definition}

For large $n$, most of the probability mass lies in $T_n^{(\varepsilon)}$, and every typical word has probability between
\[
  2^{-n(h+\varepsilon)} \quad\text{and}\quad 2^{-n(h-\varepsilon)}.
\]
From this one obtains the standard cardinality estimate:
\[
  (1-\delta_n)2^{n(h-\varepsilon)} \le |T_n^{(\varepsilon)}| \le 2^{n(h+\varepsilon)},
\]
where
\[
  \delta_n = 1 - \Pr(X_1^n \in T_n^{(\varepsilon)}) \to 0.
\]
The lower bound comes from the fact that the typical set carries probability close to $1$, while each individual typical word has probability at most $2^{-n(h-\varepsilon)}$.

\section{A Corrected Typical-Set Identity}

The displayed bounds for a \emph{fixed} $\varepsilon$ only pin down the exponential growth rate up to an $\varepsilon$-window. To obtain the exact exponent $h$, we let the window shrink with $n$.

Choose a sequence
\[
  \varepsilon_n \downarrow 0
\]
slowly enough that
\[
  \Pr(X_1^n \in T_n^{(\varepsilon_n)}) \to 1.
\]
(One can do this because the Shannon--McMillan--Breiman theorem says the deviations from $h$ vanish almost surely.) Now define
\[
  a_n := \left|T_n^{(\varepsilon_n)}\right|.
\]
For completeness one may set $a_0=1$; the asymptotic argument concerns large $n$.
There is no single canonical choice of the sequence $\varepsilon_n$, but the exponential growth rate below does not depend on that choice.

From the cardinality bounds we get
\[
  (1-\delta_n)2^{n(h-\varepsilon_n)} \le a_n \le 2^{n(h+\varepsilon_n)},
\]
with $\delta_n \to 0$. Taking logarithms and dividing by $n$ gives
\[
  h-\varepsilon_n + \frac{1}{n}\log_2(1-\delta_n) \le \frac{1}{n}\log_2 a_n \le h+\varepsilon_n.
\]
Since both error terms go to $0$, we obtain
\[
  \frac{1}{n}\log_2 a_n \to h.
\]
Therefore
\[
  a_n^{1/n} \to 2^h.
\]
Now form the counting-type generating function
\[
  A(z) = \sum_{n \ge 0} a_n z^n.
\]
By Cauchy--Hadamard,
\[
  \frac{1}{R} = \limsup_{n \to \infty} a_n^{1/n} = 2^h.
\]
So indeed
\[
  h = \log_2(1/R).
\]
This is the corrected form of the identity: it applies to an \emph{entropy-typical counting generating function}, built from a shrinking typicality window.

\section{A Crucial Distinction: Counting Growth vs. Length Distribution}

The identity above does \emph{not} apply to the length PGF of a halting model. If
\[
  P(z) = \sum_{n \ge 0} p_n z^n,
  \qquad p_n = \Pr(|W|=n),
\]
then
\[
  P(1)=\sum_{n \ge 0} p_n = 1
\]
for a proper model, so the radius of convergence satisfies
\[
  R_P \ge 1.
\]
Thus
\[
  \log_2(1/R_P) \le 0,
\]
which cannot encode a positive entropy rate.

So we must keep two generating functions separate.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A \textbf{counting-type typical-set GF}
  \[
    A(z)=\sum a_n z^n,
  \]
  whose coefficients grow like $2^{nh}$ and whose radius encodes entropy rate.
  \item A \textbf{length probability generating function}
  \[
    P(z)=\sum p_n z^n,
  \]
  whose singularity structure describes tail behavior of output length.
\end{enumerate}

A concrete example helps. For a halting model with geometric length distribution,
\[
  p_n = q(1-q)^n,
\]
one gets
\[
  P(z) = \sum_{n \ge 0} q(1-q)^n z^n = \frac{q}{1-(1-q)z},
\]
whose radius is
\[
  R_P = \frac{1}{1-q} > 1.
\]
This radius says something about how fast long outputs become unlikely. It says nothing by itself about Shannon entropy rate.

\section{Finite-State Sources: Topological vs. Shannon Entropy}

This is the place where one must be especially careful. For a finite-state automaton or subshift of finite type, the number of \emph{allowed paths} of length $n$ often grows like
\[
  \rho(M)^n,
\]
where $M$ is an adjacency matrix. The corresponding logarithm
\[
  \log_2 \rho(M)
\]
is a \emph{support-growth} or \emph{topological} entropy.

That is not the same as Shannon entropy rate of a probabilistic source in general.

\begin{example}[Biased one-state source]
Take one state and two emitted symbols, $0$ and $1$, with probabilities $0.9$ and $0.1$. Every binary word of length $n$ is allowed, so the support-counting growth rate is
\[
  2^n,
  \qquad
  \log_2 2 = 1.
\]
But the Shannon entropy rate is
\[
  h = -0.9\log_2 0.9 - 0.1\log_2 0.1 < 1.
\]
So path counting and Shannon entropy are genuinely different quantities.
\end{example}

The identity $h = \log_2(1/R)$ in this chapter applies to the entropy-typical counting object, not to naive support counting of all admissible paths.

\section{Entropy Estimation: Classical Results and Modern Reinterpretation}

The historical literature estimates Shannon entropy rate directly, not by literally writing down a typical-set generating function.

\paragraph{Shannon (1951).}
Shannon's guessing experiments \cite{Shannon1951} produced upper and lower bounds for the entropy rate of printed English. In the language of this chapter, one may \emph{reinterpret} those estimates as probing the exponential growth rate of near-typical sets. That reinterpretation is modern and conceptual; it is not something Shannon himself formulated in generating-function language.

\paragraph{Kontoyiannis et al.
(1998).}
Kontoyiannis, Algoet, Suhov, and Wyner \cite{KontoyiannisEtAl1998} study entropy-rate estimation through longest-match statistics. Their work is rigorous information theory. The connection to generating functions is again interpretive: these estimators measure the same entropy rate $h$ that appears in the exponential growth of entropy-typical sets.

\paragraph{Modern neural estimates.}
Takahashi and Tanaka-Ishii \cite{TakahashiTanakaIshii2018} and Scheibner, Smith, and Bialek \cite{ScheibnerSmithBialek2025} provide increasingly refined empirical estimates of the entropy rate of natural language using neural models and long contexts. It is reasonable to read these as sharper measurements of the same quantity $h$, but the present chapter does not construct a canonical ``English generating function'' whose radius is being measured directly.

\section{A Research Hypothesis: Convergence Rate and Singularity Type}

The location of the dominant singularity of a typical-set counting GF determines the entropy rate. A more ambitious question is whether the \emph{type} of that singularity leaves a detectable signature in the finite-context convergence of entropy estimators.

This chapter does \emph{not} prove such a theorem. Instead, we record the idea as a research hypothesis.

\begin{itemize}
  \item In simple finite-memory models, entropy estimates often converge rapidly.
  \item In more structured or long-range sources, the convergence can be much slower.
  \item It is plausible that singularity type on the counting side and estimator convergence on the information-theoretic side are related.
\end{itemize}

But turning that philosophy into a theorem would require a much more precise bridge than we have built here. So the safe conclusion is only this: long-context entropy drift is suggestive, not diagnostic. It may be a clue about deeper singularity structure, but at present that is part of the research program rather than an established consequence of Chapters~1--9.

\section*{Summary}

This chapter's strongest rigorous conclusion is the corrected identity
\[
  h = \log_2(1/R),
\]
where $R$ is the radius of convergence of an entropy-typical counting generating function built from a shrinking typicality window. Its strongest conceptual lesson is the distinction between that counting object and the length PGF of a halting model.

The next parts of the book must keep that distinction visible. Support growth, entropy rate, and output-length tails are related, but they are not interchangeable.
