\chapter{Probabilistic Grammars and Tightness}
\label{ch08:top}

The chapters so far mostly studied counting generating functions. This chapter changes the question. We now care about objects that are supposed to define genuine probability distributions on finite outputs. In that setting, local normalization is not enough. A recursive model may still place positive probability on never terminating, so that the total mass on finite strings is strictly less than $1$.

That is the conceptual core of the chapter. For probabilistic grammars and autoregressive language models alike, we must distinguish:
\begin{itemize}
  \item local conditional probabilities,
  \item the global probability of finite outputs,
  \item and the question of almost-sure termination.
\end{itemize}
The condition
\[
  F(1)=1
\]
for a length probability generating function is the right probabilistic sanity check. It says we truly have a probability distribution on finite lengths. It is \emph{not} a blanket prerequisite for analytic combinatorics as a formal method; defective generating functions can still be studied analytically. What fails first when $F(1)<1$ is the intended probability interpretation.

\section{Probabilistic Context-Free Grammars}

A \emph{probabilistic context-free grammar} (PCFG) is a context-free grammar in which, for each nonterminal $A$, the productions with left-hand side $A$ are assigned probabilities summing to $1$.

If a derivation uses productions $r_1,r_2,\dots,r_m$, then its probability is
\[
  \prod_{i=1}^m p(r_i).
\]
The probability of a terminal string $w$ is the sum of those derivation probabilities over all derivations producing $w$. So ambiguity matters exactly as it did in the counting setting, except that now we sum probabilities rather than counts.

\begin{example}
Suppose the grammar has one rule of probability $q$ and one rule of probability $1-q$. Then a derivation using the first rule three times and the second twice has probability $q^3(1-q)^2$.
\end{example}

\section{Branching-Process Intuition}

A PCFG derivation can be viewed as a population of unfinished nonterminals. Each time a nonterminal is expanded, it disappears and is replaced by whatever nonterminals occur on the right-hand side of the chosen production. That is the branching-process viewpoint.

For example, consider
\[
  S \to SS \quad (q),
  \qquad
  S \to a \quad (1-q).
\]
One expansion of $S$ produces either two new copies of $S$ or none. So the expected number of new nonterminals created by one old nonterminal is
\[
  2q.
\]
If this expectation is large, the derivation tends to keep reproducing unfinished work instead of terminating.

\section{Consistency and the First-Moment Matrix}

\begin{definition}
A PCFG is \emph{consistent} if the total probability of finite terminal derivations is $1$.
\end{definition}

Equivalently, starting from the start symbol, the derivation terminates almost surely.

The standard summary statistic is the \emph{first-moment matrix} $M$. Its entry $M_{A,B}$ is the expected number of occurrences of nonterminal $B$ created by one random expansion of $A$.

For the one-nonterminal grammar above,
\[
  M = [2q].
\]
So the spectral radius is just
\[
  \rho(M)=2q.
\]

Booth and Thompson analyzed consistency through this matrix, but one must quote the sharp theorem carefully. The safe lessons for this book are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item if $\rho(M)<1$, then the grammar is subcritical and terminates almost surely;
  \item if $\rho(M)>1$, then there is positive probability of infinite derivation;
  \item the critical case $\rho(M)=1$ is delicate and can contain both ordinary critical branching behavior and degenerate loops.
\end{itemize}

The toy grammar
\[
  S \to S \quad (1)
\]
shows why care is needed: here $\rho(M)=1$, but the derivation never terminates. So any blanket ``iff $\rho(M) \le 1$'' formulation needs extra nondegeneracy hypotheses.

\section{Length PGFs and What `F(1)=1` Means}

Let $q_{A,n}$ be the probability that a derivation rooted at nonterminal $A$ yields a terminal string of length exactly $n$. Define the length PGF
\[
  F_A(z) = \sum_{n \ge 0} q_{A,n} z^n.
\]
Then
\[
  F_A(1) = \sum_{n \ge 0} q_{A,n}
\]
is exactly the total probability that $A$ eventually produces a finite terminal string.

So for the start symbol $S$,
\[
  F_S(1)=1
\]
means consistency, while
\[
  F_S(1)<1
\]
means that positive probability mass has escaped into infinite derivations.

That distinction is probabilistic, not purely analytic. A defective PGF may still have a radius of convergence and singularities; it simply no longer encodes a genuine probability distribution on finite outputs.

\section{Chi's Theorem}

Chi proved an important result for PCFG estimation from treebanks.

\begin{theorem}[Chi, used as a black box]
A maximum-likelihood PCFG estimated from a finite treebank is consistent.
\end{theorem}

For this book, the right interpretation is modest: maximum-likelihood estimation removes one foundational obstruction, namely defectiveness. It does \emph{not} automatically guarantee all the later hypotheses one might want for singularity analysis, such as algebraicity, aperiodicity, or a specific dominant singularity type.

\section{Autoregressive Language Models}

Now turn to autoregressive models over a vocabulary $\Sigma$ with an explicit end-of-sequence token $\EOS$. Such a model specifies conditional probabilities
\[
  p(\cdot \mid h)
\]
for every history $h \in \Sigma^*$. The probability of a finite string
\[
  w = w_1 w_2 \cdots w_n
\]
is
\[
  p(w) = p(\EOS \mid w_1\cdots w_n) \prod_{t=1}^n p(w_t \mid w_1\cdots w_{t-1}).
\]
Again, local normalization at each history does not by itself imply that
\[
  \sum_{w \in \Sigma^*} p(w) = 1.
\]
The model may keep generating forever with positive probability.

\section{Tightness and the Hazard Criterion}

To formalize this, one enlarges the sample space to include infinite strings as well as finite ones. We use that measure-theoretic construction as a black box and focus on the resulting definition.

\begin{definition}
An autoregressive language model is \emph{tight} if it halts almost surely, equivalently if the total probability of finite strings is $1$.
\end{definition}

The key quantity is the average stopping hazard at time $t$. Let
\[
  \widetilde p_{\EOS}(t)
\]
denote the conditional probability of halting at step $t$, averaged over all histories of length $t-1$ that have survived that long.

The crucial product formula is
\[
  \Pr(T \ge t+1) = \prod_{s=1}^{t} \bigl(1-\widetilde p_{\EOS}(s)\bigr),
\]
where $T$ is the random halting time. This simply says: to survive past time $t$, the process must fail to halt at each earlier step.

From this one gets the tightness criterion used by Du et al.

\begin{theorem}[Du et al., used as a black box]
An autoregressive language model is tight if and only if
\[
  \sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \widetilde p_{\EOS}(t) = \infty.
\]
\end{theorem}

The intuition is that the infinite product above vanishes exactly when the cumulative stopping hazard is large enough. So divergent total hazard means almost-sure halting.

A very useful sufficient condition is the following. If there exists $\varepsilon > 0$ such that
\[
  p(\EOS \mid h) \ge \varepsilon
  \qquad\text{for every history } h,
\]
then certainly
\[
  \widetilde p_{\EOS}(t) \ge \varepsilon
\]
for every $t$, so the divergent-series criterion holds and the model is tight.

\section{Examples}

\begin{example}[A non-tight model]
Suppose along a deterministic path the EOS probability at time $t$ is
\[
  \widetilde p_{\EOS}(t) = 2^{-t}.
\]
Then
\[
  \sum_{t \ge 1} 2^{-t} < \infty,
\]
so the model is not tight: with positive probability it generates forever.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Barely tight and heavy-tailed]
Suppose instead that
\[
  \widetilde p_{\EOS}(t) = \frac{1}{t+1}.
\]
Then
\[
  \Pr(T \ge n+1) = \prod_{t=1}^{n} \frac{t}{t+1} = \frac{1}{n+1},
\]
so
\[
  \Pr(T=n) = \Pr(T \ge n)\widetilde p_{\EOS}(n) = \frac{1}{n(n+1)} \sim \frac{1}{n^2}.
\]
This model is tight, but only barely. Its expected length diverges because
\[
  \sum_{n \ge 1} n\Pr(T=n) \asymp \sum_{n \ge 1} \frac{1}{n}
\]
diverges.
\end{example}

The point is not that all borderline models have the same singularity type. They do not. Some critical branching models produce square-root singularities, while some heavy-tailed sequential hazards lead to softer singular behavior. The only safe universal message is that near-critical termination can produce heavy tails and long outputs.

\section{Transformer Tightness}

Du et al.
prove a tightness theorem for a broad class of softmax transformer language models. We treat that theorem as a black box here.

\begin{theorem}[Du et al., black box]
Under the hypotheses of their framework, softmax transformer language models are tight.
\end{theorem}

We deliberately do \emph{not} reproduce the proof sketch. The real argument is architectural and measure-theoretic, and a vague appeal to ``softmax is always positive'' is not enough to justify it. For this chapter, the safe takeaway is simply that there are important modern model classes for which tightness has been proved, not guessed.

\section{The Common Lesson}

The PCFG story and the autoregressive story are parallel.
\begin{itemize}
  \item In a PCFG, the local normalization is over productions expanding one nonterminal.
  \item In an autoregressive model, the local normalization is over next-token probabilities given a history.
\end{itemize}
In both settings, local normalization does not by itself imply a proper distribution on finite outputs. One still needs an almost-sure termination theorem.

Once properness is known, the length PGF satisfies
\[
  F(1)=1.
\]
That is the correct probability-theoretic checkpoint. It does not, by itself, determine rationality, algebraicity, or singularity type. Those are separate structural questions.
