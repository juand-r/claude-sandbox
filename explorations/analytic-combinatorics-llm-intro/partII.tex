\part{Formal Languages and Probabilistic Sources}

Part~I treated combinatorial classes abstractly. This part grounds the theory in formal languages --- the subsets of $\Sigma^*$ generated by grammars and recognized by machines. Chapter~6 surveys the Chomsky hierarchy (regular, context-free, context-sensitive, recursively enumerable) and its connection to the rational/algebraic GF dichotomy. Chapter~7 introduces weighted finite automata, whose generating functions are automatically rational, and the Kleene--Sch\"utzenberger theorem. Chapter~8 asks when a probabilistic grammar or an autoregressive model defines a genuine probability distribution (tightness), connecting Booth--Thompson's 1973 criterion with Du et al.'s 2023 measure-theoretic framework. Chapter~9 establishes the entropy-rate identity $h = \log(1/R)$ and carefully distinguishes the counting GF from the length PGF --- a distinction that will matter throughout Parts~III--V.
