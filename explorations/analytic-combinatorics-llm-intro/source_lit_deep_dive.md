## 1. Foundational Analytic Combinatorics

*Threads: T1, T2*

The technical backbone of this project is the program that Philippe Flajolet and his collaborators built over four decades and which is now codified in [Flajolet and Sedgewick (2009)](https://ac.cs.princeton.edu/home/) *Analytic Combinatorics*. The book's organizing insight is that a combinatorial class $\mathcal{C}$ with size function $|\cdot|$ can be encoded by a generating function $C(z) = \sum_{n \ge 0} c_n z^n$, and that operations on classes — disjoint union, Cartesian product, sequence, set, multiset, cycle, pointing, substitution — translate mechanically into operations on generating functions. This is the *symbolic method* of Part A: a dictionary that turns specifications into functional equations without ever computing a coefficient. For our purposes the symbolic method is the entry point to T1, because an autoregressive language model's output language, viewed as a probability-weighted set of finite strings, is exactly the kind of object that admits a class specification once one decides what counts as an "atom" (token, sentence, paragraph) and how termination is enforced (an explicit EOS atom, a geometric sequence construction, or a marked terminal class). The marking variables that the symbolic method threads through products and sequences are also what make T2 tractable: by tagging nonterminal expansions one obtains a bivariate generating function whose marginal in the marking variable is the length distribution conditional on a structural feature.

Part B of *Analytic Combinatorics* then supplies the analytic half of the program. Once $C(z)$ is in hand as a function of a complex variable, the asymptotics of $c_n$ are read off from the dominant singularities of $C$ on its circle of convergence. The exponential growth rate is $1/R$ where $R$ is the radius of convergence, and the subexponential correction is determined by the local behavior of $C$ near its singularities. This last step is the content of [Flajolet and Odlyzko (1990)](https://epubs.siam.org/doi/10.1137/0403019), "Singularity Analysis of Generating Functions" (*SIAM J. Discrete Math.* 3(2):216–240), which proves the *transfer theorems* that license writing
$$[z^n]\,(1-z/\rho)^{-\alpha} \,\sim\, \frac{n^{\alpha-1}}{\Gamma(\alpha)}\,\rho^{-n}$$
and, more generally, allow one to transfer any sufficiently regular local expansion of $C$ near $\rho$ into a coefficient asymptotic of the form $c_n \sim \rho^{-n} n^{\alpha-1} (\log n)^{\beta}/\Gamma(\alpha)$. For T1, this is precisely the machine that converts a hypothesized singular structure of an LLM's length generating function into a *prediction* about the empirical distribution of output lengths: the tail should decay geometrically at rate $\rho$ with a polynomial prefactor whose exponent reports the type of singularity. For T2, the same theorem produces the celebrated $n^{-3/2}$ correction characteristic of square-root singularities (exponent $1/2$) arising from the Chomsky–Schützenberger theorem on unambiguous context-free languages, where $C(z)$ is algebraic and its dominant singularity is generically a branch point.

[Melczer, Pemantle and Wilson's (2024)](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/analytic-combinatorics-in-several-variables/85EE6D15D39046E4A5C44136552EDC06) *Analytic Combinatorics in Several Variables* (2nd ed., Cambridge University Press) extends the same philosophy to multivariate generating functions $F(z_1, \dots, z_d)$ where one wants the asymptotics of $[z_1^{n_1} \cdots z_d^{n_d}] F$ along a ray. The technical apparatus — stratified Morse theory on the singular variety, the contribution of critical points to a saddle-point integral, height functions cut out by the direction of asymptotic interest — is exactly what lets us promote univariate length analysis to joint laws over (length, depth), (length, branching), or (length, type-token statistics). T1 in its strong form is bivariate: we want to ask not only how long outputs are but how length covaries with structural marks, and ACSV is the only systematic toolkit for that question.

[Drmota's (2009)](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-3-211-75357-6) *Random Trees: An Interplay between Combinatorics and Probability* (Springer) specializes the same machinery to the setting that is closest to what an LLM's parse-like outputs actually are. Generating functions for simply generated tree families satisfy fixed-point equations of the form $T(z) = z\,\Phi(T(z))$, whose singularities are governed by the implicit-function characteristic system; near the branch point one finds the universal square-root behavior, and Drmota's saddle-point and quasi-power theorems then deliver Gaussian limit laws for additive functionals — depth, number of leaves, occurrences of a pattern. This is the bridge from raw coefficient asymptotics to the *distributional* statements T2 needs, since the Chomsky–Schützenberger test is fundamentally a statement about the limit law of length under a context-free generating mechanism.

Finally, [Drmota and Szpankowski's (2023)](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/analytic-information-theory/D8FDCBAB0D2CB12E1A2B5D21F1D2BCFB) *Analytic Information Theory: From Compression to Learning* (Cambridge University Press) closes the loop with information theory. Their toolbox — Mellin transforms, depoissonization lemmas that move between Poissonized and fixed-$n$ models, and singularity analysis applied to entropy, redundancy and source-coding quantities — is what lets us reinterpret the singular exponents of T1 as information-theoretic invariants of the model's output process, and to compare an LLM's empirical length law against the analytic predictions in entropy-rate units rather than raw counts.

## 2. Weighted Automata and Weighted Formal Languages

*Thread: T5*

The natural algebraic home for any "approximate an LLM by a finite-state device" program is the theory of weighted finite automata (WFA) and the noncommutative rational series they recognize. The encyclopedic reference here is the [*Handbook of Weighted Automata*, edited by Droste, Kuich and Vogler (2009)](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-3-642-01492-5), which develops WFAs uniformly over an arbitrary semiring $(K, \oplus, \otimes, 0, 1)$. A WFA is a tuple $(\alpha, \{A_\sigma\}_{\sigma \in \Sigma}, \beta)$ with $\alpha \in K^{1 \times n}$, $\beta \in K^{n \times 1}$, and transition matrices $A_\sigma \in K^{n \times n}$, assigning to each word $w = \sigma_1 \cdots \sigma_k$ the weight $f(w) = \alpha\, A_{\sigma_1} \cdots A_{\sigma_k}\, \beta$. The case relevant to language modeling is the nonnegative real semiring $K = (\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}, +, \times)$, where the matrices can be normalized so that $f(w)$ is the probability of $w$ under a hidden-Markov-like generative process; stochastic WFAs in this sense are exactly the "rational stochastic languages" of the literature, and they strictly contain HMM-induced distributions while remaining strictly inside the rational class. The Handbook makes precise that every closure property one might want — sum, Hadamard product, concatenation, Kleene star, intersection with regular languages, marginalization — is a matrix operation on the $A_\sigma$, and that the minimal $n$ is computed by a Hankel-matrix rank, which is what later spectral learning algorithms exploit.

[Berstel and Reutenauer's *Noncommutative Rational Series with Applications* (2011)](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/noncommutative-rational-series-with-applications/CADC75E4ADDA69E99BB8B0D8FE9AD119) provides the algebraic spine: a formal power series $S \in K\langle\!\langle \Sigma^*\rangle\!\rangle$ is *rational* iff it is *recognizable* iff it is computed by some WFA — the noncommutative Kleene–Schützenberger theorem. This equivalence licenses moving freely between an automaton, a system of linear equations, and a closed-form series. The crucial fact for thread T5 is the following: if one collapses a rational series over a finite alphabet down to its commutative image by the substitution $\sigma \mapsto z$ for every $\sigma \in \Sigma$, the resulting univariate generating function
$$F(z) \;=\; \sum_{w \in \Sigma^*} f(w)\, z^{|w|} \;=\; \alpha\,\bigl(I - z\textstyle\sum_{\sigma} A_\sigma\bigr)^{-1}\beta$$
is a *rational function* of $z$ with poles exactly at the reciprocals of the eigenvalues of $A := \sum_\sigma A_\sigma$. This is the entry point to Flajolet–Sedgewick singularity analysis: rational GFs are the easiest possible case, because their asymptotics are governed entirely by the dominant pole $z_0 = 1/\rho(A)$, and partial-fraction expansion gives an exact formula
$$[z^n]\,F(z) \;=\; \sum_{i} P_i(n)\, \rho_i^n, \qquad P_i \in \mathbb{C}[n],$$
with the dominant term $C\,\rho(A)^n n^{m-1}$ where $m$ is the multiplicity. Asymptotic tightness is not a theorem one has to fight for; it is built into the algebra. For T5 this means that *any* WFA approximation of an LLM automatically yields a length distribution, partition function, and entropy rate whose leading-order behavior is computable from a single matrix spectrum — and the error of the approximation can be tracked by how the dominant eigenvalue and its eigenprojector move as the WFA is refined.

What WFAs *cannot* express is equally important, and this is where [Icard's "Calibrating Generative Models: The Probabilistic Chomsky–Schützenberger Hierarchy" (2020)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S002224961930166X) enters. Icard organizes the standard Chomsky levels not by the languages they accept but by the probability distributions on $\Sigma^*$ they generate, and shows that — in contrast to the classical setting, where some adjacent language classes collapse — the probabilistic hierarchy is non-collapsing: at each successive level strictly more distributions become definable. Concretely, distributions whose generating functions are algebraic but irrational, of the kind that arise from probabilistic context-free / pushdown mechanisms, sit one level above the rational stochastic (WFA-expressible) class and cannot be matched by any finite-dimensional linear recurrence. Icard's hierarchy is therefore the sharp obstruction theorem for thread T5: it tells us exactly which features of an LLM's output distribution a WFA approximation can in principle capture (anything compatible with a rational GF and a finite Hankel rank) and which it must necessarily smooth away (context-sensitive structure whose GF has branch points rather than poles). [Smith and Johnson (2007), "Weighted and Probabilistic Context-Free Grammars Are Equally Expressive"](https://aclanthology.org/J07-4003/) complements this picture from the other side: they prove that the *conditional* distribution of parses given strings defined by any weighted CFG — even one whose partition function diverges — is also expressible by some PCFG. This collapses two seemingly distinct context-free probabilistic formalisms at the level of conditional distributions over derivations, so the gap Icard identifies between the rational and algebraic levels is a genuine expressiveness gap and not an artifact of a particular weighting convention.

The loop back to neural models is closed by [Rabusseau, Li and Precup (2019), "Connecting Weighted Automata and Recurrent Neural Networks through Spectral Learning"](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v89/rabusseau19a.html). They show that a second-order (multiplicative) RNN *with linear activation functions* is expressively equivalent to a WFA over $\mathbb{R}$ — the linearity hypothesis is essential, and the equivalence does not extend to nonlinear 2-RNNs. They then extend the spectral learning algorithm for vector-valued WFAs to give a provable, consistent learning procedure for linear 2-RNNs over sequences of continuous input vectors, by recovering the model's parameters from low-rank sub-blocks of a Hankel tensor. The practical consequence for T5 is that a trained linear 2-RNN — and, by linearization arguments, a locally linearized transformer — can be projected onto a WFA of controlled rank $n$, after which the entire Berstel–Reutenauer / Flajolet–Sedgewick machinery applies to the projection: rational GF, dominant-pole asymptotics, exact length distribution, computable entropy rate, and an Icard-style certificate of which distributional features were necessarily lost. Together these five works supply the algebraic substrate, the asymptotic toolkit, the expressiveness ceiling, and the neural-to-automaton bridge that T5 needs in order to turn "approximate the LLM by a WFA" into a quantitative statement about generating-function singularities.

## 3. Formal Language Theory and Transformers

*Thread: T5*

The question that animates this thread is deceptively simple: when a transformer language model assigns probabilities to strings, what kind of formal object is it, really? The answer matters because the analytic-combinatorics program of T5 needs a concrete handle on the generating function $L(z) = \sum_{n \geq 0} p_n z^n$ induced by an LM, and that handle is sharpest when the underlying probability assignment can be located inside a known automata-theoretic class. If an LM is (approximately) a weighted finite automaton, then $L(z)$ is rational, its singularities are algebraic, and the asymptotics of $[z^n]L(z)$ follow from a finite spectral picture. If it is strictly more, the generating function may be algebraic, $D$-finite, or wilder still, and the singularity structure inflates accordingly. Pinning down which regime we are in is the prerequisite for any clean asymptotic story about LLM output distributions.

The modern survey by [Strobl, Merrill, Weiss, Chiang and Angluin (2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.00208) gives the cleanest map of the territory. Their organising idea is to grade transformer variants by the circuit complexity class they fall into, and the headline result is that essentially every "vanilla" transformer one might care about — fixed depth, soft or hard attention, log-precision arithmetic, no intermediate scratchpad — lives inside $\mathsf{TC}^0$, the class of problems solvable by constant-depth threshold circuits of polynomial size. This sits strictly inside $\mathsf{NC}^1$ under standard complexity assumptions, and it is widely believed to exclude problems like the word problem of $S_5$ or general context-free recognition. The route to this bound runs through [Merrill, Sabharwal & Smith's (2022)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.16213) earlier analysis of saturated transformers, where attention is replaced by its hardmax limit and the resulting computation is shown to be simulable by constant-depth threshold circuits. The saturated model is a faithful proxy for the asymptotic behaviour of softmax attention with bounded-norm queries and keys, and it is the technical lever that makes the $\mathsf{TC}^0$ upper bound stick.

[Hahn's (2020)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1906.06755) impossibility results sit underneath this picture as the original warning shot. He showed, by a soft-attention smoothing argument, that a fixed-depth, fixed-width self-attention stack cannot recognise $\mathrm{PARITY}$ (over $\{0,1\}^*$) or model $2\mathrm{DYCK}$ (the language of well-bracketed strings over two bracket types) — the cross-entropy of any such model on these languages is bounded away from the entropy of the true distribution by a constant that does not vanish as parameters grow, unless depth, width, or number of heads is allowed to scale with input length $n$. The moral is that periodic finite-state structure and unbounded hierarchical nesting are precisely the things a vanilla transformer cannot capture uniformly, and this is exactly the kind of structure that controls the dominant singularity of $L(z)$ for many natural distributions. A model that cannot represent $\bmod\ 2$ counting cannot put a pole at $z = -1$.

Against this backdrop, the two papers that bridge directly to T5 are [Svete & Cotterell (2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.05161) and [Rizvi, Lizaire, Lacroce & Rabusseau (2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.09728), and they should be read as a matched pair. Svete & Cotterell prove that simple Elman-style RNN language models are equivalent, as probability distributions over strings, to a specific subclass of probabilistic finite-state automata, and they sharpen the equivalence with a tight space lower bound: representing an arbitrary $N$-state deterministic PFSA over alphabet $\Sigma$ requires $\Omega(N|\Sigma|)$ hidden units. This identifies a swath of recurrent LMs with rational power series, so for that subclass $L(z)$ is a rational function and the entire Flajolet–Sedgewick toolkit applies verbatim. Rizvi et al. then carry the same story across the architectural gap: their Theorem 1 shows that transformers with bilinear layers and hard attention can *exactly* simulate any $n$-state WFA on inputs of length $T$ using depth $O(\log T)$, embedding dimension $O(n^2)$, and $O(1)$ heads; Theorem 2 gives the analogous approximate simulation for standard soft-attention transformers with MLPs, again at depth $O(\log T)$. The construction is a parallel-prefix scan of the WFA's transition matrices — exactly the computation $\mathsf{TC}^0$ admits — and the logarithmic depth is what one expects from a divide-and-conquer reduction. The paper extends the construction to weighted *tree* automata over balanced trees, also at depth $O(\log T)$. Together with [Yun, Bhojanapalli, Rawat, Reddi & Kumar's (2020)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1912.10077) universal approximation result — transformers with positional encodings are dense in continuous sequence-to-sequence functions on compact domains — this gives a quantitative sense in which transformers are "WFAs plus controlled error" at fixed length, with the approximation tightening as depth grows logarithmically.

The escape hatch is chain of thought. [Merrill & Sabharwal (2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.07923) show that allowing a transformer to emit and re-read intermediate tokens before committing to an answer expands its expressive power along a clean hierarchy: with a polynomial number of intermediate steps (and generalised pre-norm), the system recognises *exactly* $\mathsf{P}$, the class of polynomial-time decidable problems — the first exact characterisation of a transformer variant in terms of a standard complexity class, and a decisive break of the $\mathsf{TC}^0$ ceiling. From the T5 vantage point, CoT is the mechanism by which an LM can stop being (approximately) a WFA and start producing distributions whose generating functions need not be rational at all — algebraic, holonomic, or worse, with correspondingly richer singular behaviour and slower or non-uniform decay of $[z^n]L(z)$.

The synthesis for T5 is then the following. Without CoT, the distribution induced by a transformer LM is constrained to live near the WFA-approximable corner of the $\mathsf{TC}^0$ landscape; its generating function should be expected to be rational up to controlled error, with a dominant pole governing exponential length-decay and a finite spectrum of subdominant singularities controlling oscillatory corrections. With CoT, the same model can express distributions whose analytic structure escapes this regime entirely. Any empirical study of LLM output distributions through the lens of analytic combinatorics has to declare which of these two regimes it is operating in, because the asymptotic vocabulary changes accordingly.

## 4. Tightness and Language Model Foundations

*Threads: T1, T3*

Before any of the analytic-combinatorial machinery from Sections 2 and 3 can be brought to bear on a language model, one has to settle a question that is logically prior to every generating-function manipulation: does the model actually define a probability distribution over finite strings? The question sounds pedantic, but it is the hinge on which the entire program turns. An autoregressive language model factorizes as $p(w_1 \cdots w_n) = \prod_{t=1}^{n} p(w_t \mid w_{<t}) \cdot p(\text{EOS} \mid w_{\le n})$, and nothing in this definition forces $\sum_{w \in \Sigma^*} p(w) = 1$. Mass can leak. If the conditional probability of emitting EOS decays fast enough as a function of context length, then with positive probability the generative process never halts, and that missing mass sits on the set $\Sigma^{\omega}$ of infinite strings, invisible to any finite-string analysis.

[Du, Hennigen, Pimentel, Meister, Eisner and Cotterell (2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.10502) make this precise in measure-theoretic language. They construct the cylinder $\sigma$-algebra on $\Sigma^{\infty} = \Sigma^* \cup \Sigma^{\omega}$, push the autoregressive factorization forward to a probability measure $\mu$ on this space via Carathéodory extension, and define a language model to be *tight* exactly when $\mu(\Sigma^*) = 1$. Their central result (Theorem 4.7) gives an exact characterization: an autoregressive sequence model is tight if and only if $\sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \widetilde p_{\text{EOS}}(t) = \infty$, where $\widetilde p_{\text{EOS}}(t) = P(A_t \mid A_1^c \cap \cdots \cap A_{t-1}^c)$ is the EOS probability at step $t$ conditioned on having survived to that step. A more usable sufficient condition (Proposition 4.3) replaces this with a uniform lower bound across all prefixes: if there exists $f(t)$ with $p(\text{EOS} \mid x) \ge f(t)$ for every $x \in \Sigma^{t-1}$ and $\sum_t f(t) = \infty$, the model is tight. In particular, any model with a uniform floor $p(\text{EOS} \mid \cdot) \ge \varepsilon > 0$ is tight, and so is the softplus-RNN of their Example 2.5, in which $\widetilde p_{\text{EOS}}(t) = 1/(t+1)$ — the harmonic series, just barely divergent. They also exhibit the negative side of the dichotomy explicitly: Example 2.4 is a one-dimensional ReLU recurrence with $\widetilde p_{\text{EOS}}(t) = 1/(e^t + 1)$, an exponentially decaying EOS probability whose series converges and whose induced sequence model puts strictly positive mass on infinite strings. Critically, the paper proves the opposite for Transformers: §5.2 shows that every softmax-output Transformer language model is tight. There is no non-tight transformer construction. The companion course notes by [Cotterell, Svete, Meister, Liu and Du (2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.04329) embed the same definitions in the broader picture of language models as distributions over $\Sigma^*$ and trace the connection back through weighted finite-state automata.

The same condition was written down fifty years earlier, in a different dialect, by [Booth and Thompson (1973)](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/1672339). Working with probabilistic context-free grammars, they asked when a PCFG defines a genuine probability distribution over its (finite) derivation trees rather than losing mass to infinite derivations. Their *consistency* criterion — read in modern Galton–Watson / Perron–Frobenius language by Grenander and later by [Chi (1999)](https://aclanthology.org/J99-1004/) — is that the spectral radius of the expected first-moment (production) matrix of the associated multitype branching process be at most one (subcritical or critical). Equivalently, the probability generating function $F(z) = \sum_{n \ge 0} p_n z^n$ of derivation length satisfies $F(1) = 1$, and the underlying argument is Perron–Frobenius applied to the linearization of the grammar's system of generating-function equations at $z = 1$. Read in the modern light of Du et al., Booth–Thompson is the PCFG instance of the autoregressive tightness theorem: the same dichotomy between mass on finite objects and mass leaking to the infinite boundary, the same role for an expected-length / branching-rate quantity, the same equivalence with $F(1) = 1$.

For Thread T1 this equivalence is the entire reason tightness matters here at all. The analytic-combinatorial program of §§1–2 takes the length-distribution generating function $F(z) = \sum_n p_n z^n$ and reads off asymptotics of $p_n$ from the location and nature of its dominant singularity. If the model is not tight, then $F(1) = 1 - \delta$ for some defect $\delta > 0$, the unit circle is not the natural boundary of analysis, and the dominant singularity sits on or inside the unit disk in a way that reflects mass loss rather than the combinatorial structure one is trying to study. Singularity analysis in the Flajolet–Sedgewick sense presupposes a probability generating function normalized to $F(1) = 1$ with its dominant singularity at $\rho \ge 1$; tightness is exactly the hypothesis that delivers this. The Du et al. theorems are therefore not a side remark but a precondition: they certify that the object T1 wants to expand around $z = 1$ is the right object.

Thread T3, which recasts language model output distributions in Gibbs/Boltzmann form $p(w) = Z^{-1} e^{-\beta E(w)}$, depends on tightness for an even more elementary reason. The partition function $Z = \sum_{w \in \Sigma^*} e^{-\beta E(w)}$ is finite if and only if the underlying measure on $\Sigma^*$ is, and any thermodynamic quantity derived from $Z$ — free energy, entropy, susceptibilities — becomes ill-defined the moment mass escapes to $\Sigma^{\omega}$. Tightness is the statement that $Z < \infty$ before one even chooses a temperature. Booth–Thompson, Du et al., and the WFA-flavored exposition of Cotterell et al. thus converge on a single prerequisite that both downstream threads silently assume, and §4 exists to make that assumption explicit.

## 5. Entropy Rate and Information-Theoretic Connections

*Thread: T1*

The entropy rate of a stationary stochastic source is the quantity that the entire Shannon tradition has been chasing since 1951, and it is also — though the connection is rarely drawn — the same quantity that the analytic combinatorics tradition encodes as the location of a dominant singularity. Thread T1 turns on this identity. If $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ is a stationary process over a finite alphabet $\mathcal{A}$, its entropy rate is $h = \lim_{n\to\infty} \tfrac{1}{n} H(X_1^n)$, and by the Shannon–McMillan–Breiman theorem the typical sequence of length $n$ has probability $\approx 2^{-nh}$, so the typical set contains roughly $2^{nh}$ strings of length $n$. Packaging those counts into a generating function $A(z) = \sum_{n\ge 0} a_n z^n$, with $a_n \approx 2^{nh}$, gives a radius of convergence $R = 2^{-h}$, and therefore
$$h \;=\; \log_2(1/R).$$
The dominant singularity of this *counting-type* GF is, up to the choice of logarithm base, the entropy rate itself. One has to be careful to pick the right GF here: the length *probability* generating function $P(z) = \sum_n \Pr(|W| = n)\, z^n$ is a probability distribution with $P(1) = 1$, radius at least $1$, and therefore carries no information about $h$ through its radius of convergence — it encodes only the *shape of the length tail*. The entropy-rate–singularity identity lives on the counting side, not on the length-PGF side, and Gap 1/Gap 2 in the expanded gaps section of [`literature_review.md`](literature_review.md) spell out which object goes with which question. Everything else in T1 — the nature of the dominant singularity of the counting GF (pole, algebraic branch, logarithmic, essential), the subexponential corrections, the universality classes — is a refinement of what kind of object sits on the circle $|z| = 2^{-h}$.

Read in this light, the empirical entropy-rate literature for natural language is a sixty-year program of singularity localization conducted without the generating function being written down. [Shannon (1951)](https://archive.org/details/bstj30-1-50)'s guessing-game experiments on printed English produced bounds in the neighborhood of $0.6$–$1.3$ bits per character (his $N=100$ context column), the upper bound from the inverse of the guess-rank distribution and the lower bound from the entropy of that distribution. What Shannon was estimating, in T1 language, is $\log_2(1/R)$ for the generating function of English at the character level — though he had no reason to phrase it that way, and no reason to ask whether $R$ is approached as a pole or as a branch point. The classical nonparametric estimators of [Kontoyiannis, Algoet, Suhov and Wyner (1998)](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/669425) sharpen this picture without changing its nature: their longest-match-length estimator $\hat h_n = (\log_2 n)/L_n$, where $L_n$ is the length of the longest prefix of the future that has appeared in the past, is consistent for $h$ on stationary ergodic sources under a Doeblin-type mixing condition, and its convergence rate is governed by the mixing structure of the source — which is to say, by how cleanly $R$ is isolated from the rest of the spectrum of the transfer operator. A pole gives geometric mixing and fast convergence; a branch point or accumulation of singularities at $R$ gives polynomial mixing and the painfully slow convergence that is in fact observed.

The LLM-era papers inherit this geometry whether they acknowledge it or not. [Scheibner, Smith and Bialek (2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.24969) re-run a version of Shannon's guessing game with frontier LLMs as the predictor and find that the conditional entropy or code length continues to decrease with context length out to $N \sim 10^4$ characters, implying direct dependencies at those distances and an emergent certainty about an increasing fraction of characters as $N$ grows; the running estimate of $h$ continues to drift downward past any horizon Shannon could test. [Takahira, Tanaka-Ishii and Dębowski (2016)](https://www.mdpi.com/1099-4300/18/10/364) attack the same quantity from the compression side, fitting a stretched-exponential extrapolation to compressed-corpus entropy estimates across six languages and concluding that the asymptotic entropy rate is positive but roughly $20\%$ below naive estimates. [Takahashi and Tanaka-Ishii (2018)](https://www.mdpi.com/1099-4300/20/11/839), in *Entropy*, extrapolate neural LM cross-entropies in two directions at once — to infinite training data and to infinite context — and land at $\sim 1.12$ bits per character for English, lower than any previous published bound. All three are estimating the same $\log_2(1/R)$, and all three are bumping into the same wall: the convergence is slow, and the slowness is not an artifact of the estimator.

That slowness is the diagnostic that matters for T1. If the language generating function had a simple pole at $z = R$, the conditional entropy $H(X_{n+1} \mid X_1^n)$ would converge to $h$ geometrically and a few hundred characters of context would suffice; Shannon's 1951 estimates would have agreed with Takahashi and Tanaka-Ishii's 2018 estimates to three decimal places, and Scheibner et al. would not still be seeing the conditional entropy fall at $N=10^4$. They do not. The empirical pattern — long-range correlations, polynomial residuals, estimates that keep moving as context grows — is exactly what one expects when the dominant singularity is non-polar: an algebraic branch point of the form $(1 - z/R)^{\alpha}$ with $\alpha \notin \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$, or a logarithmic singularity, or a confluence of singularities on the circle $|z| = R$. In each of those cases the coefficient asymptotics carry a subexponential factor $n^{-\alpha-1}$ or $(\log n)^{-\beta}$ on top of $R^{-n}$, and the conditional entropy inherits a matching power-law residual. The information-theoretic literature has, in other words, been measuring the *exponent* $h = \log_2(1/R)$ for decades while leaving the *type* of the singularity untouched. T1's contribution is to point out that the type is the more interesting quantity, that the LLM entropy estimators give us, for the first time, enough resolution to constrain it, and that the residual $H(X_{n+1} \mid X_1^n) - h$ — the very thing every paper in this section reports as a nuisance — is the cleanest empirical handle on the analytic structure at $|z| = R$ that we currently possess.

## 6. Stochastic CFGs and Their Generating Functions

*Thread: T2*

Thread T2 concerns a single, sharp mathematical claim: that the length distributions of objects generated by a context-free mechanism carry a universal analytic fingerprint, and that this fingerprint can be read off from the singularity structure of an algebraic generating function. The foundational result is due to [Chomsky and Schützenberger (1963)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0049237X08720238), whose algebraic theory of context-free languages established that an unambiguous context-free grammar is, from the point of view of enumeration, a system of polynomial equations. Given an unambiguous CFG $G$ over a terminal alphabet, let $c_n$ denote the number of distinct words of length $n$ in $L(G)$, and form the ordinary generating function $F(z) = \sum_{n \geq 0} c_n z^n$. Chomsky and Schützenberger showed that $F(z)$ is *algebraic* over the field of rational functions $\mathbb{Q}(z)$: there exists a nonzero bivariate polynomial $P \in \mathbb{Q}[z, y]$ such that
$$P(z, F(z)) = 0.$$
The polynomial $P$ is obtained directly from the grammar by translating each production $A \to \alpha$ into an equation $A(z) = \prod_i \beta_i(z)$, where each terminal contributes a factor $z$ and each nonterminal contributes its own generating function; unambiguity guarantees that this commutative image counts each word exactly once. The system of nonterminal equations can then be eliminated to a single polynomial in $F(z)$ and $z$.

The analytic consequences of algebraicity are stringent, but it is important to be clear about provenance: the 1963 paper establishes algebraicity, not the asymptotic shape of $[z^n]F(z)$. The asymptotic content was supplied later, by the singularity analysis of Flajolet and Sedgewick. By Puiseux's theorem, the singularities of an algebraic function lie among the branch points of $P$, and generically — that is, for any irreducible, aperiodic, strongly connected grammar in which all nonterminals can mutually derive each other — the dominant singularity $\rho$ on the positive real axis is a *square-root branch point*. Locally,
$$F(z) = g(z) - h(z)\sqrt{1 - z/\rho},$$
with $g, h$ analytic at $\rho$ and $h(\rho) \neq 0$. Flajolet and Sedgewick's transfer theorems then give the asymptotic
$$[z^n] F(z) \sim \frac{h(\rho)}{2\sqrt{\pi}}\, n^{-3/2}\, \rho^{-n}.$$
Two features deserve emphasis. First, the exponential rate $\rho^{-n}$ is grammar-specific: $\rho$ is the smallest positive root of the resultant of $P$ and $\partial_y P$ and depends on the production weights or counts. Second, the polynomial correction $n^{-3/2}$ is *universal*: it is forced by the square-root branch point, which is in turn forced by the algebraic structure of $F(z)$ together with mild irreducibility conditions on the grammar. The exponent $-3/2$ is therefore not a phenomenological fit to data but a structural prediction of context-freeness itself; an empirical exponent away from $-3/2$ is, modulo the regularity hypotheses, evidence *against* a sentence-level CF generative mechanism. The $n^{-3/2}$ law is properly attributed to this analytic-combinatorics chain — Chomsky–Schützenberger algebraicity composed with Flajolet–Sedgewick singularity analysis — and not to the 1963 paper alone.

To apply this enumeration result to a *probabilistic* context-free grammar — to talk about distributions over derivations rather than counts of strings — one needs the measure-theoretic prerequisite that the PCFG actually defines a probability measure on finite trees. This is the contribution of [Chi (1999)](https://aclanthology.org/J99-1004/), who showed that PCFGs whose rule probabilities are obtained by his "relative weighted frequency" method — which specialises, on a finite treebank, to the maximum-likelihood estimator of Chi and Geman (1998) — are automatically *proper* (induced distributions assign total mass one to finite trees) and impose distributions under which the derivational entropy is finite and the random variables associated with parse-tree size have finite moments of all orders (Proposition 2). Chi's framework is that of stochastic branching processes rather than analytic combinatorics: the central technical object is the mean matrix $M$ of the corresponding multitype Galton–Watson process, and properness and moment finiteness are read off from the spectral radius of $M$ (subcriticality) rather than from singularity analysis of a GF. Generating functions in the sense of Harris (1963) do appear in Chi's proofs as an auxiliary device, but it would be a misrepresentation to say the proof "proceeds through generating functions" in the Flajolet–Sedgewick sense. The correct statement is that Chi's branching-process analysis supplies the measure-theoretic prerequisites that *let* the algebraic GF machinery be applied to a fitted PCFG. [Corazza and Satta (2006)](https://aclanthology.org/N06-1043/), "Cross-Entropy and Estimation of Probabilistic Context-Free Grammars," extend this perspective: their main result is that for a PCFG estimated by minimising the cross-entropy against an input tree distribution — and, as a corollary, for the standard maximum-likelihood estimator on finite tree banks — the derivational entropy of the fitted grammar coincides with the cross-entropy between the input distribution and the grammar. Their proof is via Lagrange multipliers and the linear system for outside probabilities, not via generating functions. Together, Chi and Corazza–Satta supply the bridge that lets one apply the Chomsky–Schützenberger machinery to a *fitted* PCFG: once properness and finite moments are established, the length distribution under the model is governed by an algebraic GF in exactly the sense of the 1963 theorem, and the universal $n^{-3/2}\rho^{-n}$ tail follows from Flajolet–Sedgewick singularity analysis.

It is essential to be precise about the scope of this prediction, and this is the crux of the retraction recorded elsewhere in this project: the $n^{-3/2}$ law applies to the length of strings (or, equivalently, the size of derivation trees) generated by a *sentence-level* PCFG fitted to sentences, and not to arbitrary notions of "response length" in a system whose generative process is not itself a sentence-level CFG. The remainder of T2 develops the consequences of this distinction.

## 7. Boltzmann Sampling

*Thread: T3*

This section underpins T3, which aims to formalize the relationship between temperature-scaled LLM sampling and the Boltzmann samplers invented by the analytic combinatorics community. Two mostly-disjoint literatures use the word "Boltzmann": one stemming from Duchon, Flajolet, Louchard and Schaeffer's work on random generation of combinatorial structures, and one stemming from the statistical-mechanics / machine-learning tradition most recently revived by Noé et al.'s "Boltzmann generators." They do not cite each other. Part of T3's contribution is to make the bridge explicit.

**What a Boltzmann sampler is.** Given a combinatorial class $\mathcal{C}$ with counting sequence $(c_n)_{n \geq 0}$ and ordinary generating function $A(z) = \sum_{n \geq 0} c_n z^n$, pick a real tuning parameter $x \in (0, R)$ inside the disk of convergence. The Boltzmann sampler of parameter $x$, denoted $\Gamma A(x)$, draws an object $\gamma \in \mathcal{C}$ with probability
$$\mathbb{P}_x(\gamma) \;=\; \frac{x^{|\gamma|}}{A(x)},$$
where $|\gamma|$ is the size of $\gamma$. Equivalently, the *size* of the output is a random variable $N$ with $\mathbb{P}_x(N = n) = c_n x^n / A(x)$, and conditioned on $N = n$ the object is uniform among the $c_n$ structures of that size. Two facts give the scheme its power. First, $x$ acts as a dial on the expected size: $\mathbb{E}_x[N] = x A'(x)/A(x)$ (Proposition 2.1 of the 2004 paper). Second, because the distribution factors multiplicatively, Boltzmann samplers for sums, products, sequences, sets and cycles can be composed recursively from the symbolic specification of the class, so essentially any class describable in the Flajolet–Sedgewick framework admits a linear-time random generator once the relevant GF values are precomputed.

[Duchon, Flajolet, Louchard and Schaeffer (2004)](https://algo.inria.fr/flajolet/Publications/DuFlLoSc04.pdf) is the foundational paper of the area (*Combinatorics, Probability and Computing* 13:577–625). It introduces the Boltzmann model above in two flavours — ordinary models for unlabelled classes (driven by the OGF) in §3, and exponential models for labelled classes (driven by the EGF) in §4 — and gives explicit samplers for each of the constructions of the symbolic method (union, product, SEQ, SET, CYC, MSET). It shows that rejection on $N = n$ recovers an exact-size uniform sampler at only polynomial overhead, and proves that for nice (e.g., algebraic) classes the scheme runs in linear expected time once a small number of real constants — the "oracle" values of $A(x)$ and its derivatives — are available. The tuning parameter $x$ is interpreted as a dial on target size: given a desired $n$, one chooses $x = x_n$ so that $\mathbb{E}_{x_n}[N] = n$, which for classes with square-root or logarithmic singularity types yields optimal "singular" samplers (§§6–7). Crucially for T3, the paper itself (not a later survey) makes the statistical-mechanics translation explicit. In the "Relation to other fields" subsection of §2, the authors note that if one identifies the size $|\gamma|$ of a combinatorial object with an energy $E(\gamma)$ and sets $x = e^{-\beta}$, the Boltzmann-sampler distribution becomes
$$\mathbb{P}_\beta(\gamma) \;=\; \frac{e^{-\beta E(\gamma)}}{A(e^{-\beta})} \;=\; \frac{e^{-\beta E(\gamma)}}{Z(\beta)},$$
i.e., the Gibbs–Boltzmann distribution with partition function $Z(\beta) = A(e^{-\beta})$ — what Duchon et al., following the physics convention, call the *Zustandsumme*. The generating function *is* the partition function; the radius of convergence corresponds to a phase transition; and in their sign convention $x \to 0$ corresponds to "freezing" while $x \to \rho^-$ corresponds to "melting" and the production of arbitrarily large objects. The paper's conceptual move — replacing exact-size uniform generation by size-biased generation with a tunable intensity — is the move that makes energy-weighted sampling tractable, and it is the move a temperature-scaled LLM performs implicitly. This translation is present in the analytic-combinatorics literature but largely unknown to machine learning, and it is the hinge on which T3 turns.

[Noé, Olsson, Köhler and Wu (2019)](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aaw1147) ("Boltzmann generators," *Science* 365, eaaw1147; preprint [arXiv:1812.01729](https://arxiv.org/abs/1812.01729)) approach the same sampling problem from the opposite end. The target is a physical Gibbs distribution $p(x) \propto e^{-u(x)/kT}$ over molecular configurations, which is not defined by a combinatorial recursion but by an intractable continuous potential. Instead of building the sampler out of the structure of the target, Noé et al. train an invertible neural network — a normalizing flow built from stacked RealNVP-style non-volume-preserving blocks — that pushes a simple latent Gaussian onto the equilibrium distribution; importance reweighting and an energy loss correct residual bias. The result is a sampler that draws independent, rare conformational states of small proteins and spin systems, something MD and MCMC cannot do in any reasonable time. For this review the paper matters for two reasons. First, it shows that the Gibbs–Boltzmann objective is tractable by learned, amortized samplers — the same computational posture as an LLM, which is an amortized sampler over strings. Second, and crucially for the historical story: Noé et al. do not cite Duchon et al. (verified by inspection of the arXiv preprint's bibliography), and Duchon et al. have nothing to say about neural networks. The two "Boltzmann sampling" literatures developed in parallel.

**The T3 hook.** An autoregressive LLM with base distribution $\mu$ and temperature $T$ generates a string $w$ with probability proportional to $\mu(w)^{1/T} = \exp(\log \mu(w) / T)$. Defining the energy $E(w) := -\log \mu(w)$, this rewrites as
$$\mathbb{P}_T(w) \;\propto\; e^{-E(w)/T},$$
which is structurally a Gibbs–Boltzmann distribution with inverse temperature $\beta = 1/T$ and energy $E$. Matching this against the Duchon et al. form $\mathbb{P}_x(\gamma) \propto x^{|\gamma|}/A(x)$ makes the identification $x = e^{-1/T}$ natural — but only *if* the LLM energy $E(w)$ can be written as a (possibly weighted) size on some combinatorial class whose generating function plays the role of $A(x)$. This is the central open question of T3: is temperature-scaled LLM sampling a Boltzmann sampler in the Duchon–Flajolet sense, and if so, for what combinatorial class $\mathcal{C}$ and what size function $|\cdot|$? The candidate classes — weighted strings, parse trees of an extracted PCFG, paths in an extracted WFA (§§2, 6, 11) — are exactly the objects studied elsewhere in this review, which is why T3 sits at the confluence of the other threads rather than as a standalone project. Bridging the Flajolet–Sedgewick and Noé-style literatures is itself a contribution: the analytic-combinatorics side supplies exact enumeration and singularity machinery, the ML side supplies amortized learned samplers, and LLMs sit uncomfortably between them.

## 8. Zipf's Law and Token Distributions

*Thread: T4*

Few empirical regularities in language are as famous, or as theoretically contested, as Zipf's law: if word types are ranked by frequency, the frequency of the $r$-th most common word decays roughly as $f(r) \propto r^{-s}$ with $s \approx 1$. The law has been rediscovered in contexts as varied as city sizes, citation counts, and — relevant to thread T4 — the output distributions of large language models. For a project that proposes to apply analytic combinatorics to LLM outputs, Zipf's law is the natural proving ground: it is a limit law over a discrete, rank-indexed object, and it cries out for the bivariate-generating-function machinery that Flajolet and Sedgewick developed precisely for such situations. Yet the existing literature, rich as it is, has not made that connection explicit.

[Piantadosi (2014)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.3758/s13423-014-0585-6)'s critical review is the appropriate starting point because it dismantles the comfortable story. Close inspection of large corpora reveals that a single power law is a poor fit: the tail breaks, there are at least two regimes (a shallow head and a steeper tail), and the exponent drifts with corpus size and tokenization choices. Piantadosi then surveys the competing theoretical explanations — information-theoretic optimization arguments (Mandelbrot, Ferrer i Cancho), Simon-style preferential-attachment models, random-typing / monkey-at-a-typewriter derivations, and mixture-of-distributions accounts — and concludes that none of them is fully satisfactory. Each predicts a clean power law where the data show curvature, and most are consistent with the gross shape of the distribution while saying little about the exponent itself. The review thus leaves a clear gap: we need derivations that predict not just "power-law-like," but the specific functional form, with a principled handle on the exponent.

[Goldwater, Griffiths and Johnson (2011)](https://www.jmlr.org/papers/v12/goldwater11a.html) offer one of the most technically disciplined responses. They argue that Zipfian behavior is naturally produced by a "two-stage" language model: a simple generator (even a unigram or HMM) is wrapped in a Pitman–Yor process adaptor, which reuses previously generated types with probability governed by a discount parameter $a$ and concentration $b$. The resulting type-frequency distribution is power-law, with the exponent controlled by the discount parameter (the standard Pitman–Yor result is a rank-frequency exponent of $1+a$ in the regime Goldwater et al. consider). The key conceptual move is to separate the generation of novel types from the caching of old ones — a structural decomposition strikingly reminiscent of the "sequence of cycles" or "set of components" constructions in symbolic combinatorics. It hints that Zipf is less about linguistics than about a certain algebraic shape of process.

[Berman (2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.17575)'s random-text analysis pushes that intuition further in exactly the direction T4 needs. Working with a deliberately non-linguistic model — independent characters drawn from a finite alphabet augmented with a space symbol, "words" defined as maximal non-space blocks — Berman derives closed-form expressions for word-length distributions, vocabulary growth, and a critical length $k^*$ above which types almost never repeat. Most importantly, he obtains a rank-frequency law $p(r) \propto r^{-\alpha}$ in which $\alpha$ is given explicitly in terms of the alphabet size and the space probability. The combinatorial mechanism is transparent: strings of length $k$ grow exponentially in $k$ while their probabilities decay exponentially, and the ratio of the two rates fixes the exponent. This is essentially a coupon-collector / generating-function calculation performed by hand, and it is the closest the existing literature comes to a purely combinatorial derivation of Zipf. It serves as both an inspiration and a target: T4 can be stated as "recover Berman's formula as a special case of a general singularity-analysis theorem, and extend it to richer grammars."

[Mikhaylovskiy (2025)](https://aclanthology.org/2025.findings-emnlp.837/) anchors the discussion empirically at the token level, which is the level LLMs actually operate on. He measures Zipf's and Heaps' laws on tokenized corpora produced by LLMs and finds that both laws hold for LLM-generated text only within a narrow, model-dependent sampling-temperature window — typically near $t \approx 1$ for base models, somewhat higher for instruction-tuned models, and largely independent of model size or prompt. Outside that window, the Zipf/Heaps fits break down, which the paper interprets as independent confirmation of recent reports of temperature-driven phase transitions in LLM output statistics. Two lessons follow for T4. First, "LLM text obeys Zipf" is a statement about a sampling regime, not a universal property: any theorem about LLM token statistics needs an explicit decoding-temperature parameter, and should predict where the law breaks. Second, the tokenizer plus the temperature together define the combinatorial object whose generating function we analyze — they are part of the data of the problem, not preprocessing to be ignored.

[Michaud et al. (2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.13506) ("The Quantization Model of Neural Scaling," NeurIPS 2023) sit somewhat apart but connect Zipf to the neural scaling laws literature. They posit that models learn discrete task "quanta" whose use frequencies in the data are themselves Zipf-distributed, and that quanta are acquired in frequency order as compute scales; the exponent of the Zipf distribution over quanta then determines the exponent of the loss-vs-compute scaling law. Their evidence comes from toy "multitask sparse parity" datasets together with an empirical decomposition of large language models via gradient clustering, in which they tentatively recover a Zipfian frequency distribution over inferred quanta whose exponent is consistent with the measured LLM scaling exponent. The proposal is speculative, but it is the only paper in this cluster that explicitly ties a Zipfian distribution over a latent combinatorial object to a macroscopic performance law for LLMs — exactly the style of argument T4 aims to formalize. (A separate 2024 paper, [Neumann & Gros (arXiv:2412.11979)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.11979), extends a quanta-style scaling story to AlphaZero board-game agents; the AlphaZero connection belongs to that follow-up, not to Michaud et al.)

Taken together, these papers define an open slot. Piantadosi tells us that the phenomenon is real but multi-regime; Goldwater et al. show that a two-stage generative architecture can reproduce it; Berman shows that raw combinatorics can, in a restricted setting, predict the exponent exactly; Mikhaylovskiy shows that the phenomenon carries over to LLM-generated tokens only inside a narrow temperature window; and Michaud et al. suggest that Zipfian latent distributions drive scaling. What no one has done is apply the Flajolet–Sedgewick limit-law machinery: treating the token as a "marked" atom in a bivariate generating function $F(z, u)$, and extracting the rank-frequency law from the behavior of the singularity $\rho(u)$ as $u$ varies. Because smooth movable singularities generically produce power-law marginals, this approach would yield the exponent as an analytic quantity derived from the grammar and the tokenizer, rather than fitted after the fact. It would also give a principled account of the multi-regime structure Piantadosi emphasizes, and of the temperature-window phenomenon Mikhaylovskiy reports: different singularities dominate in different parts of the rank axis and at different effective temperatures. T4's contribution is to carry out this derivation, recover Berman's formula as a corollary, and make explicit the distinction — forced by the token-level evidence — between the combinatorics of words and the combinatorics of subword tokens.

## 9. Energy-Based Models and Statistical Mechanics of LLMs

*Thread: T3*

A growing body of work treats large language models not as black-box function approximators but as statistical-mechanics objects, with probability distributions, partition functions, temperatures, and phase transitions in the literal physical sense. This perspective is the natural home for thread T3 of this review, because the Boltzmann-temperature correspondence we wish to formalize is already implicit (and sometimes explicit) in these papers. What is missing — and what T3 contributes — is the analytic-combinatorics half of the picture: the reinterpretation of partition functions as generating functions and of critical temperatures as singularities.

The starting point is trivial to state but consequential. For any autoregressive language model over strings $w$, one can define an energy $E(w) = -\log \mu(w)$ and recover the Gibbs form
$$
\mu(w) \;=\; \frac{\exp(-E(w))}{Z}, \qquad Z \;=\; \sum_w \exp(-E(w)).
$$
Temperature-scaled decoding — dividing logits by $T$ before softmaxing — then corresponds to sampling from
$$
\mu_T(w) \;\propto\; \exp\bigl(-E(w)/T\bigr),
$$
the Gibbs measure at inverse temperature $\beta = 1/T$. Every notion from equilibrium statistical mechanics (free energy $F = -T \log Z$, entropy, susceptibility, criticality) is in principle available. Whether these quantities are informative for real LLMs is the empirical question the papers in this section try to answer.

[Blondel et al. (2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.15605) give the cleanest theoretical articulation of the ARM–EBM correspondence. Starting from the chain rule of probability, they construct an explicit bijection between autoregressive models and energy-based models in function space (their Proposition 1), and show that this bijection can be viewed as an instance of the soft Bellman equation from maximum-entropy reinforcement learning. The practical payoff of their paper is a unification of supervised ARM training with EBM training, plus an interpretation of MaxEnt RL as distilling an EBM into an ARM. For our purposes, though, the conceptual payoff is more important: they make the partition function of an LLM an explicit, manipulable object rather than a metaphor. Once $Z$ is on the table, so is the machinery built around it — including, in principle, singularity analysis of $Z$ viewed as a generating function.

[Kempton and Burrell (2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.21929) push in a complementary direction by importing thermodynamic formalism from ergodic theory. They show that top-$k$, nucleus, and temperature sampling are all equilibrium states for different variational objectives (different combinations of entropy and expected log-probability), placing heuristic decoding strategies inside a single principled framework. Their most striking technical contribution is the identification of *local normalization distortion* — explicitly named in their abstract as "a fundamental defect of decoding strategies": because autoregressive models renormalize at every token, the resulting distribution over full strings is not the Gibbs measure of any simple energy, and the mismatch accumulates with sequence length. This distortion has no analogue in true equilibrium statistical mechanics, and Kempton and Burrell quantify its effect on diversity and quality proxies. Critically for T3, they never use generating functions, yet their equilibrium-state formulation is entirely compatible with singularity analysis — equilibrium states of subshifts of finite type are classical territory for the analytic-combinatorics toolkit, and the bridge is waiting to be crossed.

[Arnold, Holtorf, Schäfer and Lörch (2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.17088) and the closely related [Nakaishi, Nishikawa and Hukushima (2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.05335) supply the empirical evidence that LLMs actually behave like physical systems near criticality. Arnold et al. adapt system-agnostic statistical-distance methods from the physics of automated phase-transition detection and scan the temperature parameter of Pythia (multiple sizes), Mistral-7B (base and instruct), and Llama-3-8B (base and ChatQA-tuned), finding sharp transitions in the output statistics as temperature is varied. Nakaishi et al. focus on GPT-2 small (with Japanese GPT-2 and Pythia-70M in appendices) and find a transition between a low-$T$ regime of repetitive, crystalline output and a high-$T$ regime of incoherent noise. Near the critical temperature, statistical quantities diverge, correlations decay as power laws, and convergence to the stationary state slows down — the same phenomenology one sees at a genuine second-order phase transition. Energy itself obeys scaling laws near $T_c$. This is the empirical backbone for any claim that the Gibbs picture of LLMs is more than a formal analogy.

[Lin, Tegmark and Rolnick (2017)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1608.08225) predate the LLM literature but belong here because they supply the structural reason the energy landscape of a trained network is tractable at all. They argue that deep networks succeed not because of generic universal approximation but because real-world data-generating processes inherit from physics a small set of properties, listed in their abstract: "symmetry, locality, compositionality, and polynomial log-probability." A distribution whose log-density is a low-degree polynomial has a partition function whose analytic structure is close to rational or algebraic. That is precisely the class of generating functions whose singularity analysis is classical in the Flajolet–Sedgewick sense. Lin et al. do not draw this connection, but it is visible in their equations if one reads them with a combinatorialist's eye.

Taken together, these papers establish that the statistical-mechanics reading of LLMs is neither idle metaphor nor isolated speculation: it has a rigorous functional-analytic basis (Blondel et al.), a principled decoding-theoretic formalism (Kempton and Burrell), direct empirical support at the level of phase diagrams (Arnold et al.; Nakaishi et al.), and a structural justification from the data side (Lin, Tegmark, and Rolnick). Yet none of them uses generating functions, singularity analysis, or any other tool from analytic combinatorics. This is a cultural gap, not a mathematical one. Partition functions and generating functions are the same object written in different notations; free energies and logarithms of dominant singularities are the same quantity; critical temperatures and radii of convergence obey the same equations. The stat-mech community writes $Z(\beta)$ and looks for non-analyticities in $\beta$; the analytic-combinatorics community writes $F(z)$ and looks for singularities in $z$. Thread T3 of this project is the explicit translation between these two dialects. The phase transitions Arnold et al. measure in Llama and Mistral should, under the correspondence, appear as singularities of a generating function derived from the model's transition structure, with $T_c$ determined by the radius of convergence. Kempton and Burrell's equilibrium states of top-$k$ and nucleus decoding should sit inside the symbolic-method framework as constrained classes. Blondel et al.'s explicit bijection makes the partition function concrete enough to be differentiated, expanded, and analyzed as a complex-analytic object. The bridge is open, it is explicit, and — as far as we have been able to determine — no one has yet walked across it.

## 10. Analytic Combinatorics in Information Theory

*Thread: T1*

This section exists because it is the part of the literature where the program this project proposes — applying the Flajolet–Sedgewick toolkit to objects defined by an information source — has *already* been carried out, repeatedly and successfully, for sources that are simpler than an LLM but structurally of the same kind. Everything in §1 gave us the abstract machinery (symbolic method, singularity analysis, multivariate GFs); this section shows what that machinery looks like when the "combinatorial class" is a random string drawn from a probabilistic source and the quantities of interest are information-theoretic: codeword lengths, pattern counts, path lengths in a trie, redundancy of a compression scheme. For T1, whose goal is to estimate and analyze the length generating function of an LLM's output distribution, these works are the clearest existing templates we can follow.

[Szpankowski (2001)](https://www.wiley.com/en-fr/Average+Case+Analysis+of+Algorithms+on+Sequences-p-9780471240631)'s *Average Case Analysis of Algorithms on Sequences* (Wiley) is the canonical reference. Its subject is the precise-asymptotic behavior of algorithms whose input is a string drawn from a probabilistic source: digital tries, PATRICIA tries, suffix trees, digital search trees, Lempel–Ziv parsings, and several compression schemes. The methodological core is the full Flajolet–Sedgewick toolkit — bivariate generating functions, Mellin transforms to handle the sums-over-levels that arise in digital structures, Poissonization and depoissonization to decouple the randomness in the string population from the randomness in the string contents, and singularity analysis and saddle-point methods to extract asymptotics. The book is organized by *technique*, not by source model: Part I introduces the algorithms and the probabilistic models (Chapter 2 introduces memoryless, Markov, and mixing sources together), Part II develops probabilistic methods, and Part III develops analytic methods. Within individual chapters, however, the same problem is often analyzed first under a memoryless source and then lifted to a Markov source — Chapter 10 (depoissonization) treats the depth in generalized digital search trees "for memoryless and Markovian sources" as its showcase application. For T1 the relevant fact is therefore a working pattern: pick a quantity, compute its generating function under the simplest source, use singularity analysis to extract asymptotics, then re-derive under a Markov source and check that the singularity structure survives. A memoryless source is a unigram LM; a Markov source is an $n$-gram LM; an autoregressive neural LM is, formally, a Markov source of unbounded order whose state space is the entire prefix. If one can write down (or numerically estimate) the generating function $L(z)=\sum_{n\ge 0} P(\text{length}=n)\,z^n$ of an LLM's output, the techniques Szpankowski develops — in particular the connection between the location and nature of the dominant singularity of $L(z)$ and the tail behavior of the length distribution — apply essentially unchanged. Our project is, in a concrete sense, "do Szpankowski for LLMs."

[Jacquet and Szpankowski (1991)](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/133271)'s *IEEE Transactions on Information Theory* paper "Analysis of Digital Tries with Markovian Dependency" (vol. 37, no. 5, pp. 1470–1475) is the cleanest demonstration that the Markov jump is not fatal. They give a complete characterization of a trie built from $n$ strings generated by a Markov source from the depth viewpoint, showing $\mathbb{E} D_n \sim h_1^{-1} \log n + c'$ and $\mathrm{Var}\,D_n \sim \alpha \log n + c''$, where $h_1$ is the entropy rate of the Markov source and $\alpha$ is a second-order parameter of the source. The mechanism is that under a Markov model the relevant generating functions are still rational or meromorphic in a neighborhood of the unit disk — they pick up a matrix factor coming from the transition kernel, but the singularity analysis goes through. This matters for T1 because an autoregressive LM *is* (structurally) a Markov source, just with a state space large enough that the transition "matrix" is implicit in the network's weights. The Jacquet–Szpankowski result says: provided the source is sufficiently mixing, the analytic-combinatorics program survives the move from Bernoulli to Markov. The open question for LLMs — and it is the central methodological risk of T1 — is whether the long-range, high-dimensional dependencies induced by attention over a multi-thousand-token context behave, in the aggregate, like a well-mixing Markov source (in which case Jacquet–Szpankowski-style theorems should go through after averaging) or whether they preserve enough non-stationarity that the dominant singularity of $L(z)$ is not of the regular square-root or algebraic-logarithmic type the transfer theorems require.

[Nicodème, Salvy and Flajolet (2002)](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0304-3975(01)00264-X)'s "Motif Statistics" paper (*Theoretical Computer Science* 287(2):593–617) is the piece most directly adjacent to T4 (and it is where T1 and T4 couple). For patterns specified by a regular expression and text generated by a memoryless or Markov source, they give a complete analysis of the number of occurrences: they construct, via classical automata-theoretic constructions, a bivariate generating function in which one variable marks text length and the other marks occurrences, and they extract precise asymptotic formulae (means, variances, higher moments) for the number of occurrences in texts of arbitrarily large size. The construction is the De Bruijn / Goulden–Jackson correlation apparatus lifted onto a finite-state source. This is exactly the template T4 is asking for: replace "Markov source" with "LLM" and ask whether the same bivariate-GF program yields limit laws for motif counts in generated text, and in particular whether the $n$-gram repetition pathologies observed empirically in neural LMs can be read off the singularity structure of the analogous bivariate GF.

Taken together, the message of this section for T1 is concrete. If we can estimate $L(z)$ — even numerically, from samples, via the Boltzmann-sampler ideas in §7 — then Szpankowski's depoissonization, Jacquet–Szpankowski's Markov-source transfer theorems, and Nicodème–Salvy–Flajolet's bivariate-GF construction give us a ready-made pipeline for turning that estimate into statements about length tails, motif frequencies, and entropy-rate corrections. Section 1 is the foundational framework; this section is where that framework has already been put to work on sequences. T1's job is to extend the same machinery one more rung up the source-complexity ladder, to neural autoregressive LMs.

## 11. WFA Approximation of Neural Language Models

*Thread: T5*

A weighted finite automaton (WFA) with initial vector $\alpha$, transition matrix $A$, and final vector $\beta$ assigns to each length-$k$ input the scalar $f(k) = \alpha^\top A^k \beta$. The ordinary generating function of this sequence is the *rational* function
$$F(z) \;=\; \sum_{k \geq 0} f(k)\, z^k \;=\; \alpha^\top (I - zA)^{-1} \beta,$$
whose poles are the reciprocals of the eigenvalues of $A$. This is not a coincidence of notation; it is the entire reason WFAs matter for this project. Once an object is a WFA, its generating function is rational, and the full Flajolet–Sedgewick apparatus for rational GFs — partial-fraction decomposition, dominant-pole asymptotics, transfer theorems for coefficient extraction — applies directly. The asymptotic behaviour of $f(k)$ is controlled by the pole of $F$ of smallest modulus, and $[z^k] F(z) \sim C \rho^{-k} k^{m-1}$ for a dominant pole at $z = \rho$ of multiplicity $m$. Rational GFs are the one setting in which analytic combinatorics is almost trivially tight.

The relevance to threads T1–T5 is that LLMs are, on their face, the opposite of WFAs: they are high-dimensional, nonlinear, and their "state" at generation step $k$ is a key-value cache whose dimension grows with $k$. Their generating functions are not, in any literal sense, rational. The WFA-approximation literature is the bridge that makes T5 tractable anyway: if an LLM can be *approximated* by a WFA — in KL, in spectral norm, or in behaviour — then to the extent that approximation is faithful, the machinery of rational generating functions transfers. This section surveys the existing bridges.

**Black-box distillation.** The earliest line is pragmatic. [Lecorvé and Motlicek (2012)](https://publications.idiap.ch/downloads/papers/2012/Lecorve_INTERSPEECH-2_2012.pdf), working on Mikolov-style RNN language models for ASR rescoring, proposed to discretise the RNN's continuous hidden-state space by clustering on hidden-state representations, identify clusters with WFST states, and read off transition probabilities by querying the RNN at cluster centroids; the paper also applies entropy-based pruning. The result is a finite-state transducer that can be composed with pronunciation and acoustic WFSTs in the standard ASR pipeline, but it is heuristic and comes with no optimality guarantee. [Suresh, Roark, Riley and Schogol (2021, *Computational Linguistics* 47(2):221–254)](https://aclanthology.org/2021.cl-2.9/) gave the principled version: given an arbitrary source distribution $p$ (e.g., a neural LM), find the WFA $q$ of bounded size that minimises $\mathrm{KL}(p \,\|\, q)$. They factor this into a counting step and a difference-of-convex optimisation step, and apply it to distil $n$-gram WFAs from neural LMs, build compact LMs for constrained deployment, and construct open-vocabulary character models. The KL objective is the right one from an information-theoretic standpoint and is the most directly relevant baseline when what one wants is a WFA that matches an LLM's *distribution*, not just its arg-max.

**Architectural equivalence.** A second, subtler line argues that certain neural architectures are *already* WFAs up to reparameterisation, so no distillation is needed. [Schwartz, Thomson and Smith (2018)](https://aclanthology.org/P18-1028/) introduced SoPa, a soft-pattern model whose forward pass is the forward algorithm on a WFSA with neural transition weights, and showed that one-layer CNNs are equivalent to a restricted form of SoPa (and hence a restricted form of WFSA). The connection to *rational power series* in the sense of Schützenberger is foregrounded in the follow-up [Peng, Schwartz, Thomson and Smith (2018), "Rational Recurrences" (EMNLP 2018)](https://aclanthology.org/D18-1152/), which defines rational recurrences as hidden-state updates expressible as the forward calculation of a finite set of WFSAs and shows that several recent RNN variants (SRU, QRNN cells, etc.) fall in this class. These networks compute rational power series, and the Hadamard–Schützenberger closure properties for rational series are exactly the closure properties of rational GFs. The rational-recurrences paper gives a static, compile-time criterion for architectures that are WFAs by construction rather than by approximation.

**L\*-style structural extraction.** A third line is due to [Weiss, Goldberg and Yahav (ICML 2018)](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v80/weiss18a.html), who adapted Angluin's L\* algorithm to extract a *deterministic* finite automaton from a trained RNN by using the RNN as a membership/equivalence oracle and using abstraction-refinement on the hidden-state space to manufacture counterexamples. The extracted DFA is structural, not probabilistic: it captures the RNN's decision boundary but not its distribution over continuations. For T5 this is less directly useful — rational GFs with Boolean-semiring weights are regular-language indicators, not probability generating functions — but it is the sharpest existence proof that the behaviour of small recurrent nets is often genuinely finite-state modulo a tolerance.

**Operator-theoretic optimal compression.** The theoretically deepest line is the Lacroce program. [Lacroce, Panangaden and Rabusseau (ICGI 2021)](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v153/lacroce21a.html) reframe LM approximate minimisation as *Hankel-operator* approximation: every rational series has an associated Hankel matrix $H$ whose finite rank equals the number of states of a minimal WFA realising the series, and approximating the WFA in spectral norm is exactly approximating $H$ by a lower-rank Hankel operator. The Adamyan–Arov–Krein (AAK) theorem from the 1970s gives the optimal such approximation for Hankel operators on Hardy space $H^2$, and the paper applies it to extract WFAs that mimic the behaviour of black-box LMs within a given size constraint. The full multi-letter spectral-norm theory is developed in [Balle, Lacroce, Panangaden, Precup and Rabusseau (arXiv:2102.06860)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2102.06860), "Optimal Spectral-Norm Approximate Minimization of Weighted Finite Automata," which gives an algorithm returning the optimal spectral-norm approximation via Hankel/AAK machinery. The companion journal paper [Lacroce, Balle, Panangaden and Rabusseau (*MSCS* 34(8):807–833, 2024)](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/mathematical-structures-in-computer-science/article/optimal-approximate-minimization-of-oneletter-weighted-finite-automata/9733CA9F3079F9186103F8515DE86D96) sharpens the picture at the one-letter (unary) boundary: for irredundant real-weighted WFAs $\mathcal{A} = \langle \alpha, A, \beta \rangle$ over a one-letter alphabet, they give a closed-form polynomial-time algorithm that returns the optimal spectral-norm rank-$k$ approximation. The argument runs through the rational function $\phi(z) = \alpha^\top (zI - A)^{-1}\beta$, which is exactly our generating function up to the substitution $z \leftrightarrow 1/z$, and whose poles are the reciprocals of the eigenvalues of $A$ — i.e., exactly the quantities that govern Flajolet–Sedgewick coefficient asymptotics.

Two observations about the Lacroce line are central to T5. First, it uses pole structure *operationally* — the AAK construction depends on locating poles of $\phi$ inside and outside the unit disc, block-diagonalising $A$ accordingly, and performing a Hankel-norm correction — but it never extracts asymptotic coefficient information. No $[z^k]$, no singularity analysis, no transfer theorems. The bibliography tells the story: Adamyan–Arov–Krein, Glover on $H^\infty$ control, Antoulas on large-scale dynamical systems, Nikol'skii's *Operators, Functions, and Systems*, and Peller's *Hankel Operators and Their Applications*. It is entirely operator theory and $H^\infty$ model reduction, with zero citations to Flajolet–Sedgewick or to any analytic-combinatorics text. Two communities are working on literally the same mathematical object — rational generating functions whose poles lie in prescribed regions of the complex plane — with non-overlapping toolkits. Building the bridge is an open opportunity for T5, and it is not merely notational: AAK gives tight *control over which poles survive compression*, which is precisely the information an analytic-combinatoricist wants as input to dominant-pole asymptotics.

Second, the one-letter restriction in Lacroce et al. (2024) looks fatal for LLM applications and is not. A one-letter WFA is just a scalar sequence $f(k) = \alpha^\top A^k \beta$, and any projection of an LLM's output to a scalar-valued functional of position — generation length, counts of a marker token, structural depth of a parsed prefix, any Boolean indicator composed with a running sum — lives in one-letter territory regardless of the underlying vocabulary size. The multi-letter spectral-norm theory is treated in [Balle et al. (arXiv:2102.06860)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2102.06860), but the one-letter theorem already applies directly to *projected* LLM distributions, which is exactly the regime in which analytic combinatorics is most natural: we almost always care about the distribution of some scalar statistic of an LLM's output, not the joint distribution over the full $|\mathcal{V}|^k$ simplex. What looks like a crippling restriction is, for T5, the correct design choice.

**Scale realities.** The honest caveat is that no existing WFA extraction has been run against a modern LLM. Lecorvé–Motlicek distilled a small Mikolov RNN-LM. Suresh et al.'s largest experiments target $n$-gram-scale targets. Schwartz–Thomson–Smith tested on text classification. Weiss–Goldberg–Yahav extracted automata with tens to hundreds of states from small character-level RNNs. The Lacroce experiments are on synthetic WFAs and small RNN LMs. Modern LLMs have $d \in [4\text{k}, 16\text{k}]$ hidden dimensions, $|\mathcal{V}| \gtrsim 32\text{k}$, and context lengths in the hundreds of thousands; the Hankel matrices of their induced projected distributions are numerically low-rank in practice but no one has demonstrated extraction at that scale. Every method in this section gives correctness guarantees that hold *if* extraction succeeds, and each has implicit $O(d^2)$ or worse dependencies that do not obviously survive the jump. This is the empirical frontier.

**The forty-year arc.** Taken together the literature traces a long arc. Lecorvé–Motlicek (2012) opens the RNN-to-WFST line as an ASR engineering problem. Schwartz–Thomson–Smith (2018) and the Peng et al. (2018) rational-recurrences line give architectural equivalence: some neural models *are* WFAs. Weiss–Goldberg–Yahav (2018) gives structural (non-probabilistic) extraction via L\*. Suresh et al. (2021) gives KL-optimal distillation. Lacroce et al. (2021, 2024) give operator-theoretic optimal compression via AAK, with tight theorems at the one-letter boundary. Rabusseau–Li–Precup (2019), covered in §2, provides the spectral-learning bridge that identifies WFAs extractable by SVD of finite Hankel sub-blocks with linear second-order RNNs — closing the loop between the distillation and architectural-equivalence lines. What the whole arc is missing, and what T5 contributes, is the observation that the poles these methods take such care to locate and truncate are the same poles Flajolet–Sedgewick coefficient asymptotics need as input. The WFA approximation is the bridge; the asymptotics on the far side of the bridge has been waiting there the entire time.
