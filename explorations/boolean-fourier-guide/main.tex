\begin{quote}\small
This document provides a self-contained exposition of the mathematical
background needed to read the thesis proposal on spectral methods for
binary neural networks. It assumes familiarity with undergraduate linear
algebra, probability, and calculus, but develops all specialized material---Boolean
Fourier analysis, the Walsh--Hadamard transform, hypercontractivity,
influence theory, and their connections to coding theory and neural
networks---from scratch, with full proofs and references.

\chapter*{Roadmap}\label{ch:roadmap}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Roadmap}

This guide develops the mathematical background for the thesis
\emph{Spectral Methods for Binary Neural Networks} in seven chapters,
each building on the previous ones.

\begin{description}[style=nextline]
\item[Chapter~\ref{ch:prereqs}: Prerequisites.]
  Inner product spaces, the Kronecker product, and the probability tools
  (Markov/Chebyshev inequalities, CLT, Berry--Ess\'een) used later.
  Skip this if you are comfortable with undergraduate linear algebra and
  probability.

\item[Chapter~\ref{ch:boolean-fourier}: Fourier Analysis on the Boolean Hypercube.]
  The core of the guide. We define the parity basis, prove orthonormality,
  derive the Fourier expansion and Parseval's identity, and work through
  detailed examples (dictator, AND, majority, parity). We also prove the
  sign-rounding error bound used in the thesis.

\item[Chapter~\ref{ch:influence-noise}: Influence and Noise Sensitivity.]
  Influence of variables, total influence, the noise operator $T_\rho$,
  noise stability, and their spectral formulas. These quantify the
  sensitivity of Boolean functions to perturbations.

\item[Chapter~\ref{ch:concentration}: Hypercontractivity and Spectral Concentration.]
  The Bonami--Beckner inequality, the level-$k$ inequality, and the
  LMN theorem. These are the main tools for proving that ``simple''
  Boolean functions concentrate their spectral energy on low degrees.

\item[Chapter~\ref{ch:wht}: The Walsh--Hadamard Transform.]
  Hadamard matrices, the WHT as the computational realization of the
  Fourier expansion, the fast algorithm (FWHT), and its comparison with
  the FFT.

\item[Chapter~\ref{ch:ltf-codes-groups}: Linear Threshold Functions, Codes, and Groups.]
  Three topics connecting the theory to the thesis: LTFs and Chow's theorem,
  Reed--Muller codes and spectral truncation, and the group-theoretic
  perspective on the Walsh--Fourier basis.

\item[Chapter~\ref{ch:applications}: Application to Binary Neural Networks.]
  How all the preceding material applies to the thesis: the spectral
  parameterization, normalization, and a chapter-by-chapter map from
  this guide to the thesis.
\end{description}
