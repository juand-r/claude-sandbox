# Stuart Umpleby — Second-Order Cybernetics and Recent Work

## Affiliation
- Professor Emeritus, Department of Management, George Washington University
- ASC President 1980–1982; honorary life fellow since 2014

## Key Position: The AI-Cybernetics Split

Umpleby's historical account of the AI-cybernetics divergence is essential context:

In 1956, John McCarthy organized the Dartmouth workshop, and AI separated from cybernetics. The biologists, psychologists, and philosophers (McCulloch et al.) wanted to study the brain to understand cognition; the engineers (Minsky, McCarthy, Selfridge) sought to build machines.

Federal funding then shifted from both cybernetics and AI to just AI and robotics, creating the institutional separation that persists today.

Result: Two branches of cybernetics research:
1. **Technical branch** (including AI) — engineering focus
2. **Philosophical/theoretical branch** — continuing McCulloch, von Foerster, Ashby, Maturana

Umpleby diagnoses "a large gap between theoretical cybernetics research in the US and applied cybernetics research."

## Assessment: Relevance to AI Agents

**Umpleby's work does NOT directly address modern AI systems** (LLMs, AI agents, deep learning). His focus remains on:
- History and philosophy of cybernetics
- Second-order science methodology
- Social systems and governance
- Third-order cybernetics (societal evolution)

However, his framework provides essential **meta-theoretical context**:
- **Second-order cybernetics** (observer-inclusion) is the philosophical basis for why AI agents need self-models and reflexivity
- **Third-order cybernetics** (societal evolution) frames how AI agents might participate in social systems
- The **AI-cybernetics split narrative** explains why modern AI lacks cybernetic awareness

The practical bridge from Umpleby's theory to agent implementation would need to come from others (possibly Pangaro, possibly our own work).
