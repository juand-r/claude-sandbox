# Paul Pangaro — Cybernetics, AI, and Conversation Theory (2024–2025)

Pangaro is the most active bridge figure between classical cybernetics and contemporary AI critique. This file consolidates his recent conference and publication activity.

## Affiliation

- Director, Laboratory for Cybernetics, School of Architecture, Carnegie Mellon University
- President, American Society for Cybernetics (since 2021)
- PhD with Gordon Pask in Conversation Theory, Brunel University (UK)

## Core Position on AI

Pangaro's consistent argument: AI arose from the theoretical side of cybernetics, but AI moved away from the primacy of **actions and interactions** as the means of understanding and achieving intelligence. Current AI (LLMs, neural nets) lacks genuine conversational capacity — genuine feedback, genuine learning-through-interaction.

He positions Pask's *Colloquy of Mobiles* and conversation theory as offering "contrast to the current limitations of AI and neural nets," arguing that "while science and engineering have failed to tame wicked problems, designing for conversation opens new paths for collaboration across human-machine and human-human interactions."

## The "Graphical Conversation Theory" Lineage

Pangaro's site logo references a 1976 MIT Architecture Machine Group proposal — "Graphical Conversation Theory" — which outlined applying Pask's CT to human-machine interfaces. The icons represent:
1. A person interacting with a machine
2. Persons interacting through networked machines
3. **A machine, with full agency and capacity to converse, interacting through a machine to a person**

This third icon is essentially the vision of a conversational AI agent, articulated in 1976. The current AI agent wave is arriving at the same destination from a very different direction (statistical learning vs. cybernetic conversation).

## Assessment

Pangaro is the most important living link between Pask's conversation theory and contemporary AI discourse. His work provides:
1. **A critique** of current AI agents (lacking genuine conversational capacity)
2. **A design framework** (conversation theory as design methodology)
3. **An alternative vision** (agents as conversational participants, not just tool-users)

However, his output remains primarily **philosophical/design-theoretical** rather than **engineering/implementation**. He has not (yet) published a paper that directly applies CT to LLM agent architecture. The gap between his framework and working code is the gap our project might help bridge.
