# Conferences on Self-Organizing Systems (1959, 1962)

## Overview

Two conferences on self-organizing systems, both co-sponsored by the Office of Naval
Research. These conferences bridged first-wave cybernetics and emerging systems theory,
focusing on how order arises in complex systems.

---

## First Conference (May 5-6, 1959)

**Title:** Interdisciplinary Conference on Self-Organizing Systems

**Location:** Hosted by Armour Research Foundation, Illinois Institute of Technology

**Sponsors:** Information Systems Branch, Office of Naval Research; Armour Research Foundation

**Proceedings:** Yovits, Marshall C. & Cameron, Scott (eds.). *Self-Organizing Systems:
Proceedings of an Interdisciplinary Conference.* Pergamon Press, 1960.

**Planning Committee:**
- Marshall C. Yovits (ONR, Chairman)
- Scott Cameron (Armour Research Foundation, Secretary)
- Albert R. Dawe, Gordon D. Goldstein, Harold Kantner, Maynard Shelly

### Key Papers

- **Heinz von Foerster** — "On Self-Organizing Systems and Their Environments" (pp. 31-50)
  - Famous provocative argument: there are "no such things as self-organizing systems" in isolation
  - Any system that appears to organize itself must be exchanging energy/information with environment
  - Foundational for second-order cybernetics

- **Other papers:** All papers presented at the conference are included in proceedings, in order of presentation, each followed by discussion. Exception: Marvin Minsky's "Progress on the Advice Taker" could not be submitted before publication.

## University of Illinois Symposium / Second Conference (1961, published 1962)

**Title:** Symposium on Self-Organization (also called Principles of Self-Organization)

**Location:** Robert Allerton Park, University of Illinois

**Proceedings:** Von Foerster, H. & Zopf Jr., G.W. (eds.). *Principles of Self-Organization:
Transactions of the University of Illinois Symposium on Self-Organization.* Pergamon Press,
Oxford, 1962.

### Key Papers

- **W. Ross Ashby** — "Principles of the Self-Organizing System" (pp. 255-278)
  - Classic paper. Two meanings of "self-organization" explored:
    1. A machine changing its own organization (Ashby argued this is metaphysically superfluous)
    2. A dynamical system evolving toward equilibrium/attractor, reducing entropy = self-organization
  - The term "self-organizing system" was coined by Ashby in early cybernetics
  - Ashby's purpose: describe deterministic machines that could change their own organization
  - Republished by *E:CO (Emergence: Complexity & Organization)* journal with permission of Ashby's daughters
  - Also freely available at: https://csis.pace.edu/~marchese/CS396x/Computing/Ashby.pdf (returned 503 at time of research)

## Third Conference (May 22-24, 1962)

**Title:** Conference on Self-Organizing Systems

**Proceedings:** Yovits, Marshall C.; Jacobi, George T.; Goldstein, Gordon D. (eds.).
*Self-Organizing Systems, 1962.* Spartan Books, Washington DC, 1962. 574 pp.

**Note:** This is a DIFFERENT volume from the von Foerster/Zopf 1962 book above.
The task description mentions "proceedings edited by Yovits & Cameron" for both 1959 and
1962, but Cameron co-edited only the 1959 proceedings. The 1962 Spartan Books volume
was co-edited by Jacobi and Goldstein.

## Relevance to AI Agent Architectures

1. **Self-organization without central control** — directly relevant to multi-agent systems
   and emergent coordination (stigmergy, swarm intelligence)
2. **Von Foerster's environment coupling** — agents cannot be understood without their environment;
   mirrors the agent-environment loop in RL and tool-using agents
3. **Ashby's two meanings** — the distinction between genuine self-modification and
   deterministic state evolution maps onto the difference between meta-learning agents
   and fixed-policy agents
4. **Widrow's ADALINE** — early neural network work, direct ancestor of modern deep learning
