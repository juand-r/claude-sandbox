## 1. The Formal Definition of Autopoiesis

### Primary Definition (Maturana & Varela, 1980)

An autopoietic machine is:

> "a machine organized (defined as a unity) as a network of processes of production (transformation and destruction) of components that produces the components which: (i) through their interactions and transformations continuously regenerate and realize the network of processes (relations) that produced them and (ii) constitute it (the machine) as a concrete unity in the space in which they (the components) exist by specifying the topological domain of its realization as such a network."

— *Autopoiesis and Cognition: The Realization of the Living* (1980), p. 78

### Parsing the Definition: Necessary and Sufficient Conditions

The definition specifies **two jointly necessary conditions** for autopoiesis:

1. **Self-regeneration (process closure)**: The components, through their interactions and transformations, continuously regenerate and realize the network of processes that produced them. This is circular causality — the network produces components, and the components realize the network.

2. **Boundary specification (topological closure)**: The components constitute the system as a concrete unity in space by specifying a topological domain — a boundary — for its realization as a network. The boundary is not externally imposed; it emerges from the same metabolic processes it encloses.

These two conditions together are held to be **sufficient** to define a living system. The claim is that autopoiesis is the necessary and sufficient organization of the living.

### The Circular Structure

The definition has an irreducibly circular structure, and this is deliberate:
- The network produces components
- The components realize the network
- Some components form a boundary
- The boundary entraps the network that produces the boundary

This codependence between network and boundary is not a defect but the essence of the concept. A cell is the paradigm: metabolic reactions (network) produce molecules (components), including membrane molecules (boundary), which contain the reactions that produce them.

### The Six Criteria (Varela, Maturana & Uribe, 1974)

In "Autopoiesis: the organization of living systems, its characterization and a model" (*Biosystems* 5, 187–196), they proposed six decisional rules an observer must verify to determine whether a system is autopoietic. These constitute a validation test requiring close observation of "intra-boundaries" phenomenology: components' self-production, their spatial distribution, and the temporal occurrence of interaction events.

The six fundamental properties they identified: identity, integrity, self-boundness, self-maintenance, external inflow of matter, and external inflow of energy. Any rigorous assessment must go beyond merely observing self-organization, embodied boundaries, and autonomous behavior.

### Allopoietic vs. Autopoietic

An allopoietic system (e.g., a factory, a car) produces something other than itself. Its components do not produce the network that produces them. An autopoietic system's sole product is its own organization. This is the key distinction.

## 2. Organization vs. Structure

This distinction is fundamental and often poorly understood.

**Organization**: The relations between components that define the system as a unity of a particular class. Organization is what makes a system what it is — its identity. Organization is **abstract** — it specifies relations, not particular components.

**Structure**: The actual components and relations that realize the organization at any given moment. Structure is **concrete** — it is the physical instantiation.

Key principle: **The same organization can be realized by different structures.** A cell maintains its autopoietic organization while continuously replacing its molecular components. The organization is invariant; the structure changes continuously.

> "The organization of a machine is independent of the properties of its components; the same organization can be carried out by different kinds of components, therefore by different structures."
> — Maturana & Varela (1980), p. 77; Varela (1979), p. 9

This distinction is what grounds Maturana's rejection of self-organization (see Section 6).

## 3. Operational Closure vs. Thermodynamic Openness

### The Precise Distinction

Autopoietic systems are:

- **Organizationally/operationally closed**: Their internal processes form a self-referential network. The system's operations refer only to themselves and to the components they produce. There are sufficient processes within the system to maintain the whole.

- **Informationally closed**: The system does not "receive" information from the environment. An autopoietic system does not take in information from its environment and an external agent cannot control an autopoietic system. Perturbations are not instructions — the system internally generates meaning from perturbations through its own structure. In Ashby's cybernetic formulation: systems are "open to energy but closed to information and control" — they are "information tight."

- **Thermodynamically/materially open**: They exchange matter and energy with the environment, as required by physics. Maturana himself stated that autopoietic closure "is realized through a continuous structural change under conditions of continuous material interchange with the medium" and that it "does not imply the violation of [thermodynamic] constraints, but constitutes a particular mode of realization of autopoiesis in a space in which thermodynamic constraints are valid."

- **Structurally open**: Their physical structure can change through interactions with the environment, even as their organization remains invariant.

### The Critical Insight

The common error is to think of closure as isolation. Operational closure means that the system's identity-defining processes form a closed loop — not that the system is sealed off from the world. The cell membrane is both a boundary and an interface. It is produced by the very processes it encloses, while simultaneously regulating material exchange with the environment.

Piaget's formulation captures this: "closure to efficient causation and openness to material causation." The system's efficient causes (what drives the processes) are internal and self-referential. The system's material causes (what provides the substrates) are external.

## 4. Structural Coupling

### Definition

Structural coupling is the mechanism by which operationally closed systems interact with their environment without compromising their organizational closure.

> An autopoietic machine "communicates with the environment only through perturbations. In order to compensate for these perturbations, the autopoietic machine undergoes internal transformations. This relation between perturbations and transformations is a process of structural coupling through which the recurrent perturbations of the environment produce a continual selection of the system's structure."
> — Varela (1979), p. 33

### Key Features

1. **Perturbations, not instructions**: The environment does not specify or instruct changes in the system. It triggers perturbations. The system's own structure determines what constitutes a perturbation and what internal change results.

2. **Mutual specification**: Both the system and the environment undergo structural changes through their interaction. "Organisms and their environments reciprocally codetermine one another." Neither is primary.

3. **History-dependence**: Structural coupling produces a history of recurrent interactions. This history shapes the system's structure, which in turn shapes what future interactions are possible. This is a form of learning without representation.

4. **Viability, not optimality**: The system does not "adapt" to the environment in the sense of converging on a representation of it. It maintains viability — it continues to exist as a unity. Any interaction that maintains viability is sufficient.

5. **Identity preservation**: While the system's structure continuously changes through coupling, its organization (identity) remains invariant. If the organization changes, the system disintegrates — it ceases to be what it was.

### The Dance Metaphor

Maturana and Varela used the metaphor of a dance: the system and environment are "dancing together," each triggering changes in the other, but neither leading. The result is a continuous co-drift of structures — "natural drift" rather than directed adaptation.

## 5. Structural Determinism

### The Principle

> "Responses to environmental perturbations are determined by [the system's] own organization, not by the perturbation itself."

Living systems are not input-output devices. Their internal architecture constrains which responses are possible. The same perturbation can produce entirely different responses in differently structured systems.

### Implications

1. **No instructive interaction**: The environment cannot specify what happens inside the system. It can only trigger changes that the system's structure permits. "The perturbations of the environment do not determine structural change: those perturbations take effect as if read or interpreted by the organism through its own internal language."

2. **The observer's trap**: What an observer sees as "the environment causing a response" is a description from outside. From the system's perspective, there is no "environment" as such — there are only perturbations filtered through structure.

3. **No information transfer**: Information is not transmitted from environment to system. The concept of "information" as something that exists independently and is transferred is rejected. Information is always relative to the system that generates it internally.

4. **Determinism ≠ predictability**: Structural determinism does not mean the system is predictable. Its structure is enormously complex and continuously changing through structural coupling. Determinism here means only that the system's responses are determined by its structure, not that we can predict them.

## 6. The Santiago Theory of Cognition: Cognition = Life

### The Central Claim

> "Living systems are cognitive systems, and living as a process is a process of cognition. This statement is valid for all organisms, with or without a nervous system."
> — Maturana, *Biology of Cognition* (1970)

### The Formal Argument

The argument proceeds in three steps (from *Biology of Cognition*):

1. **Living systems are defined by circular organization** that maintains their identity through interactions. Their domain of interactions constitutes their cognitive reality. The system's organization determines what counts as a perturbation, what counts as a viable response — this determination IS cognition.

2. **Living systems are inferential systems**: They make predictions about classes of interactions in their niche to maintain their circular organization. A bacterium swimming up a sugar gradient is "predicting" that movement in that direction will maintain its viability. This prediction-making IS cognition.

3. **Evolution changes cognitive domains**: Evolution modifies how living systems maintain their circular organization, thereby evolving their cognitive domains over generations. Phylogeny is cognitive evolution.

### Why This Is Not Metaphor

Maturana insisted this is not metaphorical. The claim is not "bacteria are like cognitive systems." The claim is that cognition just IS the process by which an operationally closed system maintains its viability through structural coupling with its medium. If you accept the definition of autopoiesis, cognition follows necessarily:

- An autopoietic system must maintain its organization
- To maintain its organization, it must navigate perturbations
- Navigation of perturbations requires "sensing" (structure-determined responsiveness) and "acting" (structure-determined compensation)
- This sensing-and-acting IS cognition

Therefore: "cognition is simply the process by which [structural] coupling is navigated." Every organism performing viable interactions is engaged in cognition.

### The Nervous System

The nervous system does not represent the external world. It expands the domain of possible interactions available to the organism. Maturana's work on color vision in pigeons was foundational: he showed that color is not obtained as an objective reality of specific wavelengths by the nervous system, but as a representation of **internal neuronal relations**. The nervous system operates as an operationally closed network that maintains internal coherence — perception is not representation but maintenance of internal coherence responsive to perturbations.

### Implications

- A bacterium navigating a chemical gradient is cognitively engaged, in exactly the same formal sense as a human solving a differential equation. The complexity differs; the organizational principle does not.
- Cognition does not require a nervous system. A nervous system enriches the cognitive domain but does not create it.
- There is no cognition without life (in the autopoietic sense), and no life without cognition.

## 7. Maturana's Rejection of "Self-Organization"

### The Argument

Maturana explicitly rejected the use of "self-organization" as a synonym for autopoiesis:

> "[I would] never use the notion of self-organization... Operationally it is impossible. That is, if the organization of a thing changes, the thing changes."

### The Logic

The argument is precise and rests on the organization/structure distinction:

1. Organization defines identity. What makes a system the kind of system it is, IS its organization.
2. If the organization changes, the system ceases to be what it was. A cell whose autopoietic organization changes is no longer a cell — it is dead matter, or something else entirely.
3. "Self-organization" implies that the system changes its own organization — i.e., changes its own identity.
4. But an autopoietic system is defined precisely by the invariance of its organization. The whole point of autopoiesis is that the organization is maintained while the structure changes.
5. Therefore, "self-organization" is a contradiction in terms when applied to autopoietic systems.

What actually changes is **structure**, not organization. The system undergoes continuous **structural change** through structural coupling with its environment. But this is not "self-organization" — it is structural drift within an invariant organization.

### The Deeper Point

The deeper issue is that "self-organization" (in the Ashby/Prigogine tradition) is articulated in terms of dynamical systems theory — states, behaviors, regularities. Autopoietic theory uses a fundamentally different language: processes, relations, reciprocal modifications between processes, transformation of transient components. These are not merely different vocabularies for the same thing; they reflect a radical difference in ontology. Ashby's framework treats components as fixed entities whose states change. Maturana's framework treats components as transient products of processes.

## 8. Enactivism: Cognition as Bringing-Forth

### Origin

The term "enaction" was introduced in Varela, Thompson & Rosch, *The Embodied Mind* (1991). They proposed the name "enaction" to "emphasize the growing conviction that cognition is not the representation of a pre-given world by a pre-given mind but is rather the enactment of a world and a mind on the basis of a history of the variety of actions that a being in the world performs."

### The Two Principles of Enactivism

Varela, Thompson & Rosch state:

1. **Perception consists in perceptually guided action.** Perception is not passive reception of information but active exploration.
2. **Cognitive structures emerge from recurrent sensorimotor patterns** that allow action to be perceptually guided.

### Arguments Against Representationalism

#### The Formal Argument

Cognitivism (Marr, Fodor, Pylyshyn) holds that cognition involves:
- An external world with pre-given, determinate properties
- Internal representations of those properties
- Computational processing of those representations

Enactivism rejects all three:

1. **The world is not pre-given**: Organisms do not encounter a neutral, pre-specified world. They enact domains of significance. Sucrose exists as molecules in physics, but becomes "food" only through bacterial metabolic activity. The meaningful world is co-constituted by organism and environment.

2. **Representations are explanatorily vacuous**: Information understood only as covariance (correlation between internal states and external states) lacks inherent semantic content. Naturalistic theories cannot explain how non-semantic information generates contentful thought without presupposing the very content-capacity they aim to explain. This is the "hard problem of content" (Hutto & Myin).

3. **The inverse optics fallacy**: David Marr influentially framed vision as solving "inverse optics" — recovering the truth about visible properties in the environment. But this begs the question of what counts as a "property" and who determines what is "out there." The organism's physiology, its sensorimotor circuits, and its history of interactions determine what shows up. Color is not recovered from wavelengths; it is generated through internal neuronal relations (Maturana's pigeon experiments).

#### The Empirical Arguments

- **Color perception**: Varela et al. used color perception as a paradigmatic case. Color categories are not determined by wavelength distributions alone — they depend on the organism's visual physiology, ecological niche, and evolutionary history. Different organisms with different visual systems literally inhabit different color worlds. Color is "brought forth," not detected.

- **Sensorimotor adaptation**: Evidence from change blindness, visual filling-in, and sensorimotor adaptation supports the view that perception is an active process of exploration, not passive registration of a scene.

- **Bacterial chemotaxis**: Even the simplest organisms demonstrate sense-making — generating domains of significance through their activity — without anything remotely like internal representations.

#### The Scale-Up Objection

The main objection against enactivism: it may work for simple perception and bacterial chemotaxis, but can it explain higher cognition — language, imagination, planning, abstract thought? Dennett argued that "something awfully like representation is going to have to creep in like the tide" once we move beyond simple creatures. This remains the central challenge for enactivism.

### Sense-Making

Later enactivists (Di Paolo, Thompson) developed the concept of **sense-making**: the autonomous, significance-constituting activity of living systems. Sense-making is not the processing of pre-given information but the generation of meaning through the system's own norms. An organism generates its own norms through autopoietic self-maintenance — what threatens viability is "bad," what maintains it is "good." This is the origin of normativity and intentionality, grounded in the biology of self-production rather than in mental representations.

## 9. Can Artificial Systems Be Autopoietic?

### The Formal Requirements

For a system to be autopoietic, it must satisfy:

1. **Component production**: The system must produce its own components through its own processes.
2. **Network regeneration**: Those components must, through their interactions, regenerate the network of processes that produced them.
3. **Boundary specification**: The components must constitute the system as a concrete unity in physical space by specifying a topological domain (boundary).
4. **Material exchange**: The system must be thermodynamically open (exchanging matter and energy) while organizationally closed.
5. **Self-maintenance**: The system must maintain its organization through continuous structural change.

### Where Current AI Systems Fail

Current AI systems are **allopoietic**: they produce outputs other than themselves. Specifically:

1. **No self-production of components**: An AI system does not produce its own hardware (transistors, wires, chips). Its physical substrate is produced externally and maintained externally. The software does not produce the hardware that runs the software.

2. **No self-generated boundary**: The boundary of an AI system (its casing, its network interface) is externally imposed, not produced by the system's own processes.

3. **No material metabolism**: AI systems process information but do not engage in the continuous material transformation that characterizes autopoiesis. They do not consume and transform substrates to produce their own components.

4. **External maintenance**: If the power goes off, the system stops. It does not actively maintain its own conditions of existence (though it could be argued that thermoregulation in data centers is a primitive form of this).

5. **No organizational invariance through structural change**: AI systems can be reprogrammed, reconfigured, or updated — but these changes are imposed externally, not generated by the system's own processes as structural drift through coupling.

### The In-Principle Question

Maturana and Varela formulated autopoiesis in the language of machines and cybernetics, which in principle leaves open non-biological realizations. They wrote that the organization of a machine "does not specify the properties of the components which realize the machine as a concrete system, it only specifies the relations which these must generate."

This suggests that autopoiesis is a **functional** concept — what matters is the relational organization, not the specific substrate. In principle, if an artificial system could:
- Produce its own physical components through its own processes
- Generate a boundary from those components
- Maintain organizational invariance through continuous material self-production
- Be operationally closed while thermodynamically open

...then it would be autopoietic.

### Proposals

- **Schwitzgebel's minimal autopoiesis**: A system that actively maintains itself, enforces a boundary, and continually generates its own material components using predictive processing and a solar-electric metabolism could satisfy the criteria.

- **The Minary framework**: Proposes a "computationally autopoietic primitive" — functionally closed but operationally open — aimed at systems that are robust to component failure and adaptive without retraining.

### Maturana's Own View

Maturana (2002) insisted that autopoiesis exists only in the molecular domain — the only domain where entities through their interactions constitute autopoietic systems. He rejected extensions into sociology (contra Luhmann) and was skeptical of computational realizations. The molecular domain has specific properties (chemical bonding, thermodynamics, spatial organization) that may not have functional equivalents in other substrates.

### The Open Question

The debate reduces to: is autopoiesis **substrate-independent** (as the machine-theoretic language suggests) or **substrate-dependent** (as Maturana's insistence on the molecular domain suggests)? The formal definition appears to allow substrate independence, but the actual realization may require material properties (continuous production and destruction of physical components, thermodynamic self-maintenance) that digital systems do not exhibit.

## 10. Key Distinctions and Common Confusions

| Concept | Is NOT | IS |
|---------|--------|-----|
| Autopoiesis | Self-organization | Self-production with organizational invariance |
| Operational closure | Isolation from environment | Self-referential process network |
| Structural coupling | Information transfer | Perturbation-triggered structural drift |
| Cognition (Santiago) | Computation over representations | Viable navigation of structural coupling |
| Perturbation | Instruction or input | Trigger whose effect is structure-determined |
| Organization | Physical structure | Abstract relational pattern (identity) |
| Structure | Organization | Concrete instantiation of organization |
| Enaction | Representation of pre-given world | Bringing forth of domains of significance |

## 11. Critical Assessment and Debates

### Strengths
- Provides a unified account of life and cognition grounded in biology
- Avoids the homunculus problem (no internal agent reading representations)
- Explains autonomy without vitalism
- Accounts for the origin of normativity (viability as the ground of value)

### Weaknesses and Criticisms
- **Circularity**: The definition of autopoiesis is deliberately circular, which some see as a logical flaw rather than a feature
- **Solipsism concern**: The extreme language of self-referentiality and informational closure has been criticized as leading to a form of biological solipsism
- **Scale-up problem**: Enactivism works well for simple organisms but struggles with higher cognition
- **Unfalsifiability**: Some critics argue the theory is so abstract that it cannot be empirically falsified
- **Social systems**: Maturana rejected the extension of autopoiesis to social systems (contra Luhmann), and the six criteria seem to formally preclude it — social systems cannot constrain agent autonomy sufficiently without becoming incompatible with human behavior
- **Organization/structure distinction**: The boundary between what counts as "organization" (invariant) and "structure" (variable) is not always clear in practice
