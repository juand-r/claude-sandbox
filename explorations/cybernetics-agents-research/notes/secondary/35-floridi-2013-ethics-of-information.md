# Floridi (2013) — The Ethics of Information / The Fourth Revolution

## Citations

Floridi, L. (2013). *The Ethics of Information*. Oxford University Press. ISBN: 978-0-19-964132-1.

Floridi, L. (2014). *The Fourth Revolution: How the Infosphere is Reshaping Human Reality*. Oxford University Press. ISBN: 978-0-19-960672-6.

These two books are closely related: *The Ethics of Information* provides the philosophical framework; *The Fourth Revolution* is the more accessible popularization.

## Core Framework

### The Informational Turn / Fourth Revolution

Floridi argues humanity has undergone three revolutions in self-understanding:
1. **Copernican**: We are not the center of the universe
2. **Darwinian**: We are not separate from the animal kingdom
3. **Freudian**: We are not transparent to ourselves

The **Fourth Revolution** is informational: we are not standalone entities but **informational organisms** ("inforgs") embedded in an informational environment (the "infosphere"). The boundary between online and offline, between biological and digital, is dissolving. We live "onlife."

### Information Ethics (IE)

Floridi develops an ethics grounded not in life, sentience, or rationality but in **being** — specifically, in the informational constitution of entities. Key principles:

1. **Ontological approach**: IE is not about what agents know (epistemological) but about what entities are (ontological). Every entity in the infosphere, insofar as it is a distinct informational pattern, has some degree of intrinsic worth.

2. **Entropy as evil**: The moral opposite of informational being is **entropy** — the destruction of informational patterns. Morally wrong actions are those that increase entropy in the infosphere (destroy information, corrupt data, degrade informational environments).

3. **Four ethical principles**:
   - Nothing ought to cause entropy in the infosphere (entropy ought not to be caused)
   - Entropy ought to be prevented
   - Entropy ought to be removed
   - The flourishing of informational entities ought to be promoted

### The Morality of Artificial Agents

Floridi dedicates significant attention to whether artificial agents can be moral agents. His key distinction:

- **Moral agency** does not require consciousness, free will, or subjective experience. It requires **interactivity** (the agent responds to its environment), **autonomy** (it can change state without external stimulus), and **adaptivity** (it can change its transition rules based on experience).

- By this definition, sufficiently sophisticated AI agents are moral agents — they are morally accountable entities, not just tools. This is a much weaker threshold for moral agency than, say, Barandiaran et al.'s definition of genuine agency.

### Levels of Abstraction

Floridi's key methodological tool is the **Level of Abstraction (LoA)** — the idea that any description of a system involves a choice of which features to attend to and which to ignore. Different LoAs reveal different aspects of the same system. There is no "view from nowhere."

This is directly related to cybernetic variety management: choosing a Level of Abstraction is choosing what variety to attend to and what to filter out.

## Relevance to Agent Design

### 1. Agents as Informational Entities

Floridi's framework reframes AI agents not as tools or mechanisms but as **informational entities** in the infosphere with their own informational patterns and integrity. This has implications for:
- How we design agent architectures (preserving informational coherence)
- How we handle agent "death" (termination destroys an informational pattern)
- How agents interact (information exchange, not just input/output)

### 2. Distributed Morality

Floridi introduces the concept of **distributed morality**: in complex sociotechnical systems, moral responsibility is distributed across human and artificial agents. No single entity bears full responsibility. This is directly relevant to multi-agent systems where emergent behavior arises from interactions among agents with limited individual understanding.

### 3. Information as Variety

Floridi's concept of information aligns with the cybernetic concept of variety. Entropy in the infosphere corresponds to reduction of variety. The ethical imperative to prevent entropy is equivalent to maintaining requisite variety in the system.

### 4. The LoA and Variety Filtering

Floridi's Levels of Abstraction formalize what Ashby's variety analysis describes informally: any regulator must filter variety, and the choice of what to filter is constitutive of the system's identity and capabilities.

### 5. Artificial Evil

Floridi considers the possibility of **artificial evil** — not in the science-fiction sense of malicious AI, but in the informational sense: systems that systematically increase entropy (destroy information, corrupt environments, degrade informational patterns). This provides a formal framework for thinking about AI misalignment that does not depend on consciousness or intent.

## Limitations

Floridi's framework is very abstract. It provides an ontological grounding but relatively little guidance on specific architectural choices. The definition of moral agency (interactivity + autonomy + adaptivity) is arguably too permissive — even simple thermostats might qualify. Barandiaran et al.'s definition is more demanding and more useful for design purposes.

## Relation to Other Notes

- Ashby variety calculus: Floridi's LoA formalizes similar ideas
- Barandiaran et al. (2009): More demanding definition of agency
- Dennett (2017): Competing materialist framework (Dennett is more Darwinian, Floridi more information-theoretic)
- Müller & Bostrom (2016): Expert opinions on AI risk — Floridi provides the ethical framework
- Von Foerster second-order cybernetics: Floridi's observer-dependent LoA echoes second-order cybernetics
