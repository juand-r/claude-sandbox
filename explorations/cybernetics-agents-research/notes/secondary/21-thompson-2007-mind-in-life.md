# Thompson (2007) — Mind in Life: Biology, Phenomenology, and the Sciences of Mind

**Full Citation:** Thompson, E. (2007). *Mind in Life: Biology, Phenomenology, and the Sciences of Mind*. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press.

**Source:** Harvard UP catalog, Notre Dame Philosophical Reviews, PhilPapers, Goodreads, PsycNET, Google Books.

---

## 1. Core Thesis: Deep Continuity of Life and Mind

Where there is life, there is mind. Life and mind share common principles of self-organization: the self-organizing features of mind are an enriched version of the self-organizing features of life. This is the **life-mind continuity thesis** — one of the foundational claims of the enactive approach.

Thompson draws on molecular biology, evolutionary theory, artificial life, complex systems theory, neuroscience, psychology, Continental phenomenology (Husserl, Merleau-Ponty), and analytic philosophy to build this argument.

## 2. The Enactive Approach

Thompson labels his general view "the enactive approach," extending and revising the position he originally proposed with Rosch and Varela in *The Embodied Mind* (1991). Key commitments:

### 2.1 Autonomy and Self-Organization
Living organisms are autonomous systems — operationally closed, self-producing networks of processes. Their identity is constituted by their organization, not by their material components.

### 2.2 Sense-Making
Organisms are "sense-making" beings: they enact significance through their coupling with the environment. Meaning is not pregiven or imposed by an observer; it is generated by the organism's activity. A sugar gradient has no intrinsic meaning — it becomes meaningful through the bacterium's active engagement with it.

### 2.3 Embodied Dynamicism
The brain is a self-organizing system tracing trajectories in a state space, characterized by differential equations, in response to perturbations from the environment. What makes brains the self-organizing systems they are is their linkage of organisms' sensory surfaces with effectors to produce and maintain a "sensorimotor agent."

This rejects:
- **Cognitivism** (mind as symbol manipulation): projects observer-relative computational tasks onto the brain
- **Connectionism**: retains cognitivist tendencies, fails to situate mind in the world via sensorimotor activity
- **Functionalism**: abstracts away from the particular material embodiment that makes cognition possible

### 2.4 Emergence and Consciousness
Thompson addresses the explanatory gap (how physical processes give rise to consciousness) by refusing to close it through reduction. Instead, he reconceives both sides:
- The "physical side" must be enriched with notions of self-organization, autopoiesis, and dynamic systems theory
- The "consciousness side" must be reconceived through phenomenology (Husserl, Merleau-Ponty)

The result is not an explanation of consciousness but an unprecedented insight into its nature as an emergent property of living, embodied organization.

## 3. Key Arguments in Detail

### 3.1 Against Computationalism
The cognitivist approach treats cognition as rule-governed symbol manipulation over internal representations. Thompson's objection:
- The "symbols" and "rules" exist only from the perspective of the external observer/programmer
- The system itself has no intrinsic aboutness — no genuine intentionality
- Natural cognition involves meanings for the organism, not meanings imposed by an observer

### 3.2 Autopoiesis and Identity
Drawing on Maturana, Varela, and Di Paolo, Thompson argues that autopoietic organization establishes:
1. A distinct self — an identity maintained through continuous material exchange
2. Precariousness — the system must constantly work to maintain itself
3. Normativity — metabolism creates the logic by which events are good or bad for the organism

### 3.3 Participatory Sense-Making
Extending the concept of sense-making to social interactions: when two or more autonomous sense-making agents interact, a new domain of sense-making emerges at the level of the interaction itself. The interaction pattern develops its own autonomy.

### 3.4 Phenomenological Enrichment
Husserl's analysis of time-consciousness (protention, primal impression, retention) maps onto the dynamic structure of self-organizing neural processes. Lived experience has a temporal microstructure that neuroscience can investigate but not explain away.

## 4. Critical Analysis

### Strengths
- The most ambitious and rigorous synthesis of enactivism, phenomenology, and biology to date
- Successfully argues that the life-mind continuity thesis is not just a metaphor but has substantive empirical and theoretical content
- The critique of computationalism is devastating if one takes embodiment seriously

### Weaknesses
- The phenomenological sections can be opaque to readers outside that tradition
- The argument against computationalism may be too strong: it implies that LLMs (as disembodied computational systems) cannot have any form of genuine cognition, which is a hard position to defend given their demonstrated capabilities
- The life-mind continuity thesis, taken literally, implies that artificial agents cannot be genuinely cognitive without being genuinely alive — a very strong claim with uncertain consequences

## 5. Relevance to Agent Design

### 5.1 The Hard Challenge for AI Agents
If Thompson is right, current AI agents are fundamentally limited: they process information without making sense of it. Their "understanding" is observer-attributed, not self-generated. They have no intrinsic perspective, no precariousness, no autonomous identity at stake.

This does not mean AI agents are useless — but it means they are a different kind of thing from biological agents. They simulate cognition without instantiating it.

### 5.2 What Would a Thompsonian Agent Look Like?
An agent satisfying Thompson's criteria would need:
1. **Operational closure** — self-producing, self-maintaining organization
2. **Precariousness** — genuine conditions of viability that must be actively maintained
3. **Sense-making** — intrinsic evaluation of environmental perturbations relative to its own viability
4. **Embodiment** — physical coupling with an environment through sensorimotor interaction
5. **Temporality** — genuine history that shapes current evaluative disposition

No current AI system meets any of these criteria in a non-trivial way. This represents the most radical challenge to the AI agent paradigm from the cybernetics/enactivism tradition.

### 5.3 Pragmatic Implications
Even without fully implementing Thompson's criteria, agent designers can take guidance:
- **Design for sense-making:** Give agents evaluative frameworks that go beyond task completion — include internal "health" metrics, resource monitoring, operational integrity
- **Temporal continuity:** Agents should have genuine developmental histories, not just context windows
- **Participatory sense-making:** Multi-agent interactions should be designed to generate emergent coordination, not just execute pre-planned protocols

## 6. Connections to Other Sources in This Research

- **Maturana & Varela (autopoiesis):** Thompson is Varela's primary intellectual heir. This book extends autopoiesis into a full theory of mind.
- **Di Paolo (2005):** Thompson adopts Di Paolo's adaptivity concept as central to the enactive approach. The book can be read as Di Paolo's formalism + Thompson's philosophical argument.
- **Ashby:** Thompson acknowledges Ashby's ultrastability as a precursor to adaptive self-organization, but argues it is too mechanistic without the enrichment of autopoiesis.
- **Pfeifer & Bongard (2006):** Provide the engineering perspective on embodiment that Thompson's philosophy calls for.
- **Froese & Ziemke (2009):** Directly apply Thompson's framework to AI, deriving design principles.
- **Bateson:** The "ecology of mind" resonates with Thompson's situated, relational approach.
