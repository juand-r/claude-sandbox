# Froese (2012) — From Second-Order Cybernetics to Enactive Cognitive Science

## Citation

Froese, T. (2011). From Second-Order Cybernetics to Enactive Cognitive Science: Varela's Turn From Epistemology to Phenomenology. *Systems Research and Behavioral Science*, 28(6), 631–645. DOI: 10.1002/sres.1116

Note: Published 2011, though often cited as 2012. Available on ResearchGate and Academia.edu.

## Core Thesis

Varela's intellectual trajectory — from autopoiesis and second-order cybernetics to the enactive approach — was not a rupture but a **natural progression**. The enactive approach preserved the core insights of second-order cybernetics while adding what cybernetics lacked: a phenomenological grounding in lived experience.

## Key Arguments

### 1. Varela's Path: From Epistemology to Phenomenology

Varela began with the epistemological problems of second-order cybernetics: how does a system observe itself? How does self-reference work in cognition? His early work (with Maturana) on autopoiesis addressed this by defining living systems as self-producing networks — systems whose identity is constituted by their own operations.

But Varela came to see that the epistemological framing was insufficient. It could describe the **structure** of self-reference but could not account for the **experience** of being a self-referencing system. This motivated the turn to phenomenology.

### 2. What Second-Order Cybernetics Got Right

- The observer is always part of the system being observed
- Cognition is not representation of an external world but **enaction** — the bringing-forth of a world through interaction
- Operational closure: cognitive systems are organizationally closed (their operations produce the network of operations that produces them) while being thermodynamically open

These insights carry directly into enactivism.

### 3. What Second-Order Cybernetics Missed

Maturana's version of second-order cybernetics deliberately excluded teleology, intentionality, and subjectivity from scientific discourse. He treated them as observer-dependent attributions, not features of the system itself.

Varela disagreed. The enactive approach **reintroduced teleology and intentionality** as genuine features of autonomous living systems — not as observer projections but as consequences of the precariousness and self-maintenance of autopoietic organization.

### 4. Neurophenomenology

Varela's methodological innovation was **neurophenomenology**: the systematic integration of first-person experiential reports with third-person neuroscientific data. This was not just adding subjective reports as data but treating phenomenological analysis as a **constitutive part** of the research methodology.

### 5. Enactivism as Renaissance of Second-Order Cybernetics

Froese argues that "core enactivism in particular reflects a contemporary renaissance of the second-order cybernetic program." The key continuities are:
- Anti-representationalism
- Emphasis on autonomy and self-organization
- The constitutive role of the observer/agent
- Operational closure as the basis of cognition

The key additions are:
- First-person methodology
- Genuine teleology grounded in the precariousness of living organization
- Embodied, situated, and affective dimensions of cognition

## Relevance to Agent Design

### The Missing Dimension in AI Agents

Current AI agent architectures are purely third-person systems. They have no analogue of first-person experience, and this is not just a philosophical curiosity — it means they lack:

1. **Intrinsic normativity**: They have no basis for caring about anything. Their goals are externally imposed. Enactivism grounds normativity in the self-maintenance of autonomous organization. AI agents are not precarious — they do not need to maintain themselves to continue existing.

2. **Genuine autonomy**: In the enactive sense, autonomy means the system generates its own identity through its operations. AI agents do not do this — their identity is architecturally fixed.

3. **Teleology**: Enactive agents have purposes that arise from their own organization. AI agents have purposes assigned by designers or users.

### What This Means for Design

This is not just philosophical hand-wraving. It has concrete implications:

- **If you want agents that adapt their own goals**, you need something like autopoietic self-maintenance — the agent must have "stakes" in its own continued operation and coherence.
- **If you want agents that genuinely understand context**, the enactive framework says understanding comes from embodied engagement, not from processing representations. Current LLM agents process representations; they do not enact a world.
- **The cybernetics-to-enactivism trajectory suggests** that purely formal/structural accounts of agency (which is what cybernetics provides) are necessary but not sufficient. Something about the first-person, phenomenological dimension matters for full agency.

### The Optimistic Reading

On the other hand, Froese's account also shows that the **structural insights of cybernetics carry forward** into enactivism. If we cannot build truly enactive AI agents (and we probably cannot, given current architectures), we can at least design agents informed by the structural principles that cybernetics and enactivism share: operational closure, self-organization, autonomy, anti-representationalism.
