## 1. The 2007 Scholarpedia Entry: Models of Consciousness

### 1.1 Definition
A model of consciousness is a theoretical description that relates **brain properties** of consciousness (e.g., fast irregular electrical activity, widespread brain activation) to **phenomenal properties** of consciousness (e.g., qualia, first-person perspective, unity of conscious experience).

### 1.2 Major Models Surveyed

**Global Neuronal Workspace (Dehaene & Changeux):**
- Sensory stimuli mobilize excitatory neurons with long-range cortico-cortical connections
- Information becomes conscious when it enters a "global workspace" — broadcast to multiple brain regions
- Any global pattern inhibits alternative patterns → winner-take-all dynamics
- **Cybernetic reading**: Information broadcasting as variety distribution; the workspace as a coordination mechanism (Beer's System 2)

**Dynamic Core / TNGS (Edelman):**
- Primary consciousness arose when perceptual categorization was linked via reentry to value-dependent memory
- Higher-order consciousness arose with reentrant pathways linking categorization to language
- The "dynamic core" is a functionally integrated neural cluster with high complexity
- **Cybernetic reading**: Reentry is circular causation; the dynamic core is an eigenbehavior of neural self-organization

**Information Integration Theory (Tononi):**
- Consciousness corresponds to the capacity of a system to integrate information
- Measured by Phi (Φ) — the amount of information generated by a system above and beyond its parts
- High Φ = conscious; low Φ = unconscious
- **Cybernetic reading**: Φ measures the degree to which a system is more than the sum of its parts — organizational closure in information-theoretic terms

### 1.3 Significance as Precursor
The 2007 entry positions Seth as a careful reviewer of consciousness models before he developed his own distinctive framework. It shows his early concern with **bridging brain properties to phenomenal properties** — the problem that would lead him to predictive processing.

## 2. Seth's Trajectory: From Models to Cybernetic Bayesian Brain

### 2.1 The Key Insight (2011-2015)
Seth's major contribution comes after the 2007 entry. His 2011 paper with Critchley ("An Interoceptive Predictive Coding Model of Conscious Presence") introduced the idea that conscious experience of being a self is grounded in **interoceptive prediction** — the brain's predictive model of the body's internal states.

This was extended in the 2015 "Cybernetic Bayesian Brain" paper, which explicitly reconnects predictive processing to its cybernetic roots:

> "The upshot is a development of predictive processing that originates, not in Helmholtzian perception-as-inference, but rather in 20th-century cybernetic principles that emphasized homeostasis and predictive control."

### 2.2 The Cybernetic Connection
Seth argues that predictive processing is best understood not as a Bayesian inference engine (the standard computational framing) but as a **cybernetic control system**:

- The brain does not passively infer the causes of sensory signals
- The brain **actively controls** its sensory inputs by predicting them and acting to minimize prediction errors
- This is negative feedback: predict → sense → compare → adjust prediction or action
- Perception is "controlled hallucination" — a prediction actively maintained against incoming sensory evidence

The cybernetic framing shifts the emphasis from:
- **Inference** (passive, representational) → **Control** (active, performative)
- **Exteroception** (perceiving the external world) → **Interoception** (regulating the internal body)
- **Computation** (abstract information processing) → **Homeostasis** (maintaining viability)

### 2.3 Interoceptive Inference
The most distinctive element of Seth's framework:
- The brain's primary job is not to perceive the external world but to **regulate the body's internal milieu** (homeostasis)
- Emotions are interoceptive predictions — the brain's best guess about the body's physiological state
- The sense of self arises from the brain's predictive model of its own body
- Consciousness is fundamentally about being a body, not about perceiving a world

## 3. Relevance to Agent Design

### 3.1 Agents as Predictive Controllers
Seth's cybernetic predictive processing suggests an agent architecture:
1. The agent maintains a predictive model of its environment (world model)
2. Actions are selected to minimize prediction error — either by updating the model (perception) or by changing the environment (action)
3. This is active inference (Friston), but Seth emphasizes the **control** aspect over the **inference** aspect

For LLM agents: the agent's "predictions" are its expectations about what will happen when it takes actions. Successful agents are those whose predictions are accurate. Self-correction is prediction error minimization.

### 3.2 The Interoceptive Analogy
Seth's emphasis on interoception (internal body monitoring) suggests a neglected dimension of agent design:
- Agents should monitor their own internal states, not just their environment
- "Interoceptive" signals for agents: token usage rate, confidence levels, internal consistency, plan coherence
- An agent's "sense of self" could be grounded in its predictive model of its own operational state

### 3.3 Perception as Controlled Hallucination
This reframing has implications for how agents handle uncertainty:
- The agent's world model is always a "hallucination" — a prediction, not a veridical copy
- The question is not whether the model is accurate but whether it is **useful for control**
- Good enough for action is the standard, not correspondence with truth
- This provides a principled justification for agents acting on incomplete information

### 3.4 The Hierarchy of Prediction
Seth's model is hierarchical: higher levels predict the activity of lower levels. This maps onto agent architectures:
- **Low-level predictions**: What will this specific tool call return?
- **Mid-level predictions**: Will this task approach succeed?
- **High-level predictions**: Is this the right strategy for the overall goal?
- **Meta-level predictions**: Am I the right kind of agent for this task?

Prediction errors at different levels drive different responses: low-level errors → retry, mid-level errors → change approach, high-level errors → reassess strategy.

## 4. Connections to Other Sources

- **Ashby (1952)**: Seth explicitly draws on Ashby's concept of the brain as an adaptive, homeostatic controller. The homeostat is a precursor to the predictive brain.
- **Friston (FEP)**: Seth's work is closely related to active inference but diverges in emphasis: Seth focuses on interoception and embodiment, Friston on the formal mathematics.
- **Powers (PCT)**: Perceptual Control Theory is remarkably close to Seth's framework — both describe perception as a control process, not a passive reception. Seth adds the predictive/generative model dimension.
- **Di Paolo (2005)**: Seth's interoceptive inference connects to adaptivity — the organism's monitoring of its distance from viability boundaries is interoceptive prediction.
- **Thompson (2007)**: Seth provides neuroscientific content for Thompson's philosophical argument about the continuity of life and mind.

## 5. Assessment

The 2007 Scholarpedia entry is a useful reference but not Seth's major contribution. His significance for our research lies in the later work (2011-2015) that explicitly reconnects predictive processing to cybernetic control theory. This reconnection is important because it shows that the most influential current framework in consciousness science (predictive processing) is, at bottom, a cybernetic framework — and therefore the cybernetic analysis of agent architectures has neuroscientific backing.

Seth's emphasis on interoception as the ground of selfhood is the most provocative implication for agent design: agents with no "body" to monitor have no basis for genuine self-modeling.
