# Steels (2012) — Self-Organization and Selection in Cultural Language Evolution

## Citation

Steels, L. (2012). Self-organization and selection in cultural language evolution. In L. Steels (Ed.), *Experiments in Cultural Language Evolution* (pp. 1–37). Amsterdam: John Benjamins. DOI: 10.1075/ais.3.02ste

Also available on Semantic Scholar and Academia.edu.

## Core Thesis

Language evolves culturally — not biologically — through the interplay of two mechanisms: **self-organization** (bottom-up alignment among interacting agents) and **selection** (driven by communicative success, cognitive efficiency, and social conformity). These mechanisms can be studied experimentally using populations of artificial agents that play "language games."

## Key Arguments

### 1. Cultural Evolution, Not Biological

The dominant selectionist pressures on language are not biological (survival and reproduction) but cultural:
- **Communicative success**: Does the language system enable agents to communicate effectively?
- **Expressive adequacy**: Can the language express what needs to be expressed?
- **Cognitive effort**: Is the language system learnable and easy to process?
- **Social conformity**: Do agents converge on shared conventions?

This is selectionism applied to culture, not to genes. Language structures compete for adoption within a population, not for reproductive fitness.

### 2. Self-Organization Without Central Control

Agents without a pre-programmed language can develop a communication system from scratch through repeated interactions. This happens through self-organization:
- Agents propose linguistic forms during interactions
- Successful forms are reinforced; unsuccessful forms decay
- Alignment emerges from purely local interactions — no central authority dictates the language
- The resulting language is a collective product, not designed by any individual

This is swarm intelligence applied to language: local rules, global emergence, no central control.

### 3. Against Darwinian Meme Theory

Steels explicitly rejects Dawkins's meme framework. Memes treat cultural items as gene-like replicators that propagate "selfishly." Steels instead proposes a **co-evolutionary** relationship between agents and their environments:
- Languages are not autonomous replicators; they are products of agent interaction
- Agents actively construct and negotiate meaning; they are not passive hosts for memes
- Cultural evolution is a relational process, not a competitive one

This is a significant theoretical point that contrasts with Dennett's memetic framework.

### 4. The Language Game Methodology

Steels developed a methodology of **language games** — structured interactions between pairs of agents (simulated or robotic) that must communicate about aspects of their shared environment. By varying the game structure and observing what languages emerge, researchers can study:
- How naming conventions arise
- How grammar develops
- How spatial, temporal, and quantificational concepts get linguistically encoded
- How languages evolve to cope with changing ecological conditions

### 5. Case Studies

The book includes experiments on:
- Proper names and color terms (lexical emergence)
- Spatial relations and bodily actions (grounded meaning)
- Case grammar and constituent structure (syntactic emergence)
- Aspect marking and quantifiers (morphological emergence)

Each demonstrates that complex linguistic structure can emerge from simple interaction rules without being pre-programmed.

## Relevance to Agent Design

### 1. Emergent Communication in Multi-Agent Systems

Steels's work directly prefigures the emerging field of **emergent communication** in multi-agent reinforcement learning. His experiments show that agents can develop shared communication systems from scratch — relevant for multi-agent AI systems that need to coordinate without pre-specified protocols.

### 2. Self-Organization of Conventions

When multiple AI agents interact repeatedly, they can develop shared conventions (naming schemes, interaction patterns, division of labor) through the same self-organizing dynamics Steels describes. No central coordinator is needed.

### 3. Language as a Self-Organizing System

For LLM agents specifically: the prompts, templates, and interaction patterns that develop in LLM-based systems can be understood as self-organizing linguistic conventions. Prompt engineering is a form of cultural language evolution — successful prompts are selected and propagated, unsuccessful ones are abandoned.

### 4. Grounding Through Interaction

Steels's agents ground language in shared perceptual experience (both agents perceive the same scene). This is relevant to the grounding problem in AI: meaning comes from shared reference, not from internal representations. Agents that interact in shared environments (shared file systems, databases, APIs) have a form of shared reference that could ground their communication.

### 5. Against Pre-Specification

Steels's framework argues against pre-specifying communication protocols for multi-agent systems. Instead, let agents develop their own conventions through interaction. This is more robust, more adaptive, and more scalable than top-down protocol design.

## Connection to Cybernetics

- **Self-organization**: Core cybernetic principle applied to language
- **Feedback loops**: Communicative success/failure as feedback driving language evolution
- **Variety management**: Language must have sufficient variety (expressive power) to match the variety of meanings agents need to express — Law of Requisite Variety
- **Regulation**: Language conventions regulate communication. Steels's selectionism is a form of regulatory selection — the linguistic "regulator" must match the communicative "disturbance"
- **Circular causality**: Agents create language, language shapes agents' cognition
