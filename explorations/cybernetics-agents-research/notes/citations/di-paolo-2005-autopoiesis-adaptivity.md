# Di Paolo (2005) — Autopoiesis, Adaptivity, Teleology, Agency

**Full Citation:** Di Paolo, E.A. (2005). "Autopoiesis, Adaptivity, Teleology, Agency." *Phenomenology and the Cognitive Sciences*, 4, 429-452.

## 1. The Problem

Autopoiesis provides a language for intrinsic teleology (the system's own norms for survival), but the original formulation cannot explain **sense-making** — how organisms generate meaning from their interactions. The paper introduces **adaptivity** as the missing piece.

## 2. The Gap in Autopoiesis

Maturana and Varela's autopoiesis defines viability as a binary: the organism either maintains its organization (alive) or doesn't (dead). But this creates problems:

1. **Trivial autopoiesis:** Removing physicality from the definition elevates trivial examples (crystals, candle flames) to the status of living systems.
2. **No gradient of viability:** The original framework has no way to express that an organism is doing "well" or "poorly" — only alive or dead.
3. **No active regulation:** An autopoietic system that merely persists (is robust) is not the same as one that actively adjusts its behavior to maintain viability.
4. **No sense-making:** Without a gradient of viability, there is no basis for the organism to evaluate perturbations as good or bad, beneficial or harmful.

## 3. Adaptivity: The Key Extension

**Adaptivity** is defined as a many-layered property that allows organisms to regulate themselves with respect to their **conditions of viability**.

The critical distinction:
- **Robustness:** The system passively withstands perturbations without changing (a rock is robust).
- **Adaptivity:** The system ACTIVELY monitors and regulates its distance from the boundaries of viability, modifying its own structure or behavior to maintain or improve its viability.

An adaptive system does not merely persist — it evaluates its current state relative to viability boundaries and acts to improve its position.

## 4. Connection to Ashby's Ultrastability

Di Paolo explicitly builds on Ashby's ultrastable system (*Design for a Brain*, 1952):

- Ashby's **essential variables** = Di Paolo's conditions of viability
- Ashby's **step function** (triggered when essential variables go out of bounds) = the basic mechanism of adaptivity
- Ashby's **double feedback loop** = the architecture of adaptive regulation

But Di Paolo extends Ashby in a crucial way: Ashby's system is purely reactive — it changes parameters only AFTER essential variables go out of bounds. Di Paolo's adaptive system can evaluate its DISTANCE from the boundary and act preemptively.

This transforms ultrastability from a binary trigger (in-bounds / out-of-bounds) to a graded evaluation (how close to the boundary am I? which direction am I moving?).

## 5. Implications

### Sense-Making
With adaptivity, the organism generates its own norms. A sugar gradient is not intrinsically meaningful — it becomes meaningful only because the bacterium's autopoietic organization makes certain chemical concentrations relevant to its viability. The bacterium "makes sense" of the gradient through its adaptive engagement.

### Agency
An adaptive autopoietic system is an agent in a non-trivial sense: it generates its own norms (through autopoiesis), evaluates its state relative to those norms (through adaptivity), and acts to maintain or improve its condition (through structural coupling).

### Teleology
Intrinsic teleology (purpose originating from within the system) is grounded in autopoiesis + adaptivity. The system is not goal-directed because an external observer attributes goals to it, but because its own organization generates evaluation criteria.

### Temporality
Adaptivity introduces genuine temporality: the organism's past interactions shape its current structure, which determines how it evaluates and responds to current perturbations. History matters — not just as passive memory, but as active shaping of the organism's evaluative framework.

## 6. The Autopoiesis-Adaptivity-Cognition Chain

Di Paolo's argument extends the Santiago theory of cognition (Maturana: "living systems are cognitive systems"):

1. Autopoiesis → organizational identity and operational closure
2. Adaptivity → graded evaluation of viability conditions
3. Sense-making → autonomous generation of significance
4. Agency → norm-governed interaction with environment
5. Cognition → the integrated capacity for adaptive sense-making

Each step requires the previous ones. Cognition, in this framework, is not representation or computation but adaptive, autonomous sense-making.

## 7. Relation to Our Cybernetics-Agents Research

### Bridging Ashby and Maturana
This paper is the theoretical bridge between two of our main sources:
- Ashby's formal, mechanistic framework (ultrastability, essential variables, requisite variety)
- Maturana/Varela's biological, organizational framework (autopoiesis, structural coupling, operational closure)

Di Paolo shows they are compatible and complementary: autopoiesis provides the organizational framework, adaptivity (via ultrastability) provides the regulatory mechanism.

### For agent design
- **Graded evaluation:** Agents need not just binary success/failure signals but graded evaluations of how well they are doing relative to their operational constraints. This is richer than simple reward signals.
- **Self-generated norms:** The most autonomous agents would generate their own criteria for success from their operational requirements, not from externally imposed reward functions.
- **Active regulation:** Agents should not merely react to failures but actively monitor their distance from failure conditions and adjust preemptively.

### Connection to PCT
Powers' reorganization mechanism (modify hierarchy parameters when intrinsic error is high) is precisely Ashby's ultrastability applied to hierarchical control. Di Paolo's adaptivity extends this by adding graded evaluation rather than binary triggers.
