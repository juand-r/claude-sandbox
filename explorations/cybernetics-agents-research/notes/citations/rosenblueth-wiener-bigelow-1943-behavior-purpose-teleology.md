# Rosenblueth, Wiener & Bigelow (1943) — Behavior, Purpose and Teleology

## 1. Historical Context

This is the founding document of cybernetics, published five years before Wiener's *Cybernetics* (1948). It was written to rehabilitate the concept of teleology (purposeful behavior) as scientifically legitimate by redefining it in terms of feedback mechanisms. The paper was a direct response to the prevailing mechanistic orthodoxy that dismissed teleological explanations as unscientific.

The paper emerged from conversations between Wiener (mathematician), Rosenblueth (physiologist), and Bigelow (engineer) — the interdisciplinary core that would become the cybernetics movement.

## 2. The Classification of Behavior

The paper proposes a hierarchical classification of behavior using a behavioristic (input-output) approach:

### Level 1: Active vs. Passive
- **Active behavior:** The object is the source of the output energy (e.g., a man walking).
- **Passive behavior:** The output energy comes from the input (e.g., a ball being kicked).

### Level 2: Purposeful vs. Purposeless (Random)
- **Purposeful (teleological) behavior:** Behavior directed toward a goal — "a final condition in which the behaving object reaches a definite correlation in time or in space with respect to another object or event."
- **Non-purposeful (random) behavior:** No correlation with a goal.

Key move: purpose is defined **behavioristically**, not mentalisitcally. "The term purposeful is meant to denote that the act or behavior may be interpreted as directed to the attainment of a goal." This is observable from outside.

### Level 3: Feedback vs. Non-Feedback
- **Feedback (teleological) behavior:** The behaving object uses signals from the goal to modify its behavior during the act. The output is "fed back" as input.
- **Non-feedback (non-teleological) purposeful behavior:** The act proceeds to its goal without adjustment (e.g., a frog flicking its tongue at a fly — ballistic, no correction).

### Level 4: Predictive vs. Non-Predictive
Among feedback-driven purposeful behaviors:
- **Non-predictive (first-order):** The system reacts to the current position of the target.
- **Predictive (extrapolative):** The system anticipates future positions of the target. Can be further subdivided by orders of prediction (first-order predicts velocity, second-order predicts acceleration, etc.).

## 3. The Key Arguments

### Teleology rehabilitated
The central argument: "purposeful behavior" need not invoke vitalism or mentalism. It is fully definable in terms of observable feedback mechanisms. A torpedo homing on a ship is purposeful in the same formal sense as a cat stalking a mouse — both use negative feedback to reduce the discrepancy between current state and goal.

### Feedback as the mechanism of purpose
Negative feedback is the mechanism that makes behavior goal-directed. The system continuously compares its current state with the desired state and acts to reduce the difference. This is the same mechanism whether the system is biological or mechanical.

### Pathological oscillation
The authors note that excessive negative feedback gain can produce oscillatory behavior — the system overshoots the goal, overcorrects, overshoots again. They identify this with certain neurological pathologies: "If the check is inadequate the result is an oscillatory behavior" — comparing this to intention tremor in cerebellar disease.

## 4. Significance for Our Research

### Foundation of all cybernetic work
This paper is the seed from which everything else in our notes grows:
- Ashby's stability and regulation theory formalizes the feedback concept
- Powers' PCT builds the entire hierarchy of perceptual control on negative feedback
- The Good Regulator Theorem extends the question of what the feedback system must contain
- Beer's VSM operationalizes feedback at organizational scale

### The classification scheme as agent taxonomy
The four-level classification maps directly onto agent architectures:
1. **Passive vs. active** → reactive vs. autonomous agents
2. **Purposeful vs. random** → goal-directed vs. exploratory behavior
3. **Feedback vs. non-feedback** → closed-loop vs. open-loop control
4. **Predictive vs. non-predictive** → model-based vs. reactive agents

### The predictive hierarchy
The orders of prediction (first-order, second-order, etc.) anticipate modern hierarchical prediction models — including predictive coding and active inference. Higher-order predictions require more sophisticated internal models.
