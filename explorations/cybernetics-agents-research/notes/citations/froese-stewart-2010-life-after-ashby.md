## 1. The Problem

Autopoiesis was conceived by Maturana and Varela as providing the necessary and sufficient conditions for distinguishing the living from the non-living. But there is a growing consensus that the original conception is **necessary but insufficient**. It fails to meet:

- **Constructive requirements:** How does autopoiesis arise in the first place?
- **Interactive requirements:** How do autopoietic systems interact meaningfully?
- **Normative requirements:** How do they evaluate perturbations as good or bad?
- **Historical requirements:** How does developmental and evolutionary history matter?
- **Phenomenological requirements:** How does subjective experience relate to autopoietic organization?

## 2. The Root Problem: Ashby's Framework

Froese and Stewart trace these failures to the abstract general systems framework in which autopoiesis was first formulated — specifically, Ashby's cybernetics.

### Ashby's Ultrastability: Strengths
- Provides a mechanistic framework for adaptive behavior
- Shows how trial-and-error adaptation works without gradient information
- Demonstrates that purposeful behavior can emerge from non-purposeful mechanisms

### Ashby's Ultrastability: Limitations
- Frames living systems as **statically reactive** — they act only when essential variables go out of bounds
- The homeostat is "an essentially passive system whose natural state is to indefinitely remain in stability-as-stasis"
- No account of active exploration, anticipation, or meaning-generation
- No room for the kind of autonomous, self-organizing, meaning-generating activity that characterizes actual living systems

## 3. The Proposed Solution

Restrict autopoiesis to **radical embodiment in chemical self-production under far-from-equilibrium conditions**. The abstract systems-theoretic formulation (which makes autopoiesis applicable to trivial cases) must be supplemented with:

1. **Materiality:** Autopoiesis requires physical, chemical self-production — not just abstract organizational closure
2. **Far-from-equilibrium:** The system must be thermodynamically precarious — actively maintained against dissipation
3. **Active sense-making:** The system must generate its own evaluative norms, not merely respond to threshold violations

## 4. The Ashby-Maturana Tension

The paper surfaced a genuine intellectual tension:

| Ashby's Framework | Maturana's Framework |
|------------------|---------------------|
| States, behaviors, regularities | Processes, relations, mutual modifications |
| Components as fixed entities with changing states | Components as transient products of processes |
| Observer-independent description | Observer-dependent description |
| Mechanism → function | Organization → identity |
| Passive until disturbed | Actively self-maintaining |

Maturana himself responded in *Cybernetics and Human Knowing* (2011), noting: "it is unfortunate that Tom Froese and John Stewart in their article have treated the thinking of Ross Ashby as a conceptual source" for his work. He argued that Ashby's framework and autopoietic theory are "based on distinct, often incompatible, assumptions."

## 5. Relation to Our Cybernetics-Agents Research

### The fundamental design question for agents
This paper crystallizes a central question: should artificial agents be designed as:
- **Ashbian systems:** Reactive, passively stable, adapting only when error occurs (closer to current RL/control theory approaches)?
- **Autopoietic systems:** Actively self-maintaining, meaning-generating, precarious (closer to the enactivist ideal but currently unrealized in AI)?

### The passivity critique applies to current AI agents
Current AI agents are essentially Ashbian: they respond to inputs, optimize objectives, and remain passive when no task is given. They lack:
- Active self-maintenance
- Self-generated norms
- Genuine autonomy (they depend on external infrastructure)
- Precariousness (their existence doesn't depend on their own activity)

### Di Paolo's adaptivity as middle ground
Di Paolo (2005) proposed adaptivity as a bridge: systems that actively regulate their distance from viability boundaries, not just react when boundaries are crossed. This is more than Ashbian ultrastability but less than full autopoietic self-production.

For agent design, this suggests: agents should monitor their own operational health and efficiency proactively, not just respond to explicit failure signals.
