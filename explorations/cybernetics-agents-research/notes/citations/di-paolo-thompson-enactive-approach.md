## 1. The Key Differentiator: Autonomy

The concept that differentiates enactivism from other embodied approaches to mind is **autonomy**. By making use of this concept, we can give operational criteria for distinguishing cognitive systems from non-cognitive systems.

Most embodied approaches to cognition still individuate bodies and cognitive systems from outside, by convention. Enactivism insists that the body is **self-individuating** — it actively generates and maintains the distinction between itself and its environment.

## 2. Autonomy Defined

An autonomous system is defined as an **operationally-closed and precarious** system.

### Operational Closure
A network of processes is operationally closed when every process in the network is either:
- **Enabling:** it is a necessary condition for at least one other process in the network
- **Enabled:** at least one other process in the network is a necessary condition for it

This means every process both depends on and supports other processes in the network. The closure is organizational, not thermodynamic — the system is open to matter and energy but closed in its constitutive processes.

### Precariousness
A system is precarious when the processes in the network must actively be sustained. If any enabling process stops, the entire network degrades. This distinguishes living systems from stable physical patterns (a crystal is stable but not precarious — its pattern persists without active maintenance).

Precariousness + operational closure = **autonomy**. The system must actively produce the conditions for its own continued existence.

## 3. The Intuitive Test for Autonomy

Di Paolo & Thompson propose an observational procedure:

1. Observe and record processes of interest
2. Intervene in individual processes and note effects on others
3. Look for processes that are **mutually enabling** — each depends on the others
4. Verify that these processes are **precarious** — if any stops, the network degrades
5. If you find such a network, you have identified an autonomous system

## 4. From Autopoiesis to Autonomy

Autopoiesis (Maturana & Varela 1980) describes a specific form of autonomy in the molecular domain — self-production of physical components.

Autonomy generalizes this: the same organizational logic (operational closure + precariousness) can appear in:
- The immune system
- The nervous system
- Communication networks
- Potentially, computational systems

The key: autonomy is about the RELATIONS between processes, not about the specific substrate.

## 5. Sense-Making

An autonomous system generates its own norms — what counts as maintaining or threatening its viability. This capacity is **sense-making**: the autonomous generation of significance.

The sugar gradient example: sucrose molecules are not intrinsically "food." They become food only through the bacterium's metabolic organization, which makes certain chemicals relevant to its self-maintenance. The bacterium *makes sense* of its environment through its own organizational norms.

This grounds intentionality (aboutness) and normativity (good/bad) in biology rather than in mental representations.

## 6. Structural Coupling and Natural Drift

The autonomous system's structure continuously changes through interaction with its environment (structural coupling), but its organization (identity) remains invariant. This produces a **natural drift** — not directed adaptation toward optimality, but historical co-evolution of system and environment that maintains viability.

## 7. Why This Matters for Cognitive Science

### Against Functionalism
Functionalism identifies cognitive systems by convention (observer-imposed boundaries). Enactivism identifies them by the system's own self-individuation. This is not a minor methodological difference — it determines what counts as a cognitive system and what constitutes an explanation of cognition.

### Against Representationalism
If cognition is grounded in autonomy and sense-making, then:
- The system does not REPRESENT a pre-given world; it ENACTS domains of significance
- Information is not RECEIVED from the environment; it is GENERATED by the system's own evaluative processes
- Meaning is not COMPUTED from symbols; it is CONSTITUTED by the system's relationship to its own viability

## 8. Relation to Our Cybernetics-Agents Research

### The autonomy criterion for agents
Current AI agents fail the autonomy test: they are not operationally closed (their components are externally maintained), not precarious (they don't degrade without active self-maintenance), and don't self-individuate. This gives concrete criteria for what a truly autonomous artificial agent would need.

### Sense-making vs. reward
RL agents receive externally defined reward signals. Autonomous agents would generate their own evaluation criteria from their operational requirements. This is a fundamentally different architecture.

### Connection to Beer's VSM
Beer's System 5 (identity/closure) maps to organizational closure. The VSM as a whole describes a system that is operationally closed (the five systems form a self-sustaining management network) and precarious (if any system fails, viability is threatened). The VSM IS an autonomous system in Di Paolo & Thompson's sense.
