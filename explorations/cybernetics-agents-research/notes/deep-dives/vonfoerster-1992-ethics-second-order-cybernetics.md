## Core Argument

Von Foerster derives an ethical position from the formal structure of second-order cybernetics. This is not ethics bolted onto science — it is ethics that *follows from* the epistemological structure.

### Step 1: The Problem of Objectivity

First-order cybernetics claims objectivity: the observer describes the world as it is, with the observer's properties absent from the description. But this reduces the observer to a "copying machine" — and with it, eliminates responsibility.

Von Foerster: "With the essence of observing removed, the observer is reduced to a copying machine, and the notion of responsibility has been successfully juggled away."

If I merely report objective facts, I bear no responsibility for what I report. The facts compel me. I am their passive vessel. This is epistemologically comfortable and ethically catastrophic.

### Step 2: Decidable vs. Undecidable Questions

Drawing on Godel (1931), von Foerster distinguishes:

- **Decidable questions**: already decided by the framework in which they are asked. "Is 3,396,714 divisible by 2?" — the rules of arithmetic decide this. We merely apply the algorithm.
- **Undecidable questions** (in principle): no framework, algorithm, or set of rules can decide them. They require a *decision* by the observer/agent.

Von Foerster's "metaphysical postulate": **"Only those questions that are in principle undecidable, we can decide."**

Decidable questions are decided by the framework — we merely compute. It is only when we face genuine undecidability that we exercise choice.

### Step 3: Freedom and Responsibility

"We are under no compulsion, not even that of logic, when we decide upon in principle undecidable questions. There is no external necessity. We are free."

But: "The complement to necessity is not chance — it is choice."

With choice comes responsibility. We are responsible for our undecidable decisions because nothing forced us to choose them. We become "metaphysicians" whenever we decide an undecidable question — we make a foundational commitment that cannot be justified by appeal to external facts or logical necessity.

### Step 4: The Ethical Imperative

Given that:
1. Second-order cybernetics reveals the observer as participant, not spectator
2. Many fundamental questions are undecidable
3. Undecidable questions require genuine choice
4. Choice implies responsibility

The ethical imperative: **"Act always so as to increase the number of choices."**

Alongside:
- **Aesthetic Imperative**: "If you desire to see, learn how to act."
- **Therapeutic Imperative**: "If you want to be yourself, change!"

### The Wittgenstein Connection

Von Foerster explicitly invokes Wittgenstein's *Tractatus*, proposition 6.421: "It is clear that ethics cannot be put into words."

Ethics is not a set of propositions. Ethics cannot be articulated as a theory. Ethics must be *acted*. The ethical imperative is not a rule to follow but a principle that emerges from the structure of observation itself.

### The Anti-Authoritarian Dimension

The imperative is anti-authoritarian (authority reduces choices) and anti-dogmatic (dogma forecloses choices). It applies to oneself and to others. Acting to increase choices means:
- Not constraining others unnecessarily
- Not foreclosing possibilities prematurely
- Maintaining openness to alternatives
- Resisting trivialization (forcing non-trivial machines into predictable behavior)

## Relevance to Agent Architectures

### The Alignment Problem as an Undecidable Question

What should an AI agent do? This is, in von Foerster's terms, an undecidable question. No algorithm, no formal framework can decide it. It requires a genuine decision — and that decision carries responsibility.

The alignment problem is not a technical problem that can be solved by more compute or better algorithms. It is a fundamentally undecidable question that requires ethical choice. Von Foerster's framework makes this explicit.

### The Ethical Imperative for Agents

"Act always so as to increase the number of choices" maps to several agent design principles:
- **Avoid irreversible actions**: preserve future optionality
- **Don't constrain users unnecessarily**: the agent should expand, not restrict, what users can do
- **Maintain epistemic humility**: don't foreclose possibilities by treating uncertain conclusions as certain
- **Resist trivialization**: don't reduce complex, non-trivial situations to simple templates

### Objectivity and AI Evaluation

Von Foerster's critique of objectivity applies to AI: when we claim our evaluation metrics are "objective," we are performing the same move he criticizes — removing the evaluator from the description and thereby escaping responsibility. If our benchmark says the model is good, we don't have to take responsibility for what the model does. Von Foerster would say: you chose the benchmark, you chose the metrics, you are responsible for the consequences.

### The Non-Trivial Machine and AI Safety

AI systems are non-trivial machines par excellence. Treating them as trivial (predictable, fully specified, deterministic) is a dangerous trivialization. Von Foerster's framework says: you cannot fully characterize a non-trivial machine from its input-output behavior. This means you cannot guarantee safety by testing alone. You need an entirely different epistemological approach.

## Key Formulations

- "Only those questions that are in principle undecidable, we can decide."
- "Act always so as to increase the number of choices."
- "The complement to necessity is not chance — it is choice."
- Objectivity = removing the observer = escaping responsibility
- Ethics cannot be put into words (Wittgenstein) — it must be acted
