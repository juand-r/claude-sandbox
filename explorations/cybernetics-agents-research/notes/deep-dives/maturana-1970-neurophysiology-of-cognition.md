# Neurophysiology of Cognition (1969/1970)

## Citation
Maturana, H.R. (1970). "The Neurophysiology of Cognition." In Garvin, P. (ed.), *Cognition: A Multiple View*, pp. 3-23. Spartan Books, New York.

## Availability
- PDF at reflexus.org: https://reflexus.org/wp-content/uploads/Maturana-H.-R.-1970-Neurophysiology-of-cognition-536.pdf (image-based PDF)
- CEPA entry: https://cepa.info/536

## Context
Presented at a symposium section "Cognition and the Organism" while Maturana was visiting the University of Illinois (1968-1969). The talk was delivered in 1969; the edited volume appeared in 1970. This paper is a companion to the larger *Biology of Cognition* BCL report, sharing the same period and intellectual context but aimed at a different audience.

## Relationship to Biology of Cognition
This paper is shorter and more focused on the neurophysiological evidence for Maturana's claims. Where *Biology of Cognition* develops the full theoretical framework (circular organization, cognitive domain, the observer), *Neurophysiology of Cognition* grounds those claims more directly in experimental neuroscience -- particularly Maturana's work on vision in frogs and pigeons.

## Key Claims

### The Nervous System as a Closed Network
The nervous system is a closed network of interacting neurons. Changes in neuronal activity always lead to other changes in neuronal activity. The nervous system does not "receive" information from the environment -- perturbations from the environment trigger structural changes that are determined by the nervous system's own organization, not by the perturbation.

### Color Vision Evidence
Maturana's research on color vision in pigeons demonstrated that color is not a mapping of wavelength to neural response. Instead, color is generated by internal neuronal relations. The same wavelength can produce different color experiences depending on context (color constancy), and different wavelengths can produce the same color experience. Color is a product of the nervous system's organization, not a representation of physical light.

### Perception vs. Illusion
A key consequence: the nervous system cannot distinguish between perception and illusion at the level of its own operations. Both are patterns of neuronal activity. The distinction "perception" vs. "illusion" is made by an observer, not by the nervous system itself.

### The Observer's Role
The observer is a neurophysiological system embedded in a medium. The observer cannot stand outside the process of cognition to evaluate it objectively. Any description of cognition is itself a cognitive act, performed by a cognitively embedded observer.

## Significance
This paper is a precursor to both autopoiesis theory and the radical constructivist epistemology that Maturana later developed. Its importance is in grounding the theoretical claims of *Biology of Cognition* in specific neurophysiological evidence, particularly from vision research.

## Relevance to Agent Architectures
- The "closed network" concept maps to transformer architectures where internal attention patterns determine responses, not raw inputs
- The perception/illusion indistinguishability maps to the problem of hallucination in LLMs -- the model cannot internally distinguish between faithful and confabulated outputs
- The observer-dependence of evaluation maps to the challenge of benchmarking AI systems: evaluation is always relative to an evaluator's framework
