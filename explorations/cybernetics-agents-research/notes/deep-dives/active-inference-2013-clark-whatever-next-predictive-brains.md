# Clark (2013) — Whatever Next? Predictive Brains, Situated Agents

**URL:** https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/behavioral-and-brain-sciences/article/whatever-next-predictive-brains-situated-agents-and-the-future-of-cognitive-science/33542C736E17E3D1D44E8D03BE5F4CD9

## Summary

This is the landmark target article that brought predictive processing (PP) to a
broad cognitive science audience. Clark synthesizes work on predictive coding,
Bayesian brain theories, and the Free Energy Principle into a comprehensive
framework, arguing it offers "the best clue yet to the shape of a unified science
of mind and action."

## Core Thesis: The Brain as a Prediction Machine

Brains are "essentially prediction machines — bundles of cells that support
perception and action by constantly attempting to match incoming sensory inputs
with top-down expectations or predictions."

The brain does not passively react to stimuli. It actively generates predictions
about what sensory input it *should* receive, based on its generative model. Only
the prediction errors — mismatches between prediction and input — propagate up
the hierarchy.

## Key Arguments

### 1. Hierarchical Generative Models

The brain implements a hierarchical generative model where:
- Higher levels predict the inputs to lower levels
- Errors at lower levels cause higher-level models to adapt
- The result: the brain encodes rich information about causal structure

This is bidirectional: top-down predictions meet bottom-up prediction errors,
and the brain adjusts to minimize discrepancy at every level.

### 2. Perception as Theory-Laden Inference

Perception is "theory-laden" — what we perceive depends on the priors the brain
brings to bear. Following Helmholtz, perception is probabilistic, knowledge-driven
inference. We never see "raw data" — only the brain's best hypothesis about what
caused the sensory signal.

### 3. Unification of Perception and Action

Prediction error can be minimized two ways:
1. Update the model (perception)
2. Act to change the input (action)

This unifies perception and action under a single imperative. Clark draws heavily
on Friston's active inference formulation here.

### 4. Precision Weighting and Attention

Not all prediction errors are treated equally. The brain assigns **precision**
(inverse variance) to different error signals, effectively weighting how much
each error matters. High precision = this error signal is reliable, pay attention
to it. Low precision = this is noisy, ignore it.

Attention, on this account, is precision optimization — adjusting the gain on
prediction error signals. This is a formal account of what cybernetics would
call "gain control in the feedback loop."

### 5. The Action-Oriented Predictive Processing Model

Clark goes beyond passive inference to argue for an "action-oriented" version
where the brain's models are fundamentally about enabling interaction with the
environment, not about building accurate internal representations for their own
sake. This aligns with the cybernetic/enactive tradition.

## Cybernetic Connections (Implicit)

Clark does not frame the paper explicitly as cybernetics, but the connections
are pervasive:

1. **Error correction:** The entire framework is built on error minimization,
   which Ashby identified as the fundamental operation of the brain.

2. **Feedback loops:** The hierarchical predictive processing architecture is
   essentially a multi-level negative feedback system.

3. **Control through prediction:** The brain acts to maintain predicted states —
   this is control theory.

4. **Reference to Powers:** Clark explicitly cites Powers' Perceptual Control
   Theory and quotes its tagline: "behavior is the control of perception."

5. **Circular causation:** The perception-action loop is a circular causal
   system in the cybernetic sense.

## What This Paper Adds

Clark's main contribution is **accessibility and synthesis**. He:
1. Made Friston's dense mathematics comprehensible to philosophers and psychologists
2. Connected PP/FEP to embodied cognition and enactivism
3. Raised critical challenges (the "dark room problem," overfitting, etc.)
4. Positioned PP as a potential "grand unified theory" of cognition

## The Dark Room Problem

Clark raises the objection: if the brain just minimizes surprise, why don't we
all sit in dark rooms (zero surprise)? The answer: the generative model encodes
prior expectations about being a certain kind of agent in a certain kind of
environment. A human expects to eat, move, explore. Sitting in a dark room
violates those priors and generates massive prediction error.

This is homologous to Ashby's essential variables: the system must keep itself
within its characteristic states, which include metabolic and behavioral states
that require environmental engagement.

## Significance for Agent Design

Clark's paper suggests agents should:
1. Be built around hierarchical predictive models
2. Unify perception and action under a single objective
3. Use precision weighting as an attention mechanism
4. Encode goals as prior expectations about sensory states
5. Be action-oriented: models serve interaction, not representation
