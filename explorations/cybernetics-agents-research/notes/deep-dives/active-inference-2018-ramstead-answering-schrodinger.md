# Ramstead et al. (2018) — Answering Schrödinger's Question

**Paper:** Ramstead, M.J.D., Badcock, P.B., & Friston, K.J. (2018). "Answering
Schrödinger's question: A free-energy formulation." *Physics of Life Reviews*, 24, 1-16.
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29029962/

---

## Summary

This paper addresses Schrödinger's question — "What is life?" — using the Free Energy
Principle. It proposes a meta-theoretical ontology called **variational neuroethology**
that integrates the FEP with Tinbergen's four research questions to explain biological
systems across spatial and temporal scales.

## Schrödinger's Question Reformulated

Erwin Schrödinger noted that living cells appear to resist the second law of
thermodynamics by restricting the entropy of their physiological states. This is
Claude Bernard's homeostasis reformulated in physical terms.

The FEP provides a formal answer: living systems minimize variational free energy,
which bounds surprise, which bounds entropy. Systems that persist must occupy a
limited repertoire of states — they resist entropic dissolution through active
inference.

## Variational Neuroethology

The paper's main contribution is a framework called "variational neuroethology" that
combines:

1. **FEP** (the mathematical framework)
2. **Tinbergen's four questions** (the biological research program):
   - Mechanism (how does it work?)
   - Ontogeny (how does it develop?)
   - Phylogeny (how did it evolve?)
   - Function (what is it for?)

The claim: FEP provides a single mathematical framework that can address all four
questions simultaneously, across spatial and temporal scales.

## Multi-Scale Formulation

The paper describes how the FEP applies at multiple scales:

- **Molecular/cellular:** Cells maintain Markov blankets (membranes), minimize free
  energy through metabolic regulation
- **Neural/cognitive:** Brains perform predictive inference, minimizing prediction
  error through perception and action
- **Social/cultural:** Social groups have collective Markov blankets, minimize
  collective free energy through cultural norms and institutions

Each scale has its own characteristic timescale and generative model, but the
principle is the same.

## Application to Homo sapiens

The paper exemplifies the framework by applying it to humans across all scales:
- Neural predictive processing (milliseconds)
- Embodied cognition (seconds-minutes)
- Developmental niche construction (years)
- Cultural evolution (generations)

## Cybernetic Connections

### Homeostasis Formalized

The paper explicitly connects Schrödinger's question to Bernard's homeostasis and
Ashby's formalization: living systems keep essential variables within viable bounds.
FEP mathematizes this as maintaining low free energy = low surprise = staying within
characteristic states.

### Self-Organization Across Scales

The hierarchical Markov blanket structure parallels Beer's Viable System Model:
systems within systems, each maintaining its own viability, composing into larger
viable systems.

## Commentary Papers (Important)

The paper generated extensive peer commentary, including:

- **Bitbol & Gallagher:** "The free energy principle and autopoiesis" — exploring
  the connection between FEP and Maturana/Varela's autopoiesis
- **Kirchhoff:** "Hierarchical Markov blankets and adaptive active inference"
- **Pezzulo & Levin:** "Embodying Markov blankets"
- **Tozzi & Peters:** "Critique of pure free energy principle" — questioning the
  formal validity
- **Keijzer:** Arguing that free energy minimization may be necessary but not
  sufficient for characterizing life's goals

## Significance for Agent Design

1. Multi-scale agent architectures should have corresponding multi-scale generative
   models
2. Agents can be understood as minimizing free energy at multiple temporal scales
   simultaneously
3. Cultural/collective agent behavior can be formalized using the same framework
   as individual behavior
4. The framework provides a systematic research methodology for understanding and
   designing agents
