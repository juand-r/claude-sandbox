# God and Golem, Inc. (1964)

**Author:** Norbert Wiener
**Published:** MIT Press, 1964
**Award:** U.S. National Book Award (Science, Philosophy and Religion)
**Based on:** Lectures at Yale (1962) and Colloques Philosophiques
             Internationaux de Royaumont (1962)
**Access:** Open access via MIT Press;
           also: https://monoskop.org/images/1/1f/Wiener_Norbert_God_and_Golem_A_Comment_on_Certain_Points_where_Cybernetics_Impinges_on_Religion.pdf
           and: https://ia601306.us.archive.org/21/items/wiener-god-and-golem/WIENER%20god%20and%20golem.pdf

## Overview

Wiener's final book, published the year of his death (1964). More a series
of essays than a monograph. Less mathematically rigorous than his earlier
work, but philosophically provocative.

## Three Central Arguments

### 1. Machines That Learn

A computer exists that can be programmed to play checkers AND can learn
from its past experience to improve its own game. For a time, the machine
beat its inventor. This refutes the claim that machines can only do what
they are explicitly programmed to do.

The learning machine produces outputs that were not explicitly specified
by the programmer. This is creativity-by-another-name, and it challenges
the boundary between tool and agent.

### 2. Self-Reproducing Machines

Man has made machines "very well able to make other machines in their
own image" — and these are *operative* images, not merely pictorial.
Wiener draws the analogy: "Can we then say: God is to Golem as man is
to Machines?"

The Golem in Jewish legend is an embryo Adam — shapeless, not fully
created, hence a monster/automaton. The machines we create are our golems.

### 3. The Ethical Relationship Between Man and Machine

"Render unto man the things which are man's and unto the computer the
things which are the computer's." Wiener considers systems involving
elements of both man and machine, and the ethical questions that arise
when the boundary blurs.

## Secondary Concerns

- Sensory feedback in artificial limbs (prosthetics as human-machine systems)
- Human responsibility in relation to technology
- Limits of machine game-playing
- Critique of econometrics and mathematical economics as truly "scientific"
- Darwinism, Marxism, the Cold War, rigidity of ideological thinking

## The Golem as Central Metaphor

Across his writings, Wiener uses the Golem to represent dangers inherent
in machine learning. He depicts the creator-machine relationship as a
learning game where the machine may outgrow the creator's intentions.

In 1964: "the relationship between the cybernetic machine and man is
similar to the relationship between man and his creator."

## Relevance to AI Agent Architectures

- **Learning beyond programming** — the checkers example is a direct
  precursor to AlphaGo, reinforcement learning agents that exceed
  human performance
- **Self-reproduction** — AutoGPT-style agents that spawn sub-agents,
  self-modifying code, recursive self-improvement
- **The Golem problem** — the creation outgrowing its creator's control.
  This is precisely the modern AI alignment problem.
- **Man-machine hybrid systems** — RLHF, human-in-the-loop systems,
  centaur approaches to AI
- **Game-playing as a learning paradigm** — Wiener saw this clearly;
  games remain a primary testbed for AI (chess, Go, StarCraft, Diplomacy)
- **Cooperative inverse RL** — recent work deals explicitly with
  problems Wiener laid out: learning human values through interaction
