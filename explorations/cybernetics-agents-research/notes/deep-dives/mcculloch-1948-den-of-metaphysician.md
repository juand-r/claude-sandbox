# McCulloch — "Through the Den of the Metaphysician" (1948)

**Author:** Warren S. McCulloch
**Delivered:** Lecture to the Philosophical Club, University of Virginia, March 23, 1948
**First published:** French translation in *Thales*, Vol. 7, pp. 37-49, 1951
**English publication:** British Journal for the Philosophy of Science, Vol. V, No. 17, pp. 18-31, May 1954
**Reprinted:** *Embodiments of Mind* (MIT Press, 1965), pp. 142-157
**Full text:** Behind paywall (Oxford Academic / U. Chicago Press)

## The Title

Borrowed from James Clerk Maxwell, who warned physicists to stay out of "the den of the metaphysician." McCulloch's provocation: he would walk straight in, because he had mathematical tools Maxwell lacked — specifically, the McCulloch-Pitts neural net formalism.

## The Argument

### The Mind-Body Problem
McCulloch identified the physicist's dilemma:
- The physicist studies "body" (matter)
- But the physicist is also a person who thinks — i.e., has a "mind"
- If the physicist refuses to include himself in his account of matter, he remains a physicist
- If he accepts the challenge, he becomes a **metaphysician**

McCulloch's claim: cybernetics provides the tools to resolve this dilemma. Neural net theory shows how mental operations (logic, perception, value) can be described as physical processes in neural circuits. There is no gap between mind and body — only an explanatory gap that the right mathematics can close.

### Experimental Epistemology
McCulloch proposed that the Kantian synthetic a priori — the structures that organize experience before experience — can be investigated empirically through the study of neural circuits. The "forms of intuition" that Kant said were given a priori are, for McCulloch, properties of the neural architecture.

This is a radical empiricist move: what philosophy said was beyond investigation becomes a subject for experimental science.

### The Cybernetic Resolution
The distinction between "mind" and "body" dissolves when you have a formal theory of computation in neural matter. The McCulloch-Pitts neuron is both physical (a neuron in a brain) and logical (a Boolean operator). There is no dualism — only different levels of description of the same system.

## Why This Matters

### For AI Philosophy
McCulloch's argument against mind-body dualism is the philosophical foundation for strong AI. If mental operations are computational operations, and if computation is substrate-independent (as shown by the equivalence of M-P nets and Turing machines), then there is no principled reason machines cannot think.

### For Cybernetics
This paper establishes cybernetics as not just an engineering discipline but a philosophical program — one that claims to dissolve ancient metaphysical problems through formal methods.

### Prescience
McCulloch stood before brain scientists at the 1948 Hixon Symposium — with von Neumann in the audience — and decided to talk metaphysics. He was claiming territory: the questions that philosophy had monopolized for centuries were now tractable through cybernetics and neural net theory. This was either supreme confidence or supreme arrogance. History suggests the former.
