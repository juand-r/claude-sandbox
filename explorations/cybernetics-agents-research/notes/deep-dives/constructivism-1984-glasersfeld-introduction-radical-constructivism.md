# Von Glasersfeld (1984) — An Introduction to Radical Constructivism

**Full Citation:** Von Glasersfeld, E. (1984). "An Introduction to Radical Constructivism." In P. Watzlawick (Ed.), *The Invented Reality* (pp. 17–40). New York: Norton. Originally published in German in *Die Erfundene Wirklichkeit* (Munich: Piper, 1981).

**Source:** Full text PDF from Nova Southeastern University; full text extracted via pdftotext.

## 1. The Central Thesis

Knowledge is NOT a picture or representation of an independent, objective reality. Knowledge is a key that unlocks possible paths — it "fits" reality the way a key fits a lock, not the way a portrait matches a face.

This distinction between **match** and **fit** is the core of the paper:

- **Match** (the realist view): Knowledge corresponds to reality like paint matches a wall color — structural homomorphism, equivalence of relations. Knowledge "is of" the world.
- **Fit** (the constructivist view): Knowledge works like a key that opens a lock. Many differently-shaped keys can open the same lock. The fit describes a capacity of the key, not a property of the lock.

## 2. The Evolutionary Analogy

Von Glasersfeld draws a precise analogy to Darwin's theory of evolution:

- The environment does not *cause* survival. It places **constraints** and eliminates what is not viable.
- Darwin's theory is based on the **principle of constraints**, not cause and effect (citing Bateson).
- The environment is responsible for *extinction*, never for *survival*.
- What survives proves only that it is viable under the constraints encountered — nothing more.

Applied to knowledge:
- The experiential world places constraints on cognitive structures.
- Ideas, theories, and "laws of nature" either hold up to experience or they do not.
- A viable cognitive structure proves only that it works under the circumstances encountered — it tells us nothing about how many other ways there might be, or about reality "in itself."

**Key formulation:** "The 'real' world manifests itself exclusively where our constructions break down. Moreover, we can describe and explain these breakdowns only in the very concepts that we have used to build the failing structures."

## 3. The Philosophical Lineage

### Skepticism (Xenophanes, Pyrrho, Sextus Empiricus)
- The unanswerable question: we can check perceptions only by means of other perceptions, never against the thing-before-perception.
- Sextus's apple: appears smooth, scented, sweet, yellow — but is the real apple like that?

### Kant
- Added a deeper doubt: not just sensory properties but **thinghood** itself belongs to the phenomenal realm.
- Space and time are aspects of our way of experiencing, not of reality.
- "Nature is the collective conception of all objects of experience."
- The a priori describes the organism's experiential capability — the framework within which it operates.

### Vico (1710) — The First True Constructivist
- "Verum ipsum factum" — the truth is the same as the made.
- The only way of "knowing" a thing is to have made it.
- God knows his creation; we can know only what we ourselves construct.
- Crucially: knowledge is constrained not by impossible correspondence with "objective" reality, but by **consistency with prior constructions**. Whatever has been done limits what can be done in the future.

### Piaget
- "Intelligence organizes the world by organizing itself."
- Adaptation is the key process, but must NOT be read as correspondence or homomorphism.
- **Assimilation:** an experience that contains elements a, b, c is treated as "the same" as prior a-b-c experiences, as long as additional elements are not taken into account.
- **Accommodation:** when an object that manifests a, b, c behaves unexpectedly, the organism examines other properties, leading to discrimination.
- Both processes involve **fit**, not match.

## 4. Key Conceptual Apparatus

### Equivalence vs. Individual Identity
Two experiential items can be compared in two fundamentally different ways:
1. **Equivalence:** Two separate objects judged "the same" with respect to examined properties.
2. **Individual identity:** Two experiences treated as experiences of one and the same persisting object.

This distinction is NOT given a priori but must be constructed (Piaget shows children build it in the first two years). The choice between equivalence and identity is a **decision** of the experiencing subject, not a property of reality.

### Construction of Regularity
- Regularity and constancy presuppose repeated experience.
- Repetition requires comparison yielding a judgment of sameness.
- Sameness is always **relative** — relative to which properties are checked.
- Given sufficiently rich raw material, an assimilating consciousness can construct regularities **even in a chaotic world**.

### The Bricklayer Metaphor
A bricklayer who builds only with bricks discovers that arches are needed above openings. This is a constraint experienced from the inside — from the "brick perspective." We never see the constraints of the world itself; we experience only the constraints of our building materials.

## 5. Implications for What "Model" Means

This is the critical implication for agent architectures:

### Traditional View (Realist)
A model is an internal representation that corresponds to (matches) external reality. The better the model, the more accurately it mirrors the world. Success comes from representational fidelity.

### Constructivist View
A model is a cognitive structure that **fits** the constraints encountered in the experiential world. Multiple radically different models can be equally viable — they are different keys that open the same lock. The model tells us about the organism's way of operating, not about reality "in itself."

**Consequences for agents:**
1. An agent's "world model" does not represent the world. It represents the agent's way of organizing its experience to achieve its goals.
2. Two agents with different architectures can have radically different but equally viable "world models" of the same environment.
3. A model's value is measured by its **viability** — does it enable the agent to achieve its goals? — not by its correspondence to some ground truth.
4. When a model fails (construction breaks down), the failure itself is experienced only through the categories of the model.
5. The constraints that reality imposes are known only negatively — through what doesn't work.

## 6. The Self-Referential Closure

Von Glasersfeld closes the paper with a self-referential move: "Radical constructivism itself must not be interpreted as a picture or description of any absolute reality but as a possible model of knowing and the acquisition of knowledge in cognitive organisms."

The theory applies to itself. It does not claim to be TRUE in the correspondence sense. It claims to be VIABLE — a way of thinking about knowledge that is consistent and useful.

## 7. Relation to Other Papers in This Deep-Dive

- **Von Foerster's "On Constructing a Reality"** provides the neurophysiological grounding for constructivism — showing HOW the nervous system constructs reality (encoding is quantity-only, no quality; internal environment 100,000x more receptive than external).
- **Maturana's 1988 paper** extends this to the biological observer, distinguishing objectivity-in-parentheses from objectivity-without.
- **Spencer-Brown / Varela** provide the formal calculus for the act of distinction that is the primitive operation of construction.
- **Kauffman's eigenforms** formalize the stable outcomes (fixed points) of recursive construction processes — the "objects" that constructivism says we construct.

## 8. Critical Assessment

**Strengths:**
- Logically tight: the evolutionary analogy is precise and well-deployed.
- The match/fit distinction is genuinely clarifying.
- Avoids solipsism: constraints from "reality" are real (they cause constructions to break down), we just can't describe them independently.

**Weaknesses:**
- The theory is almost unfalsifiable: any failure of a model is just "reality manifesting through constraints," which is exactly what the theory predicts.
- The relationship between individual and shared knowledge (intersubjectivity) is underdeveloped. How do multiple organisms arrive at compatible constructions? Von Glasersfeld addresses this only briefly through Piaget.
- The evolutionary analogy, while illuminating, may be stretched: in biology, fitness is binary (survive or die); in epistemology, viability admits degrees.
