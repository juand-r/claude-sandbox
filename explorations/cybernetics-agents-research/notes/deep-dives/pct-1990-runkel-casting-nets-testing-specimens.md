## Overview
A methodological landmark. Runkel argues that psychology uses two fundamentally different methods, and has been using the dominant one for the wrong purpose.

## The Two Grand Methods

### 1. Casting Nets (Method of Relative Frequency)
- Collects data from **populations** (samples of many individuals).
- Uses statistical analysis (means, correlations, ANOVAs, regressions).
- Looks for patterns across individuals.
- **What it can do:** Estimate behavioral trends in a population. Answer questions like "what percentage of people do X?" or "is there a correlation between A and B across a population?"
- **What it CANNOT do:** Explain how any individual organism works. Tell you the causal mechanism within a single system.

### 2. Testing Specimens (Method of Specimens)
- Studies **individual organisms** in depth.
- Examines the internal workings of a single system.
- Uses experimental manipulation, modeling, and the TCV.
- **What it can do:** Discover how a specific mechanism works. Answer questions like "what is this organism controlling?" or "what is the internal structure of this control system?"
- **What it CANNOT do:** Generalize to populations (without separately casting nets).

### The Fundamental Error
Runkel's central argument: most social scientists have been using the **method of relative frequency** (casting nets) for the **wrong purpose** — to discover how the human animal functions as a species.

Population statistics can tell you that 70% of people turn right when they enter a store. They cannot tell you WHY any individual turns right. They cannot tell you the mechanism. They cannot tell you what the individual is controlling.

To learn how internal workings of a species enable it to do what it does, the method of specimens must be employed.

### PCT as the Framework for Testing Specimens
Runkel then introduces PCT as the theoretical framework necessary for properly testing specimens. The key elements:
1. **Circular causation** — the organism and environment are locked in a feedback loop; you cannot study one direction of causation in isolation.
2. **The controlled variable** — the fundamental unit of individual behavior is a controlled perception, discoverable via the TCV.
3. **Individual modeling** — build a PCT model of an individual, compare its behavior to the real individual, iterate until the model matches.

## Methodological Implications

### For Psychology
Standard psychology experiments (average across subjects, look for significant effects of IVs on DVs) are casting nets. They can tell you about population trends but not about individual mechanisms. For mechanism discovery, you need to test specimens: study individuals, apply the TCV, build models.

### For Agent Research
This has a direct parallel in AI/ML research:
- **Casting nets:** Benchmark evaluations across many models. Average accuracy across many tasks. Population-level statistics.
- **Testing specimens:** Deep analysis of individual agent behavior. What is *this* agent controlling? What happens when you disturb specific perceptual variables? Does the agent resist the disturbance?

Most current AI evaluation is "casting nets" — aggregate performance metrics. Runkel's argument implies that understanding agent behavior requires "testing specimens" — studying individual agents in depth, applying disturbances, and discovering their controlled variables.

### The TCV as Agent Evaluation Method
Instead of evaluating agents by their output quality (casting nets), evaluate them by identifying their controlled variables (testing specimens):
1. Hypothesize: "This agent controls perceived helpfulness."
2. Disturb: Apply inputs designed to make unhelpful responses easier.
3. Observe: Does the agent resist the disturbance and maintain helpfulness?
4. If yes: helpfulness is a controlled variable.
5. Quantify: How much disturbance can the agent resist before control breaks down? (This is the gain of the loop.)

This would give a fundamentally different picture of agent behavior than benchmark scores do.

## Key Quote (paraphrased)
The mainstream research tools of psychology are not sufficient to explain individual behavior and its causes. Following this review, the reader is familiarized with the concept of causation and with PCT, the framework necessary for a change in perspective on human behavior.
