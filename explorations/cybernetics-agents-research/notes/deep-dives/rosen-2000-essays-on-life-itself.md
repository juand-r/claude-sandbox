# Rosen (2000) — Essays on Life Itself

**Citation:** Rosen, R. (2000). *Essays on Life Itself*. Columbia University Press, New York. ISBN 978-0-231-10511-8.
**Access:** Available on JSTOR, Amazon, Columbia UP. Some chapters accessible.

## Summary

A posthumous collection of 22 essays, mostly written after *Life Itself* (1991). Published after Rosen's death in December 1998. Serves as companion to both *Life Itself* and *Anticipatory Systems*.

## Structure

The essays are organized into five parts:

### Part I: On Biology and Physics
- Opens with "The Schrodinger Question, What Is Life? Fifty-Five Years Later"
- Explores biological challenges to contemporary physics paradigms
- Examines limits of scientific knowability (from a 1994 Santa Fe Institute workshop)
- Applies Godel's Incompleteness results to the sciences

### Part II: On Biology and the Mind
- The brain as both organism and seat of reasoning — a paradox
- Challenges the notion that AI can truly replicate life

### Part III: On Genericity
- The concept of "generic" in biology vs. physics

### Part IV: Similarity and Dissimilarity in Biology
- The quandary of side effects — when science confronts unpredicted outgrowths

### Part V: On Biology and Technology
- Technology through the lens of relational biology

## Key Themes

1. **The unpredictable in science:** An enigma linking all essays — how can science explain the unpredictable?

2. **Anti-reductionism crystallized:** Breaking from constraints of reductionist reasoning which maintains that simple empirical mechanisms are the basis of all life.

3. **Godel and biology:** The impacts of Godel's Incompleteness results on biology — if formal systems have inherent limitations, what does that mean for formal models of life?

4. **Category theory and biology — final acceptance:** In this final work, Rosen accepted the need for representing organisms in terms of categories with structure that entail biological functions, both metabolic and repair. This was a refinement of his earlier, sometimes contested, formal arguments.

## Reception

"These 22 essays are intended for serious thinkers, as they are provocative and often iconoclastic. There are many new ideas, daring perspectives, and challenging modes of interpretation of concepts that readers may have mistakenly thought they understood." — Bruce J. West, *The Quarterly Review of Biology*

## Relevance to Agent Architectures

The essays in Part V (Biology and Technology) and Part II (Biology and the Mind) are most relevant. Rosen's argument that AI cannot truly replicate life is worth engaging with seriously — not to accept it uncritically, but to understand *what specifically* he thinks is missing and whether modern agent architectures address any of his concerns.

The Godel connection is also interesting: if there are inherent limits to what formal systems can prove about themselves, and agents are formal systems, then there may be inherent limits to agent self-knowledge and self-repair.
