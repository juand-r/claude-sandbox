# A Scientist Rebels (1947)

**Author:** Norbert Wiener
**Published:** The Atlantic Monthly, Vol. 179, No. 1, p. 46 (January 1947)
**Reprinted:** Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists, February 1947, p. 31
**Also in:** Pesi Masani (ed.), Norbert Wiener, Collected Works with Commentaries,
             Vol. IV, Cambridge, MIT Press, 1985, p. 748
**Original MS:** MIT Archives, Norbert Wiener papers, folder 573
**Access:** Behind Atlantic Monthly paywall; one page long

## Context

This is an open letter, only one page long, written in response to a letter from
George E. Forsythe of Boeing Aircraft Company's Physical Research Unit, who had
requested technical information related to guided missiles programs. The editor's
introduction described it as a letter "addressed by one of our ranking
mathematicians to a research scientist of a great aircraft corporation."

## Core Argument

Wiener's central claim: "the bombing of Hiroshima and Nagasaki has made it clear
that to provide scientific information is not a necessarily innocent act, and may
entail the gravest consequences."

He further argued: "to disseminate information about a weapon in the present state
of our civilization is to make it practically certain that the weapon will be used."

## Significance

This letter established Wiener's reputation as a resister to military-scientific
cooperation. After the war, he refused to accept any government funding or work
on military projects. This was a deeply unpopular position during the early Cold War.

The letter represents one of the earliest and most prominent examples of a scientist
publicly refusing to contribute to weapons research on ethical grounds. It anticipates
the modern debate about responsible AI development and the ethics of dual-use research.

## Relevance to AI Agent Architectures

While not technical, this paper establishes an important precedent:
- **Dual-use concern** — technical knowledge can be weaponized in ways the creator
  did not intend or desire
- **Responsible disclosure** — anticipates modern debates about publishing dangerous
  AI capabilities research
- **The scientist's ethical obligation** — Wiener argued scientists bear responsibility
  for foreseeable uses of their work, not just intended uses
- Wiener's ethical stance directly influenced the framing of his later technical work,
  including the 1960 "Moral and Technical Consequences" paper
