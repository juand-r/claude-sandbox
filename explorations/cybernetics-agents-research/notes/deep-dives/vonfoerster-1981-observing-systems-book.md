# Von Foerster (1981) — Observing Systems (Collected Papers)

## Citation
Von Foerster, H. (1981). *Observing Systems*. The Systems Inquiry Series, Intersystems Publications, Seaside, CA. xvi+331 pp. Introduction by Francisco J. Varela. 2nd edition 1984, xviii+331 pp.

## Source Status
Borrowable copy on Internet Archive: https://archive.org/details/observingsystems0000vonf
Physical copies available via libraries and used book dealers. Out of print but widely available secondhand.

## About the Book

### The Title's Double Meaning

"Observing Systems" is deliberately ambiguous:
1. Systems that observe (observing-systems)
2. The act of observing systems (observing systems)

This ambiguity *is* the book's thesis: you cannot separate the observer from the observed. The title performs what it describes.

### Contents

The book collects von Foerster's major papers from 1960-1977. It is the predecessor to *Understanding Understanding* (Springer, 2003), which expanded and updated the collection. Known contents include:

1. "On Self-Organizing Systems and Their Environments" (1960)
2. "Bio-Logic" (1962)
3. "Sounds and Music" (1964)
4. "Computation in Neural Nets" (1967)
5. "What Is Memory that It May Have Hindsight and Foresight as well?"
6. "Molecular Ethology: An Immodest Proposal for Semantic Clarification" (1970)
7. "Notes on an Epistemology for Living Things" (1972)
8. "On Constructing a Reality" (1973)
9. "Cybernetics of Cybernetics" (1974)
10. "The Curious Behavior of Complex Systems: Lessons from Biology" (1976)
11. "Objects: Tokens for (Eigen-)Behaviors" (1976)

Plus additional papers. The book includes a bibliography of von Foerster's publications on pp. 314-318, and index terms including: eigenvalues, self-organizing systems, heterarchy, neural nets, Maturana, McCulloch, Maxwell's Demon, recursive functions.

### Varela's Introduction

Francisco Varela wrote the introduction, placing von Foerster's work in the context of the transition from first-order to second-order cybernetics. Varela himself was deeply influenced by von Foerster's framework, and his concept of autopoiesis (developed with Maturana) grew partly from conversations at the BCL.

### Relationship to *Understanding Understanding*

*Understanding Understanding* (Springer, 2003) is essentially an updated and expanded version of *Observing Systems*, with additional later essays and revised versions. The 2003 volume is more readily available and includes papers from the 1980s and 1990s not in the original.

## Relevance to Agent Research

The collected papers form a coherent intellectual program that maps onto agent architecture concerns:

| Von Foerster's Program | Agent Architecture Concern |
|---|---|
| Self-organization | Emergence of structure from learning |
| Computation in neural nets | Neural network architecture design |
| Memory and foresight | Memory in agents (context windows, RAG) |
| Constructing reality | World models in agents |
| Epistemology for living things | Epistemic constraints on agent knowledge |
| Eigenbehaviors | Stable learned representations |
| Ethics of observation | Alignment and responsibility |
| Recursive communication | Self-referential agent architectures |

The book as a whole makes the case that cognition, computation, and ethics are not separate domains but aspects of a single recursive structure. This is directly relevant to the project's thesis that agent architectures need an integrated framework, not separate modules.

## Key Formulations

- Title performs its thesis: "observing systems" = systems that observe / the act of observing systems
- Collects the major papers of second-order cybernetics in one volume
- Varela's introduction contextualizes the first-to-second-order transition
- Predecessor to *Understanding Understanding* (2003)
