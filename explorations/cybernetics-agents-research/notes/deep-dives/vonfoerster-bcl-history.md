# The Biological Computer Laboratory (BCL) — UIUC, 1958-1976

## Overview

The Biological Computer Laboratory was a research institute in the Department of Electrical Engineering at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign. Founded January 1, 1958, by Heinz von Foerster. He directed it until his retirement in 1975. The lab closed in 1976 due to loss of funding.

According to Illinois sociology professor Andrew Pickering: "During its lifetime, the BCL was certainly the most important institutional base for cybernetics in the Western world."

## Timeline

- **1948**: Von Foerster publishes *Das Gedachtnis* (memory theory, quantum-mechanical approach)
- **1949**: Arrives at UIUC; McCulloch reads *Das Gedachtnis* and invites von Foerster to the Macy Conferences; McCulloch helps secure a position in EE department
- **1949-1953**: Von Foerster participates in Macy Conferences, eventually becomes editor of proceedings alongside Teuber and Mead
- **1956-1957**: Sabbatical studying neurophysiology (Guggenheim Fellowship)
- **1958**: BCL established
- **1960**: "On Self-Organizing Systems" paper; organizes conference on "Principles of Self-Organization" at Allerton Park
- **1961**: W. Ross Ashby joins as professor; Gotthard Gunther becomes research professor
- **1962**: Dynamic signal analyzer developed (Erickson & Neill, BCL Report 3-1)
- **1965**: Many visiting researchers have come through: Pask, Gunther, Lofgren, Maturana
- **1967**: Gunther receives regular professorship
- **1969**: *The Whole University Catalog* published (counterculture student guide)
- **1970**: Maturana's *Biology of Cognition* appears as BCL Report 9.0
- **1972**: *Metagames* published (cognition through experimental games); Gunther retires
- **1974**: BCL closes due to loss of funding; *Cybernetics of Cybernetics* (BCL Report 73.38) published
- **1975**: Von Foerster retires, moves to California; Ashby had already retired
- **1976**: *Collected Works of the Biological Computer Laboratory* published (Illinois Blueprint Corp., Peoria)

## Key Personnel

### Director
- **Heinz von Foerster** (1911-2002): Founder and director. Austrian-American physicist/philosopher. Second-order cybernetics, self-organization, eigenforms, constructivism.

### Regular Faculty
- **W. Ross Ashby** (1903-1972): Joined BCL 1961. British cybernetician. *Design for a Brain*, *Introduction to Cybernetics*, Law of Requisite Variety, homeostat. One of the original Ratio Club members.
- **Gotthard Gunther** (1900-1984): Research professor 1961-1972. German philosopher. Trans-classical (polycontextural) logic — extending beyond Aristotelian two-valued logic to handle self-referential systems without paradox. Discovered by McCulloch. His work addressed subjectivity in formal models of self-organizing systems.
- **Herbert Brun** (1918-2000): Composer of experimental electronic and computer music. Connected BCL to the UIUC School of Music. Explored computation and aesthetics.

### Visiting Researchers and Close Collaborators
- **Gordon Pask** (1928-1996): British cybernetician. Visiting professor. Conversation Theory — formalized interaction as the primitive unit of cognition. Remained in permanent contact with BCL after his visit.
- **Humberto Maturana** (1928-2021): Chilean biologist. Visiting researcher. Developed autopoiesis theory partly at BCL. *Biology of Cognition* (BCL Report 9.0, 1970) was a BCL publication.
- **Francisco Varela** (1946-2001): Chilean biologist/philosopher. Frequent visitor. Co-developed autopoiesis with Maturana. Extended Spencer-Brown's calculus into a calculus of self-reference.
- **Lars Lofgren** (1925-2013): Swedish cybernetician. Visiting professor. Worked on self-referential systems and "autology" — the metalogic of self-description. Wiener Gold Medal recipient (2008).
- **Stuart Umpleby**: Student and later collaborator. Wrote key appreciations of von Foerster's work. American Society for Cybernetics president.
- **Ricardo Uribe**: Researcher. Worked on autopoiesis. Papers held at UIUC archives.
- **Murray Babcock**: Researcher. Dynamic signal analyzer work. Papers at UIUC archives.

### Regular Visitors
- **Margaret Mead** (1901-1978): Anthropologist. Macy Conference participant. Coined "cybernetics of cybernetics" jointly with von Foerster. Frequent BCL visitor.
- **Gregory Bateson** (1904-1980): Anthropologist/cybernetician. Macy Conference participant. Noted that he and Mead understood themselves as "participant observers" — a proto-second-order stance.
- **Ernst von Glasersfeld** (1917-2010): Radical constructivism.

## Research Areas

- Self-organizing systems and order from noise
- Computation in neural nets (first parallel computers)
- Cognitive memory and computation
- Bionics and bio-inspired computing
- Cybernetics and second-order cybernetics
- Pattern recognition
- Visual, auditory, and language processing
- Computational semantics and linguistics
- Polycontextural logic (Gunther)
- Autopoiesis (Maturana/Varela)
- Population dynamics (von Foerster's "Doomsday" equation)

## Funding and Decline

### Funding Sources
- U.S. Air Force (primary, 1950s-60s)
- U.S. Navy / ONR
- NASA
- Department of Health, Education and Welfare
- NIH, NSF
- Wenner-Gren Foundation
- Point Foundation

### Why BCL Closed

In the early 1970s, the Mansfield Amendment and similar policy shifts restricted military research funding to projects with direct military utility. Basic cybernetics research no longer qualified. Von Foerster was unable to find adequate civilian sponsors. Meanwhile, Ashby and Gunther had retired and left the university. Without funding and with the departure of key personnel, BCL could not sustain itself. Von Foerster retired in 1975.

## Relevance to Agent Research

The BCL was the first institution to systematically study computation as a biological/cognitive phenomenon rather than as a purely mathematical/engineering one. Its research program — understanding how biological systems compute, self-organize, and know their environments — is the direct ancestor of modern embodied cognition, enactivism, and bio-inspired AI.

The lab's interdisciplinary structure (electrical engineering, biology, philosophy, music, linguistics, logic) prefigures the current understanding that AI research requires multiple disciplinary perspectives. The BCL's decline due to funding constraints is a cautionary tale about the fragility of basic interdisciplinary research.
