# Espejo & Harnden (1989) — The Viable System Model: Interpretations and Applications

## Bibliographic Details

- **Title:** The Viable System Model: Interpretations and Applications of Stafford Beer's VSM
- **Editors:** Raúl Espejo and Roger Harnden
- **Publisher:** John Wiley & Sons, 1989
- **Pages:** 484
- **ISBN:** 978-0471922889

## Structure

Four parts:

### Part 1: Concepts
- Beer, S. — VSM's provenance, development, methodology, and pathology (reprinted from JORS 1984)
- Anderton, R. — The need for formal development of the VSM
- Waelchli, F. — The VSM and Ashby's Law
- Espejo, R. — The VSM revisited

### Part 2: Applications
- Espejo, R. — P.M. Manufacturers: VSM as diagnostic tool
- Ben-Eli, M.U. — Strategic planning at an academic medical center

### Part 3: Methodology and Epistemology
- Beer, S. — National government (applying VSM at the nation-state level)
- Espejo, R. — A cybernetic method to study organizations
- Harnden, R.J. — "Outside and Then: An Interpretive Approach to the VSM"

### Part 4: Critical Views
- Jackson, M.C. — Evaluation of the managerial significance of the VSM
- Espejo & Harnden — "The VSM — An Ongoing Conversation"

## Significance

This is the canonical edited collection on VSM theory and practice. It bridges Beer's original works and the broader academic community. Notable for including Jackson's critical evaluation *within* the collection itself — showing that the VSM community engaged with criticism rather than dismissing it.

Jackson's chapter here is one of the foundational critiques: technical language barriers, abstract nature, questionable brain-organisation analogy, and concerns about authoritarianism.

## Key Contributions

1. Waelchli's chapter formalising the VSM-Ashby connection is important for anyone wanting the variety equations made explicit.
2. Anderton argues the VSM needs more formal mathematical development — a criticism that has never been fully addressed.
3. Espejo's revisiting of the VSM begins his trajectory toward VIPLAN methodology and the shift from "control" to "cohesion" language.
