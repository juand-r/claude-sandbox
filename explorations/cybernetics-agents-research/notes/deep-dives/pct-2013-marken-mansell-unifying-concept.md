# Marken & Mansell (2013) — Perceptual Control as a Unifying Concept in Psychology

## Citation
Marken, R.S. & Mansell, W. (2013). Perceptual control as a unifying concept in psychology. *Review of General Psychology*, 17(2), 190-195. DOI: 10.1037/a0032933

## Access

## Overview
This is the modern restatement of PCT for a mainstream psychology audience, published in the APA's *Review of General Psychology*. It argues that PCT can unify all traditional areas of psychology under a single framework.

## Core Argument

### The Inversion
Standard models (exemplified by the General Linear Model / GLM) view behavior as **controlled BY** perceptual inputs: stimuli cause responses.

PCT views behavior as **the control OF** perceptual inputs: organisms act to maintain perceptions at reference values.

This is not a minor semantic difference. It inverts the entire causal structure of behavioral science.

### Unification Claim
PCT provides a framework for:
- Motor control (controlling perceptions of limb position, force, etc.)
- Cognitive psychology (controlling perceptions of problem states, categories, relationships)
- Social psychology (controlling perceptions of social status, belonging, fairness)
- Clinical psychology (psychological distress arises from unresolved conflict between control systems)
- Cognitive neuroscience (neural circuits implement hierarchical control loops)
- Counseling (Method of Levels therapy)

The unifying mechanism is always the same: negative feedback control of perceptual variables, organized hierarchically.

### The Test for the Controlled Variable (TCV)
The paper calls for a new research methodology — the TCV — aimed at **discovering the input variables that organisms control**, rather than **the input variables that control organisms**.

This is a methodological revolution: instead of asking "what stimulus causes this response?" ask "what perception is this organism maintaining?"

## Follow-Up Work
Marken & Mansell (2015). "The Origins and Future of Control Theory in Psychology." *Review of General Psychology*. DOI: 10.1037/gpr0000057

This follow-up argues that:
- "Control" is virtually ubiquitous in psychology
- Control theory models the dynamic systems that enable control
- But control theory's origins and principles are often misunderstood
- Most uses of "control" in psychology are informal; PCT provides the formal framework

## Significance for Agent Research

### Controlled Variables as the Unit of Analysis
If we apply the Marken & Mansell framework to AI agents, the fundamental research question becomes: **What perceptual variables does the agent control?**

For an LLM agent:
- Does it control perceived coherence of its output?
- Does it control perceived helpfulness?
- Does it control perceived alignment with instructions?
- Does it control perceived factual accuracy?

These are empirically testable questions using the TCV methodology:
1. Hypothesize a controlled variable (e.g., "the agent controls perceived helpfulness")
2. Apply a disturbance (e.g., give an intentionally unhelpful prompt)
3. Observe whether the agent acts to counter the disturbance (e.g., works harder to be helpful despite the disturbance)
4. If compensatory action occurs, the hypothesis is supported

This could be a rigorous methodology for understanding what LLM agents are actually doing, as opposed to what we think they're doing.
