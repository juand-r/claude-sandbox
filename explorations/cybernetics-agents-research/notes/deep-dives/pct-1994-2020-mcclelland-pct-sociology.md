## Background
Kent McClelland is Professor Emeritus of Sociology at Grinnell College. PhD from Harvard, BA from Oberlin. His work applies PCT to collective control processes, interpersonal conflicts, and social policy.

## Core Concepts

### 1. Perceptual Control and Social Power (1994)
McClelland distinguishes **social power** from **interpersonal force/coercion/influence**.

In PCT terms:
- Force/coercion = applying a disturbance to another person's controlled variable, forcing them to expend effort counteracting it.
- Influence = providing information that might change another person's reference signals (what they want to perceive).
- **Social power** = alignment of goals (reference signals) among multiple independent control systems.

Power is not about one person controlling another. It's about the structural alignment of reference signals across a population of control systems. When many people share similar reference signals and are connected through a common environment, they collectively control aspects of that environment that no individual could control alone.

### 2. Collective Control (2004)
The key finding: agents controlling their own perceptions of a single environmental variable can stabilize it even when their intentions conflict.

This is profound. It means social order can emerge from **conflicting** individual control systems, not just cooperating ones. The stabilization occurs because:
1. Each agent acts on the environment to control its own perception.
2. Even with different reference values, the agents' outputs partially overlap and partially cancel.
3. The net effect is that the environmental variable is held within a range — more stable than any individual could achieve, but not at any single agent's preferred value.
4. This is a PCT explanation of social norms, institutions, and structures: they are the emergent result of collective control by agents with partially conflicting goals.

### 3. Social Structure Theory (2020)
McClelland's mature theory:

**What social structures ARE (in PCT terms):**
- Environmental phenomena stabilized by collective control.
- These include: formations of physical objects, repetitive patterns of human action, linguistic conventions, institutional procedures.

**How social structures WORK:**
1. Individual control of perceptions has a stabilizing impact on the physical environment. When you control your perception of "my desk is clean," you repeatedly clean the desk, stabilizing its state.
2. When multiple people control similar perceptions in a shared environment, the stabilizing impact is more lasting and widespread.
3. The stabilized environmental phenomena provide feedback paths for control of higher-order perceptions. The physical arrangement of a classroom (stabilized by collective control) provides the feedback structure that enables control of "being a student" or "being a teacher" (higher-order perceptions).
4. Social structures are thus self-reinforcing loops: collective control creates stable environmental features, which provide feedback paths for higher-order control, which maintains the lower-order control.

## Relevance to Multi-Agent Systems

### Collective Control = Emergent Social Order in Multi-Agent Systems
McClelland's work provides the theoretical foundation for understanding how multiple PCT-based agents, each controlling its own perceptions, can produce stable collective behavior without:
- Central coordination
- Shared plans
- Explicit communication protocols
- Global reward functions

This is directly applicable to multi-agent AI systems. Instead of designing coordination protocols, you could design agents that each control their own perceptual variables in a shared environment. Collective order would emerge from the physics of the environment and the agents' individual control loops.

### Social Power = Reference Signal Alignment
In a multi-agent LLM system, "power" isn't about which agent can override others. It's about which agent's reference signals (goals, values, priorities) are most widely shared or most strongly influence the environment. This reframes AI alignment as a question about reference signal alignment across a population of control systems.

### Conflict as Stabilizer
Counter-intuitively, McClelland shows that some conflict between agents' reference signals can increase environmental stability (within a range). This suggests that multi-agent systems with some diversity of goals may be more robust than perfectly aligned systems.
