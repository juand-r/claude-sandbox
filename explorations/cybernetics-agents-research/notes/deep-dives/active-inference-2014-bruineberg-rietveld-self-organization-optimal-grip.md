# Bruineberg & Rietveld (2014) — Self-Organization, Free Energy Minimization, and Optimal Grip

**Paper:** Bruineberg, J. & Rietveld, E. (2014). "Self-organization, free energy
minimization, and optimal grip on a field of affordances." *Frontiers in Human
Neuroscience*, 8, Article 599.
**URL:** https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/human-neuroscience/articles/10.3389/fnhum.2014.00599/full

---

## Summary

This paper proposes an enactivist/ecological interpretation of the Free Energy
Principle, embedding it within what the authors call "Radical Embodied Cognitive
Neuroscience." The key move: reinterpreting free energy minimization not as
internal inference about an external world, but as the self-organization of
brain-body-environment systems toward "optimal grip" on available affordances.

## Core Framework: Skilled Intentionality

The paper starts from phenomenology (Merleau-Ponty) and ecological psychology
(Gibson):

- Agents interact with their environment through **affordances** — possibilities
  for action
- Skilled agents are selectively responsive to relevant affordances — not all
  possibilities for action are equally salient
- This selective responsiveness is not cognitive (deliberate selection) but
  skillful (embodied, pre-reflective)

The question: how does a complex system like the brain selectively respond to only
some affordances in context?

## The Enactivist FEP

Bruineberg and Rietveld propose a "radical" reinterpretation of the FEP:

### Solicitations as Prediction Errors

Within their framework, a **solicitation** (an affordance that "calls for" action)
is a gradient/prediction error that can be resolved through action. The agent does
not infer the state of the world and then decide what to do. Instead, the
brain-body-environment system self-organizes to resolve prediction errors
through action.

### Optimal Grip

Merleau-Ponty's "tendency toward optimal grip" is formalized as free energy
minimization. The agent continuously adjusts its relation to the environment to
achieve the best possible grip on the situation — the lowest free energy state.

This is not internal inference about external reality. It is the self-organization
of the coupled system (agent + environment) toward a state of maximal "fit."

### No Internal Representations

The radical version of FEP "does not posit an explanatory role for propositional
internal states." Self-organization replaces representation. The brain's role is
to coordinate action-readiness patterns, not to construct internal models of the
world.

## Self-Organization and Metastability

### Synergetics

The paper uses Haken's synergetics to explain affordance responsiveness:
- The brain-body-environment system has multiple possible stable states (attractors)
- Context determines which attractors are available
- Self-organization selects among them

### Metastability

At the **metastable regime**, the system is poised near critical transitions between
different affordance configurations. This enables:
- Flexible switching between different actions
- Multiple action possibilities coexisting
- Context-sensitive responsiveness

### "Hypergrip" on the Field of Affordances

At the optimal metastable distance, a skilled agent has **hypergrip**: simultaneous
readiness for multiple affordances. Example: an expert boxer at the right distance
from a punching bag can flexibly deploy any of a wide range of punches.

## Cybernetic Connections

The paper does not explicitly frame itself as cybernetics, but the parallels are
clear:

1. **Circular causation:** The brain-body-environment system exhibits circular
   causal relations — each component affects and is affected by the others.

2. **Self-organization:** The system self-organizes without a central controller,
   echoing Ashby's ultrastability.

3. **Error correction:** Solicitations-as-prediction-errors are resolved through
   action, which is error-driven control.

4. **Feedback loops:** The continuous perception-action coupling is a feedback
   loop, but one understood as self-organization rather than mechanical control.

5. **Distinction from control theory:** Unlike classical cybernetics, this framework
   rejects the idea of a controller separate from the controlled system. There is
   no homunculus — only self-organization of the coupled system.

## How This Differs from Standard FEP

| Dimension | Standard FEP (Friston) | Radical FEP (Bruineberg/Rietveld) |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------------------------|
| Core operation | Bayesian inference | Self-organization |
| Internal states | Encode beliefs/models | Coordinate action-readiness |
| Representations | Required (generative model) | Rejected |
| Agent-environment | Separate (via Markov blanket) | Coupled (no sharp boundary) |
| Phenomenology | Compatible but not central | Central (Merleau-Ponty) |
| Intellectual heritage | Helmholtz + Bayes | Gibson + Merleau-Ponty |

## Significance for Agent Design

This paper suggests an alternative architecture for agents:

1. **No explicit world model:** Instead of building a generative model, the agent's
   internal dynamics coordinate action-readiness patterns
2. **Affordance-based:** The agent responds to environmental affordances directly,
   not through inference about hidden states
3. **Self-organizing:** Behavior emerges from the self-organization of the
   agent-environment system, not from planning
4. **Metastable dynamics:** Flexible behavior requires the agent to operate near
   critical transitions, not in stable attractors

This is closer to behavior-based robotics (Brooks) than to model-based AI, but
with a richer theoretical grounding.

## Limitations

1. Unclear how to implement computationally — the framework is more philosophical
   than algorithmic
2. Rejects the generative model that makes active inference practical
3. The relationship to the mathematical FEP is contested — is this really the
   same principle, or a different framework using similar language?

## Follow-Up Work

Bruineberg, Kiverstein, & Rietveld (2016): "The anticipating brain is not a
scientist" — further develops the ecological-enactive reading of FEP, arguing
against the "scientist" metaphor for the brain.
