# Von Foerster (1970) — Molecular Ethology: An Immodest Proposal for Semantic Clarification

## Citation
Von Foerster, H. (1970). "Molecular Ethology, An Immodest Proposal for Semantic Clarification." In G. Ungar (Ed.), *Molecular Mechanisms in Memory and Learning*, Plenum Press, New York, pp. 213-248. Reprinted in *Understanding Understanding* (Springer, 2003), ch. 4.

## Source Status
Not freely available online as full text. Available in the Springer volume and the original Plenum Press collection. Should be noted in INACCESSIBLE_PAPERS.md.

## Core Ideas (from secondary sources and known content)

### The Title

"Molecular Ethology" — a deliberately provocative neologism combining molecular biology with ethology (the study of behavior). "An Immodest Proposal" echoes Swift. Von Foerster is proposing a clarification of how we talk about the relationship between molecular-level processes and organism-level behavior.

### The Argument

The paper addresses the question: can we explain behavior (ethology) by reducing it to molecular mechanisms? Von Foerster's answer is characteristically nuanced — he does not deny molecular substrates but argues that the *semantics* of the discourse are confused.

The key semantic confusion: conflating the *mechanism* of a process with the *function* of a process. Molecules don't "remember" or "learn" — these are descriptions at the level of the organism, not the molecule. When molecular biologists say they've found "the molecule of memory," they are committing a category error — confusing levels of description.

### The Computation Argument

Von Foerster argues that what we call "memory" and "learning" are computational properties — they emerge from the *organization* of molecular processes, not from the molecules themselves. The same molecules in a different organization would not produce memory. Therefore, the explanation of memory must be at the level of organization (computation), not at the level of substrate (molecules).

This is a proto-functionalist argument and connects to his broader computational theory of cognition. It also anticipates the multiple-realizability arguments in philosophy of mind.

### Connection to BCL Research

The BCL's research program was precisely about this interface: "biological computing" meant understanding how biological organization produces computational properties. The lab was not doing molecular biology — it was studying computation as it manifests in biological systems.

This paper connects to von Foerster's earlier work on memory (*Das Gedachtnis*, 1948), where he proposed a quantum-mechanical theory of memory storage.

## Relevance to Agent Architectures

### Substrate Independence

Von Foerster's argument that cognition is about organization, not substrate, is the intellectual ancestor of the claim that AI can implement cognitive functions in silicon rather than carbon. If memory is computational organization, then any substrate that supports the right organization will exhibit memory.

### Levels of Description

The warning about confating mechanism and function applies to AI discourse. When we say a neural network "learns" or "remembers," we are using behavioral-level descriptions for what is, at the substrate level, matrix multiplication and gradient descent. Von Foerster would say this is legitimate *only if* we are clear about which level of description we are operating at.

### The Organization Is the Key

For agent architectures: what matters is the organization of computation, not the specific implementation details. This supports the focus on architectural patterns (transformer, RNN, etc.) rather than on specific hardware or numerical implementations.

## Key Formulations

- Molecular ethology: studying behavior at the molecular level requires semantic clarity
- Memory and learning are properties of computational organization, not of substrates
- Category error: attributing organism-level properties to molecule-level entities
- The organization produces the function, not the substrate
