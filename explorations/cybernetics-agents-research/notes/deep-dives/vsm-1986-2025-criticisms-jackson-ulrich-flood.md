# Criticisms of the VSM: Jackson, Ulrich, Flood & Jackson

## Overview

The VSM has faced sustained criticism since the mid-1980s, primarily from the "critical systems thinking" (CST) movement. The main critics are M.C. Jackson, R.L. Flood, and W. Ulrich. The debate continues to this day (Espinosa 2025, Zeini 2025).

---

## M.C. Jackson — The Primary Critic

### Jackson (1986) — "The Cybernetic Model of the Organisation: An Assessment"
- Two versions of cybernetics examined: "management cybernetics" and "organisational cybernetics"
- Jackson argues criticisms hold against management cybernetics but *cannot be sustained* against organisational cybernetics
- This is important: Jackson's own early work acknowledges the VSM (organisational cybernetics) is stronger than its critics sometimes claim

### Jackson (1988) — "An Appreciation of Stafford Beer's 'Viable System': Viewpoint on Managerial Practice"
- Journal of Management Studies, Vol. 25(6), pp. 557-573
- **Core criticisms:**
  1. **Technical language barrier:** Specialist cybernetic language and engineering-influenced visual representations create cognitive barriers for managers
  2. **Abstract nature:** Purely theoretical design, scant formalisation, no clear procedures for application
  3. **Questionable analogy:** The brain-organisation analogy is debatable
  4. **Lack of empirical confirmation** (at that time)

### Jackson (1988) — with Flood
- **Formal criticisms:**
  1. Abstract nature and inexistence of clear application procedures complicating empirical confirmation
  2. Questionable brain-organisation analogy
  3. Hierarchical layout, authoritarian nature, lack of flexibility
  4. Minimises importance of individuals in favour of structural design

### Flood & Jackson (1991) — System of Systems Methodologies (SoSM)
- Classified VSM as "unitary-complex" methodology
  - **Complex:** dealing with highly interconnected systems (agreed by all)
  - **Unitary:** assuming participants share common values and interests (contested)
- VSM placed alongside hard systems approaches, not with pluralist or coercive methodologies
- This classification became the dominant framing of VSM limitations for decades

### Jackson's Additional Critiques
- VSM may oversimplify cultural and social dynamics
- Implementation challenging in organisations with low systems literacy
- VSM assumes a functionalist worldview — that the organisation *has* a purpose that everyone shares
- VSM lacks reflexive capacity — cannot examine its own boundary judgments

### Jackson's Evolution (noted by Espinosa 2025)
- Jackson has become "more supportive" of VSM and Team Syntegrity over time
- In Jackson (2019), he highlights Team Syntegrity as relevant for "coercive complexity"
- The critique has softened but not been fully retracted

## Werner Ulrich — Normative Critique

### Ulrich — Critical Systems Heuristics (CSH)
- CSH focuses on *boundary critique*: whose values, interests, and worldview define the system?
- VSM takes the system boundary as given; CSH asks *who drew the boundary and why?*

### Ulrich's Specific Critique of Beer
- Ulrich published "Critique of Pure Cybernetic Reason: The Chilean Experience with Cybernetics" (c.1981-1983)
- Beer responded: "A Reply to Ulrich's 'Critique of Pure Cybernetic Reason'" (JASA, 1983, Vol. 10, pp. 115-119)
- Core issue: cybernetic systems design serves *somebody's* interests. Whose? VSM does not have built-in mechanisms for asking this question.

### Philosophical Difference
- Ulrich's CSH is critical in terms of Kantian/Hegelian idealism (via Churchman)
- Not critical in the Marxist/Frankfurt School sense
- Ulrich wants to examine the *ideas* entering social systems design, not the material conditions producing those ideas
- VSM and CSH can be complementary: VSM for structure, CSH for normative reflection

## Responses and Counter-Arguments

### Beer's Own Response
- Beer (1983) directly responded to Ulrich
- Beer insisted VSM embodies *maximum autonomy* as a design principle
- The metasystem is a "liberty machine" — it serves operations, not dominations

### Espejo's Response (ongoing since 1989)
- "Control" → "cohesion" language change
- Demonstrated VSM in democratic, participatory settings (Colombian cities, co-operatives)
- VIPLAN methodology explicitly includes stakeholder analysis

### Espinosa (2025) — "Revisiting the VSM as an Emancipatory Systems Approach"
- Definitive response to Jackson's criticisms
- Arguments:
  1. Beer's metasystem is a *disseminated network*, not a hierarchy
  2. It acts on *distributed information*, not authority
  3. VSM has been successfully applied in politically complex, multi-stakeholder environments
  4. Beer and followers have addressed several original criticisms
  5. Jackson himself has evolved toward more supportive positions

### Zeini (2025) — Power-Aware VSM
- Integrates John Gaventa's "power cube" framework with VSM
- Visible, hidden, and invisible forms of power across decision-making levels
- Adds a "power-sensitive analytical layer" to VSM functions
- This directly addresses the critique that VSM ignores power relations

### Schwaninger (2016) — Empirical Counter-Evidence
- 71% correlation between VSM viability criteria and organisational survival
- Empirical evidence counters the "abstract and unvalidated" criticism

## Summary: The Five Core Criticisms and Their Status

| Criticism | Source | Status (2025) |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| 1. Technical language barriers | Jackson (1988) | **Partially addressed.** Hoverstadt, Walker, Pérez Ríos have created accessible versions. But Beer's originals remain dense. |
| 2. Lack of clear application procedures | Jackson & Flood (1988) | **Addressed.** VIPLAN (Espejo), Four-Stage Method (Pérez Ríos), Walker's Guide all provide explicit procedures. |
| 3. Questionable brain-organisation analogy | Jackson (1988, 2000) | **Partially addressed.** Beer himself derived VSM from first principles (Heart of Enterprise) without the analogy. But the analogy persists in popular treatments. |
| 4. Authoritarian/hierarchical structure | Flood & Jackson (1991) | **Largely addressed.** Applications in co-operatives, eco-communities, indigenous communities. Beer's design maximises autonomy. "Liberty machine" framing. Zeini (2025) adds power analysis. |
| 5. Ignores individual/social/political dimensions | Jackson, Ulrich | **Partially addressed.** Espinosa (2025) and Zeini (2025) show VSM can handle political complexity. Leonard (1992) shows VSM+STS compatibility. But VSM itself does not have built-in normative reflection mechanisms — CSH remains a useful complement. |

---

## Relevance to Agent Architectures

1. The "authoritarianism" critique maps to agent autonomy concerns: are agents controlled by a hierarchical master, or do they operate in a network?
2. The "boundary critique" maps to defining agent system scope: whose goals does the system serve?
3. The "lack of social dimensions" maps to human-agent interaction: how do humans participate in agent governance?
4. Zeini's power-aware VSM suggests agent systems should explicitly model power relations, not just functional relationships
5. The fact that these criticisms have been largely addressed (with specific methods like VIPLAN, power-aware VSM) means VSM-based agent architectures can incorporate the solutions
