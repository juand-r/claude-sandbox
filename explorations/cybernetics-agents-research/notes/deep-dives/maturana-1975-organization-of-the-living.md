# The Organization of the Living: A Theory of the Living Organization (1975)

## Citation
Maturana, H.R. (1975). "The organization of the living: A theory of the living organization." *International Journal of Man-Machine Studies*, 7(3), 313-332. DOI: 10.1016/S0020-7373(75)80015-0

## Context
Presented at the Conference on Biologically Motivated Automata Theory, Maclean, U.S.A., June 19-21, 1974. Published 1975. This paper is Maturana's solo presentation of autopoiesis theory following the joint 1973 book and 1974 *Biosystems* paper with Varela and Uribe.

## Key Content

### Autonomy as Self-Production
The fundamental feature that characterizes living systems is autonomy. Any account of their organization as systems that can exist as individual unities must show what autonomy is as a phenomenon proper to them. Maturana's answer: autonomy in living systems is a feature of self-production (autopoiesis).

### Living Systems as Networks of Component Production
A living system is properly characterized as a network of processes of production of components. This is the autopoietic organization. Living systems are autopoietic systems in the physical space.

### The Nervous System
The paper develops the idea that the nervous system is a closed network of interacting neurons structurally coupled to the living system. The nervous system does not process information about an external world; it participates in the organism's autopoiesis by expanding the domain of possible states.

### Structural Coupling
This paper contains one of Maturana's early discussions of structural coupling within the framework of autopoiesis. The organism and its environment undergo congruent structural changes through their history of interaction. Neither instructs the other.

### Ontogeny and Evolution
Maturana discusses how ontogeny (individual development) and phylogeny (evolutionary history) are both processes of structural change within the framework of autopoietic organization. Evolution is not optimization toward a pre-given environment but "natural drift" -- the maintenance of structural coupling through viable interactions.

## Significance
This paper consolidates the autopoiesis framework as Maturana's solo theoretical position. It is notable for its venue -- the *International Journal of Man-Machine Studies* -- which situates the theory at the intersection of biology and computing. The paper reveals the "philosophical richness of autopoiesis" (per secondary commentary) through its discussion of the nervous system, structural coupling, and evolution.

## Relevance to Agent Architectures
- Published in a journal explicitly about man-machine studies, suggesting Maturana was aware of the relevance to artificial systems
- The discussion of autonomy as self-production sets a high bar for what should count as an autonomous agent
- The closed nervous system concept anticipates modern concerns about AI systems being "closed" to external reality
