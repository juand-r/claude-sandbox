# Wiener-Rosenblueth Collaboration Papers

## Overview

Wiener's collaboration with the Mexican physiologist Arturo Rosenblueth was the
most important partnership in the development of cybernetics. They received a
five-year Rockefeller Foundation Grant, alternating between Mexico and MIT.

## "The Role of Models in Science" (1945)

**Authors:** Arturo Rosenblueth, Norbert Wiener
**Published:** Philosophy of Science, Vol. 12, No. 4, pp. 316-321 (Oct. 1945)
**JSTOR:** http://www.jstor.org/stable/184253
**Full text PDF:** https://nemenmanlab.org/~ilya/images/9/99/Rosenblueth-wiener-1945.pdf
**Access:** FULL TEXT OBTAINED

### Core Argument

Science proceeds by replacing complex reality with simpler models. Two types:

1. **Material models** — physical representations with simpler structure but
   similar properties to the original system
2. **Formal/intellectual models** — symbolic assertions in logical terms of
   idealized situations sharing structural properties with the original

### Key Points

- "No substantial part of the universe is so simple that it can be grasped
  and controlled without abstraction."
- An experiment is a question. Foolish answers indicate a foolish question.
- There is a hierarchy of questions determined by generality of answers sought.
- Good experimenters excel at interchanging symbols and events; theorists
  deal with operations at various levels within abstraction.

### The "Closed Box" vs "Open Box" Framework

This is a precursor to the black-box concept central to cybernetics:
- **Closed box** — finite input variables determine finite output variables;
  internal structure unknown
- **Open box** — additional terminals/variables reveal internal structure
- Scientific progress = progressive opening of boxes
- "All scientific problems begin as closed-box problems"

### The Limit of Models

The best material model of a cat is another, or preferably the same, cat
(cf. Lewis Carroll's map that was the country itself). The ideal formal model
would cover the entire universe with one-to-one correspondence — but then
it would be unnecessary. This is an argument for the inherent limitations
of modeling.

### Criteria for Useful Material Models

A material model is useful when:
(a) It helps translate unfamiliar phenomena into familiar terms
(b) It enables experiments under more favorable conditions

A model is USELESS when:
- The formal model suggesting it is weak/trivial (gross analogy)
- It doesn't suggest experiments beyond what the formal model alone predicts
- It is more complex than the original system

### Relevance to AI

- The closed-box/open-box framework directly maps to interpretability research
- The model hierarchy maps to levels of abstraction in agent architectures
- The warning about gross analogies applies to anthropomorphizing AI systems
- The cat-model-of-a-cat limit is relevant to simulation-based approaches

## "Purposeful and Non-Purposeful Behavior" (1950)

**Authors:** Arturo Rosenblueth, Norbert Wiener
**Published:** Philosophy of Science, Vol. 17, No. 4, pp. 318-326 (1950)
**Access:** JSTOR, Cambridge Core (paywall)

### Context

This paper responds to Richard Taylor's critique of their 1943 "Behavior, Purpose
and Teleology." Taylor questioned whether feedback-based definitions truly capture
purpose or merely describe mimicry of purpose.

### The Debate

- Taylor (1950): "Comments on a mechanistic conception of purposefulness"
  (Phil. Sci. 17(4), 310-317)
- Rosenblueth & Wiener (1950): This paper (318-326)
- Taylor (1950): "Rejoinder" (327-332)
- Moulyn (1951): Commentary (Phil. Sci. 18(2), 154)

This debate remains alive in philosophy of AI: is an agent that uses feedback
to achieve goals genuinely "purposeful"? Or is it a simulacrum?

## "The Mathematical Formulation of the Problem of Conduction of Impulses
   in a Network of Connected Excitable Elements, Specifically in Cardiac
   Muscle" (1946)

**Authors:** Norbert Wiener, Arturo Rosenblueth
**Published:** Archivos del Instituto de Cardiologia de Mexico, Vol. 16, No. 3,
              pp. 205-265 (July 1946)
**PubMed:** 20245817
**Access:** PubMed, collected works (1974 reprint)

### Significance

This paper introduces one of the **earliest cellular automaton models** — predating
Conway's Game of Life by 24 years. Wiener and Rosenblueth developed a cellular
automaton model of excitable media to describe impulse conduction in cardiac tissue.

### Key Contributions

- Modeled cardiac tissue as a network of connected excitable elements
- Each element has states: resting, excited, refractory
- Demonstrated how reentry waves (spiral waves) circulate through obstacles
- Explained cardiac arrhythmias mathematically
- Since computers were minimal in 1946, this was purely theoretical — they
  did not implement the automaton computationally

### Relevance to AI

- One of the earliest examples of agent-based modeling / multi-agent systems
- The excitable medium model is a precursor to cellular automata and
  artificial life
- Shows how complex global behavior emerges from simple local rules —
  the same principle underlying swarm intelligence
