# Beer (1990) — On Suicidal Rabbits: A Relativity of Systems

## Citation
Beer, S. (1990). "On Suicidal Rabbits: A Relativity of Systems." *Systems Practice*, 3(2), 115–124. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/BF01060874

Received: 15 May 1989. Published: April 1990.
Reprinted in *Think Before You Think* (Wavestone Press, 2009).

## Type
Journal paper (festschrift for Russell Ackoff)

## Key Arguments

This paper, written in honor of Russell Ackoff, engages with three of Ackoff's characteristic approaches:

1. **The use of fables**: Beer uses a fable about suicidal rabbits as a vehicle for discussing systemic properties that seem irrational at one level but make sense at another.

2. **"Messes" vs. well-formulated problems**: Ackoff's concept of "messes" — complex, interacting problem systems that resist decomposition into individual problems. Beer extends this, arguing that we often commit suicide as a system while each component acts "rationally."

3. **Idealized planning**: Ackoff's method of designing the ideal system first, then working backward to identify what's feasible. Beer attaches his own thoughts on recursive logic and democratic organization.

### The "Relativity of Systems"
The core concept: what appears rational at one level of recursion may be suicidal at another. A rabbit (subsystem) acting in its own interest may be destroying the rabbit population (metasystem). This is the systemic equivalent of the tragedy of the commons, but framed in terms of recursive levels of the VSM.

Beer uses this to discuss:
- **Recursive logic**: How the same pattern repeats at every level of a viable system
- **Implementation of change**: Why systemic change is so difficult — each level has its own rationality
- **Democratic organization**: How to design systems where local autonomy doesn't produce global pathology

## Relevance to Agent Architectures

The "suicidal rabbits" problem is directly applicable to multi-agent systems:
- **Individual agent rationality vs. system coherence**: Each agent in a multi-agent system may act "rationally" by its own objectives while collectively producing pathological behavior. This is a well-known problem in multi-agent RL.
- **Recursive level conflicts**: An agent optimizing for a sub-task may undermine the higher-level objective. Beer's "relativity of systems" provides a framework for thinking about these cross-level conflicts.
- **The need for System 2 (coordination)**: The suicidal rabbits problem is exactly what System 2 in the VSM is designed to prevent — anti-oscillatory coordination that prevents autonomous units from destructively interfering.

## Connections
- Festschrift for Russell Ackoff (another major systems thinker)
- Engages with Ackoff's "interactive planning" methodology
- Directly relates to the VSM's recursive structure
- The "relativity" in the title is deliberate — Beer draws an analogy to Einstein's relativity, suggesting that systemic properties are observer-dependent and level-dependent
