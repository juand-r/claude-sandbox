# Cybernetic Foundations for Psychology

- **Author:** Bernard Scott
- **Journal:** Constructivist Foundations 11(3): 509–517
- **Year:** 2016
- **URL:** https://constructivist.info/11/3/509.scott
- **Open Access:** Yes

## Summary

Scott argues that psychology is a fragmented discipline lacking shared conceptual foundations, and proposes second-order cybernetics as a unifying framework. Cybernetic concepts already operate implicitly across psychology; making them explicit would integrate "process" and "person" approaches while bridging individual and social psychology.

## Key Arguments

1. **Psychology's fragmentation problem**: The field consists of many specialist domains without shared foundations — cognitive psychology, social psychology, developmental psychology, clinical psychology all operate with different conceptual vocabularies.

2. **Cybernetics as unifying framework**: Second-order cybernetics can provide the missing shared foundations because:
   - Self-organization is fundamental to psychological processes
   - Feedback loops underlie learning, perception, and behavior
   - System dynamics apply across psychological scales (individual to social)

3. **Process vs. person reconciliation**: The mechanistic ("process") and humanistic ("person") traditions in psychology can be reconciled through cybernetics, since both describe self-organizing systems operating through feedback.

4. **Individual-social bridge**: Systems thinking reveals how individual psychological processes and social systems operate through equivalent cybernetic principles (circular causality, self-reference, emergence).

## Key Cybernetic Concepts Applied

- Self-organization as fundamental to psychological processes
- Control mechanisms via feedback loops
- Constructivist perspectives from second-order cybernetics
- Conversation theory (Pask) as bridge between individual and social

## Relevance to Our Research

Scott's argument has a direct analog for agent architecture: just as psychology fragments into incompatible sub-disciplines, AI agent design fragments into perception, reasoning, planning, learning, etc. A cybernetic unification — where all these are seen as aspects of a single self-organizing, feedback-driven process — could yield more coherent agent architectures. The process/person distinction maps onto the agent design tension between mechanistic components and holistic agent behavior.
