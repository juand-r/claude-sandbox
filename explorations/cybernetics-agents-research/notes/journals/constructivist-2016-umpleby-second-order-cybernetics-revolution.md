# Second-Order Cybernetics as a Fundamental Revolution in Science

- **Author:** Stuart A. Umpleby
- **Journal:** Constructivist Foundations 11(3): 455–465
- **Year:** 2016
- **URL:** https://constructivist.info/11/3/455.umpleby
- **Open Access:** Yes

## Summary

Umpleby argues that the transition from first-order to second-order cybernetics constitutes a fundamental scientific revolution, not just a development within cybernetics. The inclusion of the observer within the domain of scientific investigation changes the foundations of how science is conducted, especially for living systems.

## Key Arguments

1. **Beyond cybernetics**: The shift is not just a sub-field development — it represents a paradigm shift for science as a whole, comparable to other fundamental revolutions.

2. **Observer inclusion**: Traditional science excludes the observer to manufacture objectivity. Second-order cybernetics includes the observer, recognizing that for living systems, complete objectivity is impossible.

3. **Von Foerster's 1974 formulation**: The term was introduced as the "cybernetics of observing systems" — dual meaning: systems that observe, and the act of observing systems.

4. **Science "from within"**: Rather than studying systems from an external vantage point, second-order cybernetics enables investigation from within the system.

5. **Still underknown**: Despite its radical implications, "the term is still not widely known outside the fields of cybernetics and systems science."

## Practical Applications

- Social sciences (where the researcher is part of society)
- Management (where the manager is part of the organization)
- Environmental sciences (where humans are part of the ecosystem)
- Action research and engaged scholarship methodologies

## Relevance to Our Research

This paper provides the clearest articulation of WHY second-order cybernetics matters for agent design. If an AI agent is part of the system it is reasoning about (e.g., a conversational agent is part of the conversation), then first-order approaches that treat it as an external observer are fundamentally limited. Umpleby's framing of this as a "revolution" — not just a refinement — strengthens the case for building agents on second-order principles.

## Key Quote

The transition from first-order to second-order cybernetics is characterized by moving the scientist "within the domain of what is described and analyzed."
