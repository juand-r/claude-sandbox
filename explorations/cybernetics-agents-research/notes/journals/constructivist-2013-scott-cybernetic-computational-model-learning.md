# A Cybernetic Computational Model for Learning and Skill Acquisition

**Authors:** Bernard Scott, Abhinav Bansal
**Year:** 2013
**Journal:** Constructivist Foundations, Vol. 9, No. 1, pp. 125-136
**URL:** https://constructivist.info/9/1/125
**Type:** Article in special issue on Computational Constructivism

## Core Contribution

A computational model grounded in second-order cybernetics that explains cognitive mechanisms underlying motor skill development. Originally created by Scott in the 1970s, reimplemented by Bansal for contemporary use.

## Key Arguments

- Addresses two puzzling phenomena: (1) why learners lose conscious awareness of skill structure during mastery, and (2) how they detect errors before receiving external feedback.
- Offers "a constructivist account of skill acquisition and associated phenomena"
- Grounded in second-order cybernetics rather than conventional cognitive architectures.
- The cybernetic model provides an alternative cognitive architecture that can be contrasted with non-constructivist architectures in cognitive science.
- Practical applications in physical therapy and sports science.

## Historical Note

The fact that this model originated in the 1970s and was reimplemented decades later is interesting — it suggests cybernetic computational approaches were ahead of their time, or at least exploring a path that mainstream AI abandoned.

## Relevance to Project

Demonstrates that second-order cybernetics can produce actual computational models, not just philosophical frameworks. The model of skill acquisition (hierarchical learning with loss of conscious access) could inform agent architectures that develop layered competencies. The error-detection-before-feedback mechanism is especially interesting — it suggests agents that construct internal models of their own performance.
