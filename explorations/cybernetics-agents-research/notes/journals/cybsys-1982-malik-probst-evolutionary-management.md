## Summary
Applies cybernetic and self-organization concepts to management theory. Argues that organizations successfully adapted to their environment constitute the result of evolutionary processes, and that order evolves during and as a result of that process.

Key tension explored: Drucker's observation that "the only things that evolve by themselves in an organization are disorder, friction, and malperformance" vs. Hayek's insight that "the only possibility of transcending the capacity of individual minds is to rely on those superpersonal 'self-organizing' forces which create spontaneous orders."

Part of the St. Gall Management Approach (Swiss-Austrian tradition of management cybernetics).

## Key Themes
- Self-organization in social/organizational systems
- Evolutionary vs. designed order
- Cybernetics applied to management (management cybernetics tradition)
- Connects to Stafford Beer's Viable System Model

## Relevance to Our Research
The tension between designed control and emergent self-organization is central to multi-agent systems. Do you design agent coordination top-down (orchestrator pattern) or let it emerge bottom-up (swarm/stigmergy)? Malik and Probst argue for a hybrid: create conditions for self-organization rather than trying to control directly. This "evolutionary management" approach maps to modern ideas about agent scaffolding -- providing structure without over-constraining.
