## Abstract/Summary
Tektologia (1912), the "rediscovered" magnum opus of the Russian philosopher and scientist A. Bogdanov, is regarded as the first fundamental variant of general systems theory and a precursor of cybernetics. The article outlines tektology's basic concepts and methods and notes similarities and differences between tektology on one hand, and General System Theory (von Bertalanffy) and systems approaches in cybernetics on the other.

Key argument: tektology is not merely a variant of GST or cybernetics, but a unique paradigm of general science of organization. Its clear organizational focus, universal scope, all-scientific methods, and comprehensive conceptual framework make it something more than each taken separately.

## Key Themes
- Tektology as proto-cybernetics (1912, decades before Wiener)
- The Russian contribution to systems thinking predating Western work
- Organization as a universal concept
- Historical roots of cybernetic ideas

## Relevance to Our Research
Useful for understanding the deep historical roots of cybernetic/systems thinking. Bogdanov's idea that "organization" is the fundamental concept (not "control" or "information") offers a different lens for thinking about agent architectures. The organizational perspective might be more relevant to multi-agent systems than the control-theoretic framing.

## Note
Published in the same issue (Vol. 18, No. 2, 1987) as Glanville's "Question of Cybernetics" and Umpleby's "American and Soviet Discussions" -- this was clearly a special issue on foundations.
