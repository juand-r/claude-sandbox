# Orengo (2018) - Theoretical Notes on Practical Application of VSM

**Citation:** Orengo, M. (2018), "Theoretical notes regarding the practical application of Stafford Beer's viable system model", *Kybernetes*, Vol. 47 No. 2, pp. 262-272.

**DOI:** 10.1108/K-02-2017-0069

**Access:** Emerald Insight (paywalled).

## Purpose

Capture current difficulties with the practical application of VSM and suggest improvements.

## Key Argument: VSM Is Stuck in the Chasm

The paper argues that VSM is currently stuck in the typical **chasm** of a bell-shaped innovation diffusion curve -- the gap between early adopters and early majority. Despite decades of development, VSM has not achieved mainstream adoption.

## Six Propositions for Bridging the Chasm

The paper makes six propositions to move VSM from early adopters to early majority:

1. Focus on **variety balances** as the core abstract contribution of VSM (rather than trying to make it a comprehensive management framework)
2. Better connect VSM with the suite of established management methods and tools
3. Train fewer but better VSM experts (rather than trying to make VSM accessible to everyone)
4. Have these experts support managers who have generic management knowledge
5. Address the practical gap between VSM theory and implementation
6. Focus on the algedonic channel and cyclic recursion as practical entry points

## Contrarian Position

Against the common claim that "VSM should be made easily accessible to a larger number of people," Orengo argues the opposite: a **smaller number of well-trained VSM specialists** should support a larger number of managers. This is a professionalization argument.

## Relevance to Our Project

This paper is a reality check on VSM adoption and has direct implications for our work:

1. **Abstraction level matters:** If VSM's core contribution is variety balancing (not the full five-system model), then our translation to agent architecture should focus on variety engineering mechanisms, not on literally mapping Systems 1-5.

2. **Integration with existing tools:** Just as VSM needs to connect with established management methods, a cybernetic agent framework needs to integrate with existing AI/ML tooling, not replace it.

3. **The chasm problem applies to our work too:** If we design a cybernetic agent framework, it will face the same adoption challenges. Orengo's propositions suggest we should make the framework practically useful alongside existing tools, not as a replacement.

4. **Expert-supported approach:** Rather than expecting every developer to understand cybernetics, the framework should encode cybernetic principles in a way that developers can use without deep theory knowledge.
