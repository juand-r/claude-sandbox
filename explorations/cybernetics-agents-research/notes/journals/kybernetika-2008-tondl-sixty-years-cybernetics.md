## Summary

A philosophical and historical reflection on cybernetics' first sixty years, published in
the journal that has been the Czech/Central European home of cybernetics since 1965.

## Key Points

- Traces cybernetics from Wiener's original 1948 definition ("communication and control
  in living organisms and machines") through six decades of development.
- Emphasizes the role of interdisciplinary workshops (Macy Conferences and similar) in
  creating intellectual networks across mathematics, engineering, biology, and linguistics.
- Key figures discussed: Shannon, von Neumann, Jakobson -- showing how diverse fields
  converged around cybernetic ideas.
- Highlights that these collaborations "kept enhancing respect, weight and significance
  attached to mathematics, to mathematical methods" across scientific domains.
- Discusses the role of semantics and semiotics in the cybernetic program -- the attempt
  to unify different scientific disciplines under a common mathematical framework.

## Relevance to Cybernetics-Agents Research

- **Historical grounding:** Provides the intellectual genealogy of cybernetics as it was
  understood by the Central European mathematical tradition (distinct from the American
  or British schools).
- **Interdisciplinary character:** The paper demonstrates that cybernetics was always
  about bridging disciplines -- exactly what agent design requires (bridging control
  theory, information theory, decision theory, and AI).
- **Mathematical emphasis:** The Czech cybernetics tradition (represented by Kybernetika
  journal) has always been more mathematically rigorous than the "soft" cybernetics of
  management science or social systems. This mathematical rigor is what makes these
  foundations useful for agent design.
- **Communication + Control unity:** Wiener's original insight that communication and
  control are inseparable is directly relevant to agent architectures where sensing,
  reasoning, and acting must be tightly coupled.
