# Interactional Moral Systems: A Model of Social Mechanisms for the Moral Regulation of Exchange Processes in Agent Societies

## Citation
da Rocha Costa, A.C., & Coelho, H. (2019). Interactional Moral Systems: A Model of Social Mechanisms for the Moral Regulation of Exchange Processes in Agent Societies. *IEEE Transactions on Computational Social Systems*, 6(4), 778-796. DOI: 10.1109/TCSS.2019.2926950

## Access

- Full text not freely available. No arXiv preprint found.

## Summary

Introduces a formal model of moral regulation in agent societies. Defines moral agents, moral systems, and agents "sensible to moral sanction." Models how moral gains and losses in social exchange processes are regulated through reputation-based mechanisms and negotiation.

## Key Contributions

1. **Moral agent formalization**: Defines what it means for an agent to be a "moral agent" in a multi-agent system — specifically, one that is sensible to moral sanctions and adjusts behavior accordingly.

2. **Reputation-based moral regulation**: Proposes that moral regulation of exchange processes works through reputation mechanisms — agents who violate moral norms suffer reputation loss, which in turn affects future exchange opportunities.

3. **Negotiation-driven equilibrium**: Models how negotiation processes among morally-aware agents can drive exchange balances toward equilibrium, a form of social homeostasis.

## Relevance to Cybernetics Research

- **Social homeostasis**: This is one of the closest papers to the concept of social homeostasis in IEEE TCSS. Moral regulation acts as a feedback mechanism that keeps social exchange processes near equilibrium — directly analogous to homeostatic regulation.
- **Self-regulation without central authority**: The moral regulation emerges from agent interactions, not from a central enforcer. This is distributed governance through social norms — a cybernetic mechanism.
- **Reputation as information feedback**: Reputation serves as a signal that feeds back into agent decision-making, creating a regulatory loop. Agents who deviate from moral norms get negative reputation signals, which constrain future behavior.
- **Connects to viable system model**: The moral regulation mechanism functions like System 2 (coordination) and System 3 (control) in Beer's VSM, but implemented through distributed social mechanisms rather than hierarchical management.
