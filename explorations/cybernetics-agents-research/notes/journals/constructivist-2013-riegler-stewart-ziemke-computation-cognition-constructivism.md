# Computation, Cognition and Constructivism: Introduction to the Special Issue

- **Authors:** Alexander Riegler, John Stewart, Tom Ziemke
- **Journal:** Constructivist Foundations 9(1): 1–6
- **Year:** 2013
- **URL:** https://constructivist.info/9/1/001.riegler
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **Special Issue:** Computational Approaches to Constructivism (eds. Riegler & Ziemke)

## Summary

This editorial introduces a special issue addressing a fundamental gap: most constructivist discourse remains at the philosophical-conceptual level without computational implementations. The authors ask two core questions: (1) Can constructivist concepts be transformed into working computational models? (2) Does the concept of computation align with constructivist theory?

## Key Arguments

1. **The ambiguity of "computational model"**: The notion is ambiguous depending on whether it refers to:
   - The computational theory of mind (strong claim: cognition IS computation)
   - Computation as a tool for exploring constructivist ideas (weak claim: computation can MODEL constructivist processes)
   This distinction is critical and often conflated.

2. **Bidirectional extension**: The special issue reveals potential for both:
   - A computational extension of constructivist approaches (using computation to implement/test constructivist ideas)
   - A constructivist extension of computational approaches (using constructivist epistemology to rethink AI and computation)

3. **The constructivist agent problem**: How can artificial agents comply with constructivist principles? If agents don't refer to input data as representation of a pre-given world, they cannot be programmed with extrinsic goals defined against pre-defined world states. New criteria are needed.

## Relevance to Our Research

This is the most directly relevant paper for our project. The two questions it poses are exactly our questions:
1. Can we build computational agents that embody constructivist principles?
2. Does computation itself need to be reconceived through a constructivist lens?

The identification of the ambiguity in "computational model" is crucial — we need to be clear whether we're using computation as a tool to explore second-order cybernetics, or claiming that second-order cybernetic processes ARE computational. The "bidirectional extension" framing is productive: we can both computationally implement constructivist ideas AND use constructivist epistemology to rethink how we build AI agents.
