## Summary

Landmark paper extending the free energy principle from perception to action. Previously,
Friston and colleagues showed that perceptual inference can be cast as minimization of a
variational free-energy bound on surprise. This paper asks: what happens if the sensory
data *themselves* are sampled to minimize this bound?

## Key Arguments

- **Active inference:** Agents do not just passively infer the world; they actively sample
  sensory data to minimize free energy. Action and perception share a common objective function.
- **Ergodic argument:** The very existence of adaptive agents mandates active sampling --
  agents that persist must occupy a limited set of states (their attracting set).
- **Motor control as inference:** Motor behavior (from retinal stabilization to goal-seeking)
  can be understood as fulfilling prior expectations about proprioceptive sensations.
  This reframes optimal control as inference, eliminating the need for explicit cost functions.
- **Homeostatic foundation:** The free energy bound is motivated by homeostasis -- organisms
  must keep their internal states within viable bounds, which is equivalent to minimizing
  surprise about sensory observations.

## Relevance to Our Research

This is the foundational paper connecting the free energy principle to action and embodiment
in Biological Cybernetics. It shows how cybernetic concepts (homeostasis, regulation, feedback)
can be formalized through variational inference. Directly relevant to:
- Active inference as a cybernetic framework for agents
- The connection between homeostasis and intelligent behavior
- Eliminating the action-perception divide (both minimize free energy)

## Key Quotes / Ideas

- Action is "active inference" -- sampling the world to confirm predictions
- No need for separate cost functions; priors encode goals
- Formally connects Helmholtz's perceptual inference to motor control
- Ergodic theory provides existential justification for free energy minimization
