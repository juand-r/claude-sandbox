## Abstract / Summary

Scheutz frames the "Affect Dilemma": humans are deeply affective beings who expect other human-like agents to be sensitive to and express affect. Complex artificial agents without emotional communication capabilities risk harming humans through inappropriate interactions. But developing genuinely affective agents introduces the possibility of machine suffering.

The dilemma: affective artificial agents may be "the lesser evil compared to (complex) artificial agents without affect."

## Key Arguments

1. **Five independent reasons** supporting development of affective artificial agents
2. Affective agents are necessary for natural human-agent interaction
3. Agents without affect may cause harm through emotional insensitivity
4. But genuine affect implies potential for suffering — ethical obligation follows
5. Affect is integral to rational decision-making (not opposed to it)
6. The paper ultimately argues we should develop affective agents but with ethical awareness

## Relevance to Cybernetics-Agents Research

This is a philosophical/ethical paper but deeply relevant:
- Positions affect not as decoration but as **functionally necessary** for complex autonomous agents
- Connects to the cybernetic insight that emotion/affect is a regulatory mechanism, not noise
- The "dilemma" maps onto questions about artificial homeostasis — if an agent has genuine drives, it can be frustrated, which is a form of suffering
- Raises the question: does a truly cybernetic agent (one with genuine self-regulation) necessarily have the capacity for something like suffering?
