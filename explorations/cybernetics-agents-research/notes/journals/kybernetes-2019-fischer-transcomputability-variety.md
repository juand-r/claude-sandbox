# Fischer (2019) - Transcomputability, Glanville's Corollary of Ashby's Law, and Epistemic Processes

**Citation:** Fischer, T. (2019), "Transcomputability, (Glanville's corollary of) Ashby's law of requisite variety and epistemic processes", *Kybernetes*, Vol. 48 No. 4, pp. 793-804.

**DOI:** 10.1108/K-11-2017-0457

**Affiliation:** Xi'an Jiaotong-Liverpool University

**Access:** Emerald Insight (paywalled).

## Purpose

Review and examine Ranulph Glanville's claim that strict control of even modest systems transcends the computational limits of our planet (referencing Bremermann's Limit and transcomputability). Glanville used this argument to support an ethical position against strict control in epistemic processes like learning and designing.

## Glanville's Corollary of Ashby's Law

Glanville argued that if a controller needs requisite variety to control a system, and the variety of even modest systems exceeds planetary computational capacity, then strict control is not just ethically wrong but computationally impossible. Therefore, epistemic interactions (learning, designing, conversing) must adopt an "out-of-control" posture.

## Key Findings

- **Challenges the quantitative argument:** Fischer shows that epistemic processes do NOT necessarily involve computational demands of astronomical proportions. The transcomputability claim is overstated.
- **Maintains the ethical argument:** Despite debunking the quantitative impossibility claim, Fischer agrees that out-of-control postures in epistemic processes are justified on *ethical* rather than computational grounds.
- **Rare discussion of Glanville's Corollary** -- one of the few papers to formally examine this extension of Ashby's law.

## Relevance to Our Project

This paper is important for several reasons:

1. **Limits of control in agent systems:** The question of whether an agent can have "requisite variety" to fully control/predict its environment is central. Fischer clarifies that the limitation is not always computational, but the ethical argument for not pursuing total control still holds.

2. **Design implications:** If we accept Glanville's ethical argument (even without the transcomputability claim), it suggests agent architectures should be designed for *conversation* and *collaboration* with their environment, not domination. This aligns with second-order cybernetic principles.

3. **Practical variety management:** Rather than trying to match all environmental variety (which may be unnecessary, not just impossible), agents should selectively attenuate and amplify variety -- which is exactly what Beer's VSM does through its variety engineering mechanisms.

4. **Epistemic humility in AI:** The paper provides a cybernetic grounding for the observation that AI systems work better when they acknowledge uncertainty rather than pretending omniscience.
