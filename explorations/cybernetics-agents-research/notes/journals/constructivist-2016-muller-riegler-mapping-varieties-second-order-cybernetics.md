# Mapping the Varieties of Second-Order Cybernetics

- **Authors:** Karl H. Müller & Alexander Riegler
- **Journal:** Constructivist Foundations 11(3): 443–454
- **Year:** 2016
- **URL:** https://constructivist.info/11/3/443
- **Open Access:** Yes

## Summary

Müller and Riegler map the current landscape of second-order cybernetics, which despite being proposed around 1970, still lacks a clear general status. They identify that second-order cybernetics developed along two distinct pathways and propose a five-part agenda for its modern application.

## Key Arguments

1. **Epistemic mode "from within"**: The central methodological innovation of second-order cybernetics is an epistemic stance that operates from within the system being studied, traceable to Heinz von Foerster.

2. **Two distinct pathways**: Second-order cybernetics and second-order science operate in different domains — the former in cybernetic systems theory, the latter in philosophy of science and methodology.

3. **Reflexivity's underrecognized importance**: Despite growing adoption across social sciences (Bourdieu, Archer, Beck/Giddens/Lash, Soros), reflexivity's cybernetic origins remain largely unacknowledged.

4. **Extends beyond science**: Second-order cybernetics has implications well beyond purely scientific domains, reaching into design, management, education, therapy.

## Key Concepts

- **Endo- vs. exo-concepts**: Inside-the-system vs. outside-the-system perspectives on observation and description.
- **Self-organizing and self-referential systems**: Core phenomena that second-order cybernetics is uniquely suited to address.
- **Lefebvre's algebra of conscience**: Computational and awareness structures as formalized reflexive systems.

## Relevance to Our Research

This is a map-of-the-territory paper — essential for understanding how second-order cybernetics fragments and coheres. The distinction between "from within" and "from without" epistemic modes is directly relevant to agent design: a second-order agent would need to operate "from within" its own cognitive system rather than having externally imposed goals. The five-part agenda they propose could structure our own research program.
