# Darivandi Shoushtari et al. (2025) - Strategic Fit of Task-Based Crowdsourcing: A Cybernetic Approach

**Citation:** Darivandi Shoushtari, K., Tohidi, H., Talebian, M. and Shadnam, M. (2025), "Strategic fit of task-based crowdsourcing: a cybernetic approach", *Kybernetes*, ahead-of-print.

**DOI:** 10.1108/K-03-2025-0792

**Access:** Emerald Insight (paywalled, ahead-of-print).

## Purpose

Examine conditions under which task-based crowdsourcing is an adaptive choice of organizing, using cybernetic theory (specifically Ashby's law of requisite variety) and agent-based simulation.

## Method

- Agent-based simulation model grounded in Ashby's law of requisite variety
- Explores how operational performance metrics (efficiency and responsiveness) depend on internal and environmental complexity drivers
- Two dimensions of environmental complexity: velocity and unpredictability

## Key Findings

- **Crowdsourcing is superior when environmental complexity is very low OR very high.** In intermediate environments, the choice depends on efficiency vs. responsiveness trade-off.
- **Traditional organizations excel in responsiveness** (higher worker reliability), especially for urgent tasks.
- **Crowdsourcing maintains better overall fit** across varying environmental conditions -- more suitable for dynamic, turbulent contexts.
- **Tie strength as design parameter:** The strength of ties between crowdworkers and the platform can be adjusted to optimize alignment with environmental complexity.

## Relevance to Our Project

This paper is a modern example of using cybernetic theory (requisite variety) to analyze organizational design with agent-based simulation:

1. **Variety matching as organizational design principle:** The paper treats Ashby's law as a design criterion, not just a theoretical observation. This is exactly what we want to do for agent architectures.

2. **Agent-based simulation of cybernetic principles:** Demonstrates that cybernetic concepts can be operationalized in agent-based models. The simulation validates the theoretical predictions.

3. **Environmental complexity drives design choices:** The finding that different organizational structures (loose/crowdsourced vs. tight/hierarchical) are optimal for different complexity levels directly maps to agent architecture decisions (e.g., when to use a single monolithic agent vs. a swarm of specialized agents).

4. **Tie strength as a design lever** is analogous to coupling strength between agents in a multi-agent system -- a tunable parameter that should match environmental demands.
