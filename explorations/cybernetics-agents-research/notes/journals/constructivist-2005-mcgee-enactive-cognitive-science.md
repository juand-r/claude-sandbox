# Enactive Cognitive Science. Part 1: History and Research Themes

- **Author:** Kevin McGee
- **Journal:** Constructivist Foundations 1(1): 19–34
- **Year:** 2005
- **URL:** https://constructivist.info/1/1/019.mcgee
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **Note:** Published in the inaugural issue of CF

## Summary

McGee provides a foundational introduction to enactive cognitive science — the approach that seeks to explain how autonomous cognitive systems can arise and participate in the generation of viable perceiver-dependent worlds, rather than attempting to "recover" pre-given features of an objective world. Published in the journal's very first issue, this sets the stage for CF's ongoing engagement with enactivism.

## Key Arguments

1. **Rejection of cognitivist recovery**: Traditional cognitive science assumes cognition involves recovering fixed, pre-existing features of an objective world. Enactive cognitive science rejects this, proposing instead that cognitive agents participate in generating their experienced worlds.

2. **Perceiver-dependent worlds**: The worlds that organisms inhabit are not pre-given but co-constituted through the organism's sensorimotor activity. Different organisms inhabit different worlds (Umwelt).

3. **Autonomy as central**: Enactive cognitive science places the autonomy of the cognitive system at its center — cognition serves the maintenance of the system's organization, not the accurate representation of an external world.

4. **Scientific rigor maintained**: Despite its radical epistemological stance, enactive cognitive science maintains standard scientific rigor: "testable hypotheses, empirical observation, supportable mechanisms and models, rigorous experimental methods, acceptable criteria of validation."

5. **Questions about scope**: The approach raises important questions about whether traditional scientific methods need expansion to account for phenomena like meaning and consciousness, and for the observer's role in studying cognition.

## Relevance to Our Research

This paper provides the historical and conceptual foundation for the enactive strand of our research. Key implications for agent design:

1. **Agents as world-generators, not world-recoverers**: Shifting from representation-based to enactive architectures means agents don't try to recover objective world states — they generate their own experiential worlds through interaction.

2. **Autonomy-first design**: The agent's primary imperative is maintaining its own organizational closure, not achieving externally defined goals.

3. **Umwelt as design principle**: Different agents should inhabit different experiential worlds based on their sensorimotor capabilities — there is no universal "ground truth" environment.

4. **Rigorous but expanded science**: We can maintain scientific standards while expanding what counts as valid cognitive science.
