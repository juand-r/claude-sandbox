## Abstract / Summary

Explores how **autonomous robots** can serve as embodied computational models for studying emotions across multiple disciplines. Argues that robot models of emotion are valuable interdisciplinary research instruments that allow scientists to test emotion theories in interactive, embodied systems.

## Key Concepts

- **Embodied emotion models**: Emotions in robots are not just computational — they are grounded in physical bodies interacting with environments
- **Functional emotion**: A system possessing mechanisms that fulfill functional roles of emotions can be said to partially have emotions from a functional point of view
- **Arousal**: Associated with general alertness, mobility, and readiness for action; treated as a dimension of emotions related to intensity/activity level
- **Robot models for psychology**: Robot models allowed psychologists to investigate less-studied situations like learning and exploration episodes
- **Human influence on robot development**: Humans can positively influence exploration patterns and learning outcomes of a developing "infant" robot
- **Affective cognition**: Integration of emotional processing with cognitive function in embodied systems

## Relevance to Cybernetics-Agents Research

Cañamero is one of the key researchers bridging cybernetics and affective robotics:
- **Embodiment is essential**: You cannot study emotion properly in disembodied systems — the body's regulatory needs drive emotional states
- **Functional approach**: Emotions are defined by what they DO (regulate behavior, signal states, coordinate action) not what they FEEL — classic cybernetic functionalism
- **Arousal as a regulatory variable**: Arousal modulates the agent's readiness to act, analogous to a gain parameter in a control system
- **Developmental approach**: Robot "infants" learn emotional regulation through interaction — connects to developmental cybernetics
- The emphasis on embodiment connects directly to interoception (body → brain signals) and homeostatic regulation
