# Eigenform and Reflexivity

- **Author:** Louis H. Kauffman
- **Journal:** Constructivist Foundations 12(3): 246–252
- **Year:** 2017
- **URL:** https://constructivist.info/12/3/246
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **Special Issue:** Eigenbehavior (eds. Riegler & Füllsack)

## Summary

Kauffman introduces eigenform in the context of second-order cybernetics and explores how eigenform arises at the point where observer and observed meet — apparently the same and yet apparently different. Objects in immediate experience are eigenforms: constructs arising through interaction, not pre-existing entities.

## Key Arguments

1. **Objects as eigenforms**: What we experience as "objects" are stable patterns (eigenforms) arising from our recursive interactions with the world. They are constructed, not discovered — yet they exhibit independence from the particular actions that generated them.

2. **Observer-observed nexus**: Eigenform emerges precisely at the boundary where observer and observed are "apparently the same and yet apparently different." This paradoxical identity/difference is the generative source of cognition.

3. **Independence property**: Despite being constructed, eigenforms exhibit autonomy — they have properties that go beyond what the constructor intended or predicted. This is how construction yields genuine knowledge.

4. **Social eigenforms**: Individuals generate eigenform models of others and themselves. Social reality is a web of mutually constructed eigenforms.

5. **Science without objectivity assumptions**: Rigorous investigation is possible without assuming a pre-given objective world, because eigenforms provide stable referents.

## Core Concept: Eigenform

Von Foerster's key insight: "Objects are tokens for eigenbehavior." An eigenform is what remains stable under recursive application of a process. Examples:
- A person's identity (stable pattern maintained through constant biological/psychological change)
- A scientific concept (stable pattern of experimental results and theoretical descriptions)
- A social institution (stable pattern of behaviors, expectations, and norms)

## Relevance to Our Research

This is the most directly applicable paper for agent identity and world-modeling. Key implications:

1. **Agent identity as eigenform**: An agent's "self" need not be a static data structure — it can be an eigenform that emerges from recursive self-observation. This is more robust and adaptive than hardcoded self-models.

2. **World-models as eigenforms**: Rather than representing an objective world, agent world-models can be eigenforms — stable patterns arising from agent-environment interaction. This sidesteps the representation problem.

3. **The independence property solves a key problem**: If constructed models can exhibit independence and surprise their constructor, then constructivism does not collapse into solipsism. Agents can discover genuinely new things even though their knowledge is constructed.

4. **Social eigenforms for multi-agent systems**: Agents modeling each other create mutual eigenforms — a formal basis for emergent social structure without central coordination.
