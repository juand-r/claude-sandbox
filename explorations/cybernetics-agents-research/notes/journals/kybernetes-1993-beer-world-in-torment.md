## Summary (from abstracts and secondary sources)

This is a major programmatic essay by Beer applying cybernetic analysis to global governance. It:

1. Introduces the components of contemporary change
2. Discusses the diagnostic approach of management cybernetics
3. Outlines a summary theory of autonomy
4. Considers autonomy at the global level
5. Offers an account of "the cybernetics of chronic societary triage"

## Key Arguments

### Triage Machine
Beer argues that mounting human misery is the product of a "triage machine" endemic to the ruling world ideology. Triage -- the practice of sorting and prioritizing -- operates systematically in global governance, creating winners and losers structurally, not accidentally.

### Redundancy of Potential Command
The action plan must "circumvent triage altogether" by invoking the **redundancy of potential command** as the methodology for a new system of world governance. This is a key Beer concept: rather than centralizing control, distribute the *potential* for command across the system so that whoever is best positioned to act, can act.

### Cybernetic Diagnosis at Scale
Beer applies his cybernetic diagnostic method (normally used for organizations) to the world system itself. This is a bold recursive application of the VSM -- treating the entire globe as a viable system that is failing its viability conditions.

## Relevance to Our Research
- The concept of "redundancy of potential command" is directly relevant to multi-agent architectures: agents should have distributed authority, not centralized control.
- The idea that systemic failures produce predictable pathologies (triage) maps to understanding why agent systems fail -- the system's structure produces certain behaviors regardless of stated goals.
- Recursive application of the VSM to different scales (firm -> nation -> world) demonstrates the power of recursive organizational models.
- Beer's analysis of global governance failure through a cybernetic lens shows the breadth of the VSM framework.

## Context
Published in a special issue of Kybernetes (Vol. 22 No. 6) which also included Leonard's paper on catastrophe response. This issue appears to have been a significant collection of applied VSM work.
