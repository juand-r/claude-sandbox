# Effective Approaches to Adaptive Collaboration via Dynamic Role Assignment

**Authors:** Yin Sheng, Haibin Zhu, Xianzhong Zhou, Wenting Hu
**Journal:** IEEE Transactions on Systems, Man, and Cybernetics: Systems, vol. 46, no. 1, pp. 76-92, 2016
**DOI:** (IEEE Xplore doc ID: 7109932)
**IEEE Xplore:** https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/7109932/

## Summary

Formalizes adaptive collaboration (AC) using the E-CARGO (Environment-Class, Agent, Role, Group, Object) model. Proposes algorithms for dynamic role assignment in collaborative systems.

### E-CARGO model components
- **Environment**: the context in which collaboration occurs
- **Classes**: categories of entities
- **Agents**: the collaborating entities
- **Roles**: functions agents can perform
- **Groups**: collections of agents organized for a task
- **Objects**: targets of collaborative activity

### Three algorithms for different scenarios
1. Based on **current group state** (reactive)
2. Based on **predicted group state** after a period (anticipatory)
3. Based on group state **throughout collaboration** (sustained optimization)

### Extended algorithms
- Two additional algorithms that account for **reassignment costs** (switching roles has overhead)

### Key finding
Dynamic role assignment outperforms static collaboration when reassignment costs are manageable.

## Relevance to Our Research

This is one of the most directly cybernetics-relevant papers in the collection:
- **Role-based organization** maps directly to Beer's VSM (roles as functional units within a viable system)
- **Adaptive collaboration** = the system reorganizes itself in response to changing conditions (autopoietic self-organization)
- **E-CARGO** provides a formal computational model for what Beer described qualitatively in the VSM
- **Dynamic role assignment** embodies Ashby's law: the system adapts its internal variety (role assignments) to match environmental demands

The consideration of reassignment costs adds realism -- reorganization is not free, which is a practical constraint that cybernetic theory often underemphasizes.

This paper comes from Haibin Zhu's group at Nipissing University, which is one of the main research groups in IEEE SMC working on role-based collaboration. Zhu is an IEEE Fellow and VP of the SMC Society.
