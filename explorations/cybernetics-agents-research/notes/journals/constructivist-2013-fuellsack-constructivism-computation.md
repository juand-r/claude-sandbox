# Constructivism and Computation: Can Computer-Based Modeling Add to the Case for Constructivism?

- **Author:** Manfred Füllsack
- **Journal:** Constructivist Foundations 9(1): 7–16
- **Year:** 2013
- **URL:** https://constructivist.info/9/1/007.fuellsack
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **Special Issue:** Computational Approaches to Constructivism

## Summary

Füllsack investigates whether digital computation's inherent deterministic reductionism contradicts constructivist philosophy. Through examples from dynamical systems, agent-based modeling, and AI, he argues that constructivism is not necessarily contradictory to computation — and that computation can actually strengthen the case for constructivism.

## Key Arguments

1. **The apparent contradiction**: Computational systems appear mechanistic and deterministic, seemingly at odds with constructivist epistemology which emphasizes the active construction of knowledge by observers. If everything reduces to deterministic bit-flipping, where is the construction?

2. **Emergence resolves the tension**: Computer simulations reveal complex, unpredictable behaviors arising from simple rules. This supports constructivist views — observers at higher levels genuinely construct knowledge that cannot be predicted from lower levels.

3. **Downward causation**: Higher-level phenomena influence lower levels, challenging pure reductionism. Computational models demonstrate this: macro-level patterns in agent-based models constrain micro-level behavior. This supports the constructivist claim that knowledge is not simply "bottom-up."

4. **Predictive processing**: Recent neuroscience using computational approaches suggests the brain engages in predictive processing — actively constructing expected inputs rather than passively receiving them. This aligns with radical constructivist epistemology.

5. **Observation as construction**: Computational modeling demonstrates how observers actively construct knowledge rather than passively receiving it — the model is always a construction, and its relationship to the "real system" is itself constructed.

## Relevance to Our Research

This paper addresses the fundamental question of whether our project is even coherent: Can computation embody constructivism? Füllsack says yes, and gives reasons:

1. **Emergence as constructivist vindication**: If computational agents exhibit emergent behavior that cannot be predicted from their base rules, then something genuinely new is being constructed at higher levels. This is not mere metaphor — it is constructivism in action.

2. **Agent-based models as constructivist tools**: ABMs are inherently constructivist — each agent constructs its own local view, and global patterns emerge from these local constructions. This is a natural computational substrate for constructivist agents.

3. **Predictive processing alignment**: The brain-as-prediction-machine view (predictive coding, active inference) is both computational and constructivist. This convergence suggests a productive path for our work.

4. **The representation question**: Füllsack helps clarify that computational models need not be "representations" in the naive sense — they can be tools for exploring constructivist dynamics.
