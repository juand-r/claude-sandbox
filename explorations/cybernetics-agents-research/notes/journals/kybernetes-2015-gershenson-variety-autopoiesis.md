# Gershenson (2015) - Requisite Variety, Autopoiesis, and Self-Organization

**Citation:** Gershenson, C. (2015), "Requisite variety, autopoiesis, and self-organization", *Kybernetes*, Vol. 44 No. 6/7, pp. 866-873.

**DOI:** 10.1108/K-01-2015-0001

**Preprint:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1409.7475 (FULL TEXT AVAILABLE)

**Context:** Part of Special Issue "The Cybernetics of Self-organisation", guest edited by Raul Espejo. Based on invited keynote at WOSC 2014 congress.

## Core Argument

Three foundational cybernetic concepts are unified:
1. **Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety** - a controller must have at least as much variety as the controlled
2. **Maturana & Varela's Autopoiesis** - self-production defines living systems
3. **Self-organization** - the mechanism by which systems can match environmental variety

## Key Ideas

- **Autopoiesis as a ratio:** Proposes measuring autopoiesis as the ratio between system complexity and environment complexity. This is a quantifiable measure, not just a philosophical concept.
- **More "living" systems survive better:** Systems with higher autopoiesis ratios are fitter in their environments, whether biological, social, technological, or urban.
- **Guided self-organization:** Can produce systems that adapt to requisite variety, offering more efficient solutions for time-varying problems than traditional (designed/optimized) techniques.
- **Traffic light example:** Demonstrates that self-organizing traffic lights outperform optimized fixed-phase traffic lights because they can match the variety of changing traffic patterns.

## Relevance to Our Project

This is a key theoretical bridge paper:
- The autopoiesis-as-ratio concept is directly applicable to agent design: an agent needs enough internal variety to match the variety of its task environment.
- Self-organization as the mechanism for achieving requisite variety maps onto adaptive agent architectures -- agents that reorganize their internal processes based on environmental feedback.
- The traffic light example is a concrete illustration of how cybernetic self-organization outperforms pre-designed optimization, which is essentially the argument for adaptive agents over hardcoded pipelines.
- Guided self-organization = a design approach where we set constraints/goals but let the system find its own way to match variety. This is analogous to goal-directed agents with tool selection.
