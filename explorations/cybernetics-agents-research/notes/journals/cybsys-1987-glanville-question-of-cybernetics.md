## Abstract
The recently developed notions of second-order cybernetics bring into focus a number of traditional concerns (e.g., self-reference). A simple derivation of some of these notions is made from a brief characterization of the initial cybernetics ideas developed by N. Wiener and others. Matters of concern and consequence are then stated, and are summarized as facets of what should be the major area of research in the field: the question of cybernetics.

## Key Themes
- Second-order cybernetics and self-reference
- Derives second-order ideas from first-order Wiener cybernetics
- Glanville was a student of Gordon Pask and key figure in second-order cybernetics
- The "question" is essentially: what happens when cybernetics turns on itself?

## Relevance to Our Research
Glanville bridges Pask's conversation theory and von Foerster's second-order cybernetics. The self-referential dimension is important for understanding how agents model themselves -- a concern that maps to modern work on self-reflection in AI agents (Reflexion paper, meta-cognition in LLM agents). The architectural framing (Glanville was in a School of Architecture) also connects to design cybernetics.
