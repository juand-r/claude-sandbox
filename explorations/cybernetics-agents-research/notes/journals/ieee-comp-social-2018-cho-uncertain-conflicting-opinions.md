# Dynamics of Uncertain and Conflicting Opinions in Social Networks

## Citation
Cho, J.-H., Rager, S., O'Donovan, J., Adali, S., & Horne, B.D. (2018). Dynamics of Uncertain and Conflicting Opinions in Social Networks. *IEEE Transactions on Computational Social Systems*, 5(2), 518-531. DOI: 10.1109/TCSS.2018.2833542

## Summary

Studies opinion dynamics where agents are uncertain about their own opinions and those opinions may conflict with others in the network. Uses Subjective Logic to model opinions as tuples of (belief, disbelief, uncertainty). Extends classical opinion dynamics models to handle the realistic case where agents have varying confidence levels.

## Key Contributions

1. **Subjective Logic for opinion modeling**: Rather than representing opinions as simple scalars, uses (belief, disbelief, uncertainty) tuples — a richer representation that captures real-world ambiguity.

2. **Informed vs. uninformed agents**: Models two classes of agents — informed (high confidence, low uncertainty) and uninformed (low confidence, high uncertainty) — and studies how their relative numbers affect opinion convergence.

3. **Uncertainty propagation in networks**: Shows how uncertainty interacts with network structure to affect consensus formation and opinion fragmentation.

## Relevance to Cybernetics Research

- Opinion dynamics is fundamentally about information feedback in social systems — agents adjust beliefs based on signals from neighbors, a cybernetic feedback loop.
- The uncertainty dimension connects to Ashby's requisite variety: agents with insufficient information (high uncertainty) have less ability to influence the system.
- The informed/uninformed agent distinction maps to cybernetic concepts of regulation: who has the information needed to steer the system?
- Consensus formation is a self-organizing process — the system finds attractors through local interactions, no central controller.

## Key Concepts
- Opinion dynamics, subjective logic, uncertainty, social networks, consensus formation, multi-agent systems, belief propagation
