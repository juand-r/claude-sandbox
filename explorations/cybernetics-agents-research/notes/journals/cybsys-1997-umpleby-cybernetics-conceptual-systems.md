## Summary
Identifies three periods in the evolution of cybernetics in the United States:

1. **1950s-1960s:** Engineering cybernetics -- designing control systems, building machines to emulate human reasoning. Focus on observed systems.

2. **1970s-1980s:** Biological cybernetics / second-order cybernetics -- biology of cognition and constructivist philosophy (von Foerster, Maturana, Varela). Shifted attention from what is observed to the observer.

3. **1990s onward:** Social cybernetics / cybernetics of conceptual systems -- emphasis on multiple observers and their beliefs. Constructivist cybernetics as a conceptual system to promote evolution of social systems.

Key argument: The third period requires understanding that scientific theories are not just descriptions of reality but conceptual systems that influence the systems they describe (reflexivity).

## Key Themes
- Three waves of cybernetics (engineering -> biological -> social)
- Reflexivity: the observer changes what is observed
- Constructivism as epistemological foundation
- Conceptual systems as tools for social change

## Relevance to Our Research
The three-wave model is useful for situating our work. Current AI agent research is largely in "first wave" mode (engineering control systems), with some second-wave elements (self-modeling agents). The third-wave insight -- that the frameworks we build shape the systems we study -- is relevant to how we think about agent architectures. If agents embody conceptual frameworks, then the framework shapes what the agent can become.

Also relevant: the reflexivity problem maps directly to the challenge of self-referencing agents (agents that model their own behavior and adjust).
