# Systems Theory and Algorithmic Futures: Interview with Elena Esposito

**Authors:** Elena Esposito, Katrin Sold, Benedicte Zimmermann
**Year:** 2021
**Journal:** Constructivist Foundations, Vol. 16, No. 3, pp. 356-361 (target article), pp. 377-380 (author's response)
**URL:** https://constructivist.info/16/3/356
**Type:** Target article (interview) with open peer commentaries and author's response

## Core Contribution

Applies Luhmann's systems theory to algorithmic prediction and AI. Treats ML algorithms as "non-trivial machines" (von Foerster's term) that function as communication partners rather than tools to be understood.

## Key Arguments

### From the Interview (pp. 356-361)
- Society is made not by people but by what connects them — communication
- Algorithmic predictions parallel ancient divinatory predictions: both construct futures rather than discovering them
- Systems theory offers a genuine alternative to Frankfurt School critical theory for analyzing technology
- The complex intertwinement of past, present, and future is central to understanding algorithms

### From the Author's Response: "Opacity and Complexity of Learning Black Boxes" (pp. 377-380)
- ML algorithms are "non-trivial machines that do not have to be understood, but controlled as communication partners"
- This shifts the problem: instead of demanding algorithmic transparency, study how societies manage opacity
- The normative component of algorithmic control should be addressed with critical attitude
- From systems theory perspective, algorithms participate in communication — they are not merely tools

### Key Commentaries
- Clarke (2021) "How Can Algorithms Participate in Communication?" — bringing cybernetic topics back into Luhmann's constructivist framework
- Füllsack (2021) "Automated Societal Option Maximization" — can automated future option maximization have a social dimension?
- Leydesdorff (2021) "Social Order: Continuously Reconstructed in Terms of Expectations"
- Loosen & Scholl (2021) "The Epistemological Dimension of Algorithms"

## Relevance to Project

The framing of algorithms as "non-trivial machines" and "communication partners" is a direct application of von Foerster's cybernetics to AI. The move from transparency demands to opacity management is a genuinely constructivist contribution to AI discourse. If agents are communication partners rather than tools, the design problem shifts fundamentally — you design for interaction, not for correctness.

Reference: Esposito E. (2021) *Artificial Communication: How Algorithms Produce Social Intelligence.* MIT Press.
