# Second-Order Science: A Vast and Largely Unexplored Science Frontier

- **Authors:** Karl H. Müller & Alexander Riegler
- **Journal:** Constructivist Foundations 10(1): 7–15
- **Year:** 2014
- **URL:** https://constructivist.info/10/1/007.introduction
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **Special Issue:** Second-Order Science (eds. Riegler & Müller)

## Summary

Müller and Riegler introduce second-order science as a framework addressing observer-dependency in fields like cognition and quantum physics. They distinguish between first-order science, second-order science, Science 2.0, and Science II, arguing that second-order science — based on the operation of "re-entry" — offers three essential contributions.

## Key Arguments

1. **Re-entry as foundational operation**: Second-order science is grounded in re-entry — the process by which a system applies its operations to itself, creating feedback loops between observation and method. This is borrowed from Spencer-Brown and von Foerster.

2. **Three vital functions of second-order science**:
   - **Novelty and innovation**: Self-referential examination of scientific methods generates genuinely new insights not available to first-order approaches.
   - **Quality control**: Second-order science provides self-corrective mechanisms by examining how scientific methods affect scientific results.
   - **Integration and generality**: By examining science itself as a system, second-order science can bridge disciplinary boundaries.

3. **Distinction from other "second" labels**: The authors carefully distinguish their concept from Science 2.0 (internet-era collaborative science) and Science II (post-normal science). Second-order science is specifically about applying scientific methods reflexively to science itself.

4. **A new cybernetics needed**: The authors argue that "a new form of cybernetics is needed" to address emerging challenges — the existing frameworks are necessary but insufficient.

## Relevance to Our Research

The re-entry concept is directly relevant to agent architecture. An agent that can apply its reasoning processes to its own reasoning (re-entry) gains:
- Self-monitoring and error-correction capabilities
- The ability to modify its own learning strategies
- Meta-cognitive awareness

This paper frames these capabilities not as engineering add-ons but as fundamental features of any genuinely second-order system. The three functions (novelty, quality control, integration) map onto desirable agent properties: creativity, reliability, and generalization.
