# Info-computational Constructivism and Cognition

**Author:** Gordana Dodig-Crnkovic
**Year:** 2014
**Journal:** Constructivist Foundations, Vol. 9, No. 2, pp. 223-231
**URL:** https://constructivist.info/9/2/223.dodig
**Type:** Target article with open peer commentaries and author's response (pp. 246-255)

## Core Contribution

Proposes info-computationalism (IC) as a framework that reinforces constructivist ideas about cognition while showing how constructivist insights can inspire new models of computing. Argues that cognition IS the process of life, operating through information and computation.

## Key Arguments

- IC provides a common perspective for studying cognition across biological organisms (bacteria to humans) and future artificial cognitive agents.
- "Cognition and knowledge generation in a cognizing agent is interaction with the world (potential information)" which becomes actual information through natural computation processes.
- Knowledge emerges through agents' active interactions with their environment and other agents — supporting constructivist epistemology.
- Identifies Heinz von Foerster as a precursor to natural computing and biocomputing approaches.
- The transition from potential to actual information requires integration processes.
- Draws parallels with other constructivist approaches to cognition and knowledge generation.

## Commentaries

- Ehresmann (2014) proposed a mathematical model (multiplicity principle) for IC
- Basti (2014) argued IC needs integration with quantum field theory
- Brier (2014) raised concerns that natural computing "has neither been defined theoretically nor implemented practically" — a sharp critique

## Relevance to Project

This paper attempts to ground constructivism in computation at a fundamental level — not just "using computers to model constructivist ideas" but arguing that computation IS the substrate of cognition in nature. The critique from Brier is important: is "natural computing" a real concept or a metaphor? This tension between literal and metaphorical computation is central to whether cybernetic ideas can actually inform AI agent design or remain philosophical commentary.
