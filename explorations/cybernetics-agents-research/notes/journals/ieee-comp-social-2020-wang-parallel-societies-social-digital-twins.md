## Summary

Introduces the concept of "parallel societies" — where real-world physical and social systems are mirrored and interact dynamically in virtual environments through Social Digital Twins (SDTs). Extends the digital twin concept from engineering/manufacturing to entire social systems. The virtual and real societies run in parallel, each informing and influencing the other.

## Key Contributions

1. **Social Digital Twins (SDTs)**: Extends digital twin technology from individual objects/processes to entire social systems — modeling not just physical infrastructure but human behaviors, interactions, and social networks.

2. **Virtual-real interaction**: The parallel society isn't just a simulation; it's an active feedback system where the virtual model and real society continuously exchange information and influence each other.

3. **Urban systems as "places" not "spaces"**: Shifts from modeling cities as physical spaces to modeling them as dynamic places that encompass both tangible (infrastructure) and intangible (social) dimensions.

## Relevance to Cybernetics Research

- **Cybernetic feedback at societal scale**: The parallel societies concept is essentially a cybernetic feedback loop writ large — build a model of society, compare it to reality, use the differences to improve both the model and the real system.
- **ACP framework in action**: This is the Artificial systems + Computational experiments + Parallel execution (ACP) framework applied to entire societies. Each component maps to cybernetic concepts: artificial systems = model construction, computational experiments = hypothesis testing, parallel execution = feedback-driven regulation.
- **Governance through simulation**: The SDT concept enables "what-if" governance — test policies in the virtual society before deploying them in the real one. This is cybernetic governance: steer the system using model-based prediction and feedback.
- **Observer-system entanglement**: The parallel society blurs the line between observer and observed — the model (observer) and the society (observed) influence each other. This is second-order cybernetics.
