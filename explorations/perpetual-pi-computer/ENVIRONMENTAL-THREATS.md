# Environmental and Geological Threats to a Surface/Subsurface Device Over 5 Billion Years

## Executive Summary

The dominant threat is **solar evolution**. The Sun's increasing luminosity will boil Earth's oceans in ~1 billion years and melt the surface in ~3.5-4 billion years. The Sun engulfs Earth entirely at ~7.6 billion years. Everything else is secondary to this, but matters enormously for the first ~1-2 billion years.

---

## 1. Ice Ages

### Cycle Frequency
- **Current regime (last ~1 Myr):** Glacial-interglacial cycles every ~100,000 years, driven by Milankovitch orbital cycles (eccentricity).
- **Earlier regime (2.5-1.0 Mya):** Cycles every ~41,000 years (obliquity-dominated).
- **Three Milankovitch components:** Eccentricity (~96 kyr), obliquity (~41 kyr), precession (~23 kyr).

### Ice Sheet Depth and Severity
- **Maximum ice thickness:** The Laurentide Ice Sheet reached **3,000-4,000 m (3-4 km)** at its thickest over Hudson Bay/Labrador-Quebec.
- **Average thickness:** ~1,500 m.
- **Edge thickness:** ~300 m in peripheral areas (e.g., southern Alberta/Saskatchewan).
- **Temperature depression:** Global temperatures were ~5 C lower than today at the Last Glacial Maximum (LGM, ~20,000 years ago).
- **Sea level change:** Sea level was ~125 m lower at the LGM.
- **Insolation variation:** Milankovitch cycles cause up to 25% variation in incoming solar radiation at mid-latitudes.

### Next Ice Age
- **Natural timing:** ~50,000 years from now, based on Milankovitch orbital parameters.
- **With anthropogenic CO2 (500 PgC historical emissions):** Unlikely to delay inception significantly.
- **With 1,000 PgC total emissions:** Delays inception by ~50,000 years (so ~100,000 years from now).
- **With 5,000 PgC emissions:** Could alter glacial cycles for the next 500,000 years.
- **2025 study (Nature Communications Earth & Environment):** Inception generally expected within the next 200,000 years for emissions up to 5,000 PgC.

### Long-Term Glaciation Outlook
- Ice ages are expected to continue in roughly 100 kyr cycles for as long as Milankovitch forcing operates.
- **However:** The Sun's luminosity increase (~10% per billion years) means that within ~500 million to 1 billion years, the Earth will be too warm for glaciation to occur at all. Ice ages are a near-term concern only (next few hundred million years).

### Impact on a Device
- A device at the surface in high latitudes could be buried under **up to 4 km of ice**.
- The ice itself does not destroy things -- it compresses and scours. Glacial scouring can remove tens of meters of bedrock.
- A device buried >10 m in stable bedrock would likely survive glaciation cycles, though glacial rebound and pressure effects are non-trivial.

## 2. Volcanic Activity / Supervolcanoes

### Eruption Frequency
- **VEI 8 (supervolcanic) eruptions:** At least 60 identified in Earth's history. Average recurrence: estimates range from every 45,000 to 714,000 years. Revised 2017 estimates suggest roughly every 17,000-45,000 years.
- **Last VEI 8 eruption:** Oruanui eruption (Taupo, New Zealand), ~25,600 years ago.
- **Yellowstone:** Erupts roughly every ~725,000 years (2.1 Ma, 1.3 Ma, 640 ka).
- **Scaling law:** Eruption frequency drops by ~7x for every 10x increase in eruption size.

### Probability Over 5 Billion Years
- Supervolcanic eruptions (VEI 8) are 5-10x more likely than civilization-ending asteroid impacts.
- Over 5 billion years, Earth would experience tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of VEI 7+ eruptions.
- **However:** Volcanic activity will gradually decline as Earth's interior cools. The mantle may become too cool for subduction-driven volcanism in ~1.45-5 billion years.
- The probability of any specific location being hit by lava is low, but over billions of years, cumulative probability becomes significant, especially near plate boundaries.

### Lava Survivability
- **Lava temperatures:** 700-1,200 C (basaltic lava ~1,000-1,200 C).
- **Heat transfer to buried infrastructure:** Research shows considerable heat transfers from lava flows into the ground. Maximum operating temperatures for buried cables are exceeded within 1 week of a lava flow's arrival.
- **Depth protection:** At depths of 500-1,000 m, a device would be well insulated from surface lava flows. The geothermal gradient adds ~25-30 C per km, so ambient temperature at 1 km depth is ~40-55 C.
- **Direct burial in lava:** Not survivable for conventional electronics. Lava at 1,000+ C exceeds silicon's absolute physical limit (~300-500 C).
- **Mitigation:** Location on a stable craton, far from plate boundaries and hotspots, dramatically reduces volcanic risk. The Canadian Shield has had no significant volcanic activity for hundreds of millions of years.

## 3. Asteroid/Comet Impacts

### Frequency and Probability
- **1 km+ asteroids (civilization-threatening):** Impact roughly every ~700,000 years.
- **Football-field-sized (~100 m):** Every ~2,000 years.
- **Dinosaur-killer (10 km+):** Probability of 0.000001% per year (~once per 100 million years).
- **Over 1 billion years:** Probability of a giant impact (100 km crater) is 0.03-0.3 (3-30%).
- **Over 5 billion years:** Near certainty of multiple civilization-ending impacts; probability of a single 100+ km crater event approaches 1.

### Could Underground Placement Help?
- **Surface impacts:** A 10 km asteroid creates a crater ~150-200 km wide and ~30-40 km deep at the impact point. Nothing survives at ground zero.
- **But the Earth is large:** The probability of a direct hit on any specific location is very small. Even a 200 km crater covers only ~0.00008% of Earth's surface.
- **Indirect effects:** Global firestorms, impact winter, tsunamis, seismic waves. These are devastating to surface life but largely irrelevant to a device buried hundreds of meters underground.
- **Underground placement at 500+ m depth:** Would survive everything except a direct hit. The device only needs to avoid being within the crater itself.

### Cumulative Risk Over 5 Billion Years
- For a device at a random surface location, the probability of being within a large impact crater over 5 billion years is very low (well under 1%).
- Underground placement at depth makes the device essentially immune to all but direct strikes.

## 4. Plate Tectonics

### Movement Rates
- **Continental drift:** 2-15 cm/year (average ~5 cm/year).
- **Over 1 billion years:** ~50,000 km of movement (more than Earth's circumference).
- **Over 5 billion years:** ~250,000 km of cumulative movement.
- **Subduction rate:** Oceanic plates subduct at ~2-11 cm/year. 55,000 km of subduction zones exist globally, roughly equal to the 60,000 km of mid-ocean ridges.

### Subduction Risk
- **Oceanic crust:** Essentially guaranteed to be subducted. Oceanic crust is recycled every ~200 million years on average. A device on the ocean floor would be pulled into the mantle within a few hundred million years.
- **Continental crust on a craton:** Continental cratons are NOT subducted under normal circumstances. They are too buoyant (low density) and too thick (200-300 km lithospheric root). The Canadian Shield has survived for 4+ billion years.
- **Craton destruction is extremely rare:** The North China Craton is the best-studied example, and its destruction required >500 million years of continuous circum-craton subduction, metasomatism, and a unique confluence of tectonic events. Most cratons survive indefinitely.
- **Continental collision:** Cratons survive continental collisions (they've survived multiple supercontinent cycles -- Pangaea, Rodinia, Columbia, etc.).

### When Will Plate Tectonics Stop?
- **Estimates: 1.45-5 billion years from now,** as the mantle cools below the temperature needed for convection.
- There may be intermittent pauses; one model predicts a pause in ~350 million years when the Pacific closes.
- Once plate tectonics stops, the subduction risk goes to zero -- but so does the recycling of the atmosphere, potentially accelerating surface degradation.

### Survival Strategy
- **Place on a stable craton.** Continental shields have survived billions of years of plate tectonics. The Canadian Shield, West African Craton, Kaapvaal Craton (South Africa), Pilbara Craton (Australia), and others have persisted for 2.5-4+ billion years.
- **Avoid:** Ocean floor (subducted in <200 Myr), plate boundaries, active orogens, rift zones.

## 5. Solar Evolution

### The Dominant Threat

This is the single most important factor. The Sun is getting brighter.

### Timeline

| Time from Now | Solar Luminosity | Earth Surface Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Now | 1.0 L_sun | Current conditions |
| +500 Myr | ~1.05 L_sun | C3 photosynthesis stressed |
| +600 Myr | ~1.06 L_sun | CO2 falls below C3 photosynthesis threshold; trees die |
| +800 Myr | ~1.07 L_sun | C4 photosynthesis fails; most plants extinct |
| +1.0 Gyr | ~1.10 L_sun | **Moist greenhouse begins.** Oceans start evaporating. Surface temp rises significantly. |
| +1.0-1.5 Gyr | ~1.10-1.15 L_sun | **Oceans gone.** Hydrogen escapes to space via photodissociation. |
| +2.0 Gyr | ~1.20 L_sun | Surface uninhabitable by any known life. |
| +2.8 Gyr | ~1.28 L_sun | Surface temperature reaches ~150 C (422 K) even at poles. |
| +3.5-4.0 Gyr | ~1.35-1.40 L_sun | **Runaway greenhouse.** Surface temperature reaches ~1,330 C (1,600 K). Surface melts. |
| +5.0 Gyr | ~1.67 L_sun | Core hydrogen exhausted. Sun leaves main sequence. |
| +7.6 Gyr | Red giant | **Sun engulfs Earth.** Sun expands to 256x current radius (1.2 AU). Earth is consumed. |

### When Does It Become Too Hot for Electronics?
- **Standard silicon electronics fail at:** 125-200 C (practical commercial limit), with absolute physical limit at ~300-500 C.
- **Surface temperature reaches 150 C:** ~2.8 billion years from now.
- **Surface temperature reaches 300 C:** ~3-3.5 billion years from now (estimate by interpolation).
- **Underground at 500 m depth:** Ambient temperature is already ~40-55 C today. As surface warms, underground temperatures rise too, but with a lag and buffer. Underground placement buys perhaps a few hundred million years of additional time.
- **Surface melts (~1,330 C):** ~3.5-4 billion years. Nothing survives on or near the surface at this point.

### Key Insight
The Sun's luminosity increase means that **the device's functional lifetime is capped at roughly 2.5-3.5 billion years** regardless of all other factors, unless it is designed with exotic high-temperature materials (SiC, GaN, diamond semiconductors can operate above 500 C).

## 6. Magnetic Field

### Current State
- Earth's magnetic field is generated by convection in the liquid iron outer core (the geodynamo).
- Current strength: ~25-65 microteslas at the surface.
- The field has been weakening by ~10% since 1800, and ~3,000 years of general decline.

### Reversals
- **Average frequency:** Every several hundred thousand years (183+ reversals identified in the geological record).
- **Last major reversal:** 772,000-774,000 years ago (Brunhes-Matuyama reversal).
- **During reversal:** Field does NOT vanish but weakens to 5-10% of normal strength and becomes multipolar (e.g., Laschamp excursion ~41,000 years ago: field dropped to ~5% strength).
- **Duration of reversal:** Typically 1,000-10,000 years for the transition.
- **Effect on surface:** Increased UV radiation (ozone depletion from solar wind particles), higher radiation dose. Not catastrophic for a device -- this is mainly a biological threat.

### Risk of Permanent Loss
- **No.** Earth's geodynamo is robust and powered by the crystallization of the inner core. The core is growing at ~1 mm/year and would take ~91 billion years to fully solidify (far longer than the Sun's remaining lifetime).
- **Mars lost its field** because it is smaller and cooled faster. Earth is large enough to maintain its dynamo for billions of years.
- The magnetic field will persist for the remaining lifetime of the Earth (until the Sun engulfs it).

### Impact on a Device
- **Minimal direct threat.** Magnetic field reversals increase surface radiation, which could affect exposed electronics (increased single-event upsets from cosmic rays). Underground placement eliminates this risk entirely -- rock provides excellent radiation shielding.

## 7. Erosion

### Rates
- **Stable continental surfaces:** Tens of meters per million years (~10-30 m/Myr).
- **Tectonically active mountains:** 100-1,000+ m/Myr.
- **Glacial erosion:** An order of magnitude faster than fluvial erosion globally.
- **Human-caused erosion:** Currently ~10x natural rates (hundreds of meters per million years equivalent).

### Over Long Timescales
- **10 million years:** A surface structure on stable rock is eroded or buried by 100-300 m.
- **100 million years:** 1-3 km of erosion on stable surfaces. The Appalachians, once Himalaya-height, have been reduced to rolling hills over ~300 Myr.
- **700 million years:** The Neoproterozoic erosion event removed 3-5 km of crust across all continents -- the largest erosion event in Earth history.
- **1 billion years:** All surface structures are completely obliterated. No surface structure survives this timescale.
- **The Great Unconformity:** A gap of ~500 million years in the rock record at the Grand Canyon demonstrates that surface material was completely removed.

### Impact on a Device
- A device on the surface is either eroded away or buried under sediment within millions to tens of millions of years.
- A device buried at 500 m depth in stable rock would be exposed to the surface in ~15-50 million years through erosion, then itself eroded away.
- A device buried at 1,000+ m depth in a stable craton could remain entombed for hundreds of millions to billions of years, especially if the region experiences sedimentary deposition rather than erosion.

### Paradox
- You want the device deep enough to avoid erosion exposing it, but shallow enough to avoid excessive geothermal heating.
- Sweet spot: **200-1,000 m depth** in a stable craton that is in a depositional rather than erosional regime.

## 8. Best Placement Strategy

### The Ideal Location

**Deep within a stable continental craton, 500-1,000 m below the surface, in crystalline basement rock (granite/gneiss), far from any plate boundary, rift zone, or hotspot.**

### Specific Recommendations

| Factor | Recommendation | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Rock type** | Granite or gneiss in Precambrian shield | Lowest erosion rates, highest stability, lowest permeability |
| **Depth** | 500-1,000 m | Deep enough to survive glaciation (max ice 4 km, but scouring only affects top tens of meters), erosion, impacts; shallow enough to keep geothermal temp manageable (~40-55 C at 1 km) |
| **Location** | Center of a large craton | Canadian Shield, West African Craton, Kaapvaal Craton, Pilbara Craton, Baltic Shield, Siberian Craton |
| **Distance from plate boundary** | >1,000 km | Minimizes volcanic and seismic risk |
| **Latitude** | Mid-latitude on a craton | Avoid equatorial weathering rates; high latitudes face glaciation but this is survivable at depth |
| **Avoid** | Ocean floor (subducted in <200 Myr), rift zones, volcanic hotspots, orogens, coastlines |  |

### Best Candidate Locations

1. **Canadian Shield (Superior Craton)** -- 4+ billion years old, among the most stable geology on Earth, well-studied, massive area (8 million km2).
2. **Kaapvaal Craton, South Africa** -- 3.6 Gyr old, extremely stable.
3. **Pilbara Craton, Western Australia** -- 3.6 Gyr old.
4. **Baltic Shield (Fennoscandia)** -- Sweden's Forsmark nuclear waste repository is sited in 1.9 Gyr old crystalline bedrock at 500 m depth, designed for 1 million year containment.

### Nuclear Waste Repository Analogy
The nuclear waste disposal community has already solved much of this problem:
- **Finland's Onkalo repository:** 400 m deep in granite, designed for 100,000+ year isolation.
- **Sweden's Forsmark:** 500 m deep in 1.9 Gyr old crystalline bedrock.
- **France's Cigeo:** 500 m deep in clay that has been stable for 160 million years.
- **Natural analogue (Oklo, Gabon):** Natural nuclear reactors produced fission products and plutonium ~2 billion years ago. These remained immobile in the rock to the present day despite groundwater access -- proving geological containment over billion-year timescales.

### Survivability Estimates by Threat

| Threat | Timescale of Concern | Survivable at 500-1000m in craton? |
|---|---|---|
| Ice ages | Next ~500 Myr | **Yes.** Ice scouring only affects top meters to tens of meters. |
| Supervolcanoes | Next ~2-5 Gyr | **Yes.** Stable cratons are far from volcanism. Lava flows don't penetrate to 500m depth. |
| Asteroid impacts | Next ~5 Gyr | **Almost certainly yes.** Probability of direct hit on specific location is negligible. |
| Plate tectonics / subduction | Next ~1.5-5 Gyr | **Yes.** Cratons are not subducted. They survive supercontinent cycles. |
| Erosion | Next ~1 Gyr+ | **Yes at sufficient depth.** 1 km depth in stable craton with low erosion rate survives hundreds of Myr to Gyr. |
| Magnetic reversals | Ongoing | **Yes.** No threat to underground device. Rock provides radiation shielding. |
| Solar luminosity increase | 1-3.5 Gyr | **THE LIMITING FACTOR.** Surface reaches 150 C in ~2.8 Gyr, ~1,330 C in ~3.5-4 Gyr. Underground temperatures also rise. Nothing survives the runaway greenhouse. |
| Sun becomes red giant | ~7.6 Gyr | **No.** Earth is engulfed. Total destruction. |

### Bottom Line

A device placed at 500-1,000 m depth in the center of a stable craton (e.g., Canadian Shield) could plausibly survive for **~2-3 billion years** before solar evolution renders even underground temperatures unsurvivable for conventional electronics. With exotic high-temperature materials (SiC, diamond), this could be extended somewhat.

The device will NOT survive the full 5 billion years. The Sun's increasing luminosity is an inescapable hard limit. By ~3.5-4 billion years from now, Earth's surface is molten. By ~7.6 billion years, the Sun engulfs the planet.

**For true 5-billion-year survival, the device must leave Earth.**
